### Slide 10: "The Design Problem"
- **Borstad 2005, Figure 1** — Scapular rotation terminology diagram (posterior view, superior view, lateral view showing upward/downward rotation, internal/external rotation, anterior/posterior tilt). This is the clearest published diagram of the three scapular rotations. Clean, educational, orients the audience to the vocabulary.

---

### Slide 11: "The Numbers That Made Me Pay Attention"
- **Kibler 1998, Figure 1** — The kinetic chain link sequencing diagram showing force generation from ground through legs, trunk, scapula to arm/hand. This is the original kinetic chain figure that's been cited hundreds of times. Simple, iconic.
- Optional secondary: **McClure 2001, Figure 1** — photograph of a living subject with bone pins drilled into the scapular spine and electromagnetic sensors attached. This is visually striking — it shows the audience the rigor of the measurement methodology. It's memorable because bone pins in a living volunteer is dramatic.

---

### Slide 12: "The Evolutionary Context"
- **Kuhn 2016, Figure 3** — Four-panel figure showing glenohumeral cartilage alignment. Panel A: humeral head cartilage thickness (thickest at center). Panel B: glenoid cartilage thickness (thinnest at center). Panel C: cartilage NOT aligned in upright resting position. Panel D: cartilage aligned at ~90° abduction (throwing position). This is the single most powerful image in your talk — it shows that the joint is literally designed for throwing, not resting. The audience will remember this figure.
- **Kuhn 2016, Figure 4** — Comparative humeral morphology across primate species showing the depressed greater tuberosity in humans vs. other primates (Papio anubis, Cercopithecus aethiops, Presbytis melalophos, Homo sapiens). This visually demonstrates the evolutionary trade-off: depressed tuberosity enables extreme external rotation for throwing but reduces supraspinatus moment arm and creates the watershed zone.
- Optional: **Selby 2017** — Comparative scapular morphology across hominoids showing the two fundamental changes (mediolateral narrowing and glenoid migration). More academic, better for a publication than a talk.

---

### Slide 14: "The Critical Shoulder Angle"
- **Moor 2013, Figure 2a and 2b** — Side-by-side radiographs showing CSA measurement in a shoulder with a cuff tear (CSA 43°) and a shoulder with OA (CSA 22°). This is THE defining image of the CSA concept. The audience immediately sees the difference. Essential slide.
- **Nyffeler 2006, Figure 1** — Acromion Index measurement diagram showing the three parallel lines on an AP radiograph. Good for showing the intellectual lineage (AI → CSA).
- **Nyffeler 2006, Figure 3a and 3b** — Side-by-side radiographs: one with a large lateral acromial extension and cuff tear (AI 0.82) and one with OA and a short acromion (AI 0.50). Visually dramatic contrast.

---

### Slide 15: "CSA Predicts Surgical Failure"
- **Garcia 2017, Figure 1** — CSA measurement diagram (similar to Moor but from the retear paper — ties the measurement directly to the surgical outcome context).
- Consider a **bar graph or table** you create showing: CSA in no-retear (34.3°) vs. partial retear vs. full-thickness retear (38.6°) from Garcia's data. Simple, clean, high impact.
- Optional: **Gerber 2018** — intraoperative or schematic images of the lateral acromioplasty technique if available in the paper. These would show the audience exactly what the procedure looks like.

---

### Slide 16: "Posterior Acromial Morphology"
- **Meyer 2019, Figure 1** — The measurement technique for posterior acromial tilt on a true lateral (Y-view) radiograph. Shows the reference line from inferior scapular angle through the Y intersection, and the acromial tilt line. This teaches the audience a measurement they can use.
- **Gerber 2025 SCOPE, Figure 1** — 3D comparison of a scapula with posterior instability (yellow) overlaid on a normal scapula (statistical shape model). Shows the high, horizontal acromion in posterior instability vs. the steeper normal acromion. This is the most visually compelling figure for the morphology argument — the difference is immediately apparent in 3D.
- **Gerber 2025 SCOPE, Figure 2** — The planned biplanar closing-wedge acromial osteotomy with the correction marked. Shows the 3D-planned cutting guides. This is cutting-edge personalized medicine applied to the scapula — visually impressive.
- **Hochreiter 2023** — 3D-printed scapula models showing the different configurations tested (normal vs. B1 vs. corrections). Visually demonstrates the biomechanical testing setup.

---

### Slide 17: "The B0 Question"
- **Bryce 2010, Figure 1** — The scapular coordinate system showing how the plane of the scapula is defined and how version is measured. Helps the audience visualize how scapular rotation changes the apparent version.
- **Domos 2017 (Walch B0), Figure 1** — The three stages of posterior subluxation: Stage 1 (B0 — subluxation without cartilage/bone changes), Stage 2 (B1 — partial cartilage loss), Stage 3 (B2 — biconcave erosion). This progression diagram is essential for the audience to understand the clinical significance.
- Optional: **Akgun 2024** — any figures showing the scapulothoracic orientation differences between C1 patients and controls.

---

### Slide 19: "The rTSA Paradox"
- **A simple bar graph** comparing SHR: Normal (3:1) vs. rTSA (1.3:1). You can create this — two bars, clean, labeled. From Walker 2015 data. This is the single most impactful visual for Thread 3.
- **Boileau 2005** — Diagram showing the Grammont design concept: medialized and distalized center of rotation, increased deltoid moment arm. This helps the audience understand WHY the biomechanics change.
- Optional: **Kawashima 2025** — scatter plot or graph showing correlation between SHR and ASES scores if available.

---

### Slide 20: "Why Dyskinesis Persists"
- **Gibbons 2016, Figure 1A** — The sarcomere length-tension curve. This is a classic physiology figure but Gibbons contextualizes it for cuff pathology. Shows how sarcomere length determines force-generating capacity and how massive tears push sarcomeres below the optimal operating range.
- **Burkhart 2003 Part III, Figures 1-2** — Clinical photographs of SICK scapula from posterior and anterior views. These are the original clinical photos that defined the syndrome — the "dropped" scapula, the inferior medial border prominence. Recognizable and memorable.
- **Burkhart 2003 Part III, Figure 6** — The scapular retraction test in action: Panel A showing limited forward flexion with coracoid pain, Panel B showing full flexion restored with manual scapular repositioning. This is essentially the SAT — ties back to your Thread 1 actionable item.

## Notes on Image Use

- Include "Adapted from [Author], [Journal], [Year]" below each figure
- For the SHR bar graph, create your own from the published data — cleaner and avoids copyright
- Consider redrawing key diagrams (CSA measurement, kinetic chain) in a consistent visual style for a more polished look
