## PART 0: THE CORE PRINCIPLE

Everything in this document derives from a single observation: cells respond to mechanical force through conserved transduction pathways, and the specific force environment determines the specific cellular response.

This is not a hypothesis. It is established biology. Engler et al. (Cell, 2006, 10,000+ citations) showed that identical stem cells differentiate into neurons, muscle, or bone depending solely on the mechanical stiffness of their substrate. Ingber (Annu Rev Physiol, 1997; J Cell Sci, 2003; Rep Prog Phys, 2014) showed that the mechanism is cytoskeletal tensegrity: mechanical forces at the cell surface transmit through the actin-microtubule tension network to the nucleus, where they influence gene expression. Wolff's Law is the macroscopic manifestation of this cellular process applied to bone. Thomopoulos showed it governs tendon healing at the enthesis.

The implication for the shoulder: if the mechanical environment determines cellular fate, and if the scapula determines the mechanical environment for every downstream structure, then the scapula is not merely a biomechanical regulator of shoulder function. It is a biological regulator of shoulder tissue fate. The clinical pathology we observe (cuff degeneration, articular cartilage wear, ligament hypertrophy, calcific tendinitis, enthesis failure after repair) is the macroscopic readout of billions of cells responding to their local mechanical environment over years and decades, and that mechanical environment is set, in significant part, by the scapulothoracic articulation.

The diversity of shoulder pathology is emergent complexity arising from a simple underlying rule applied across tissue types and timescales. One upstream variable (scapulothoracic mechanics), one transduction mechanism (cellular mechanotransduction), one governing principle (cells remodel based on their mechanical environment). The specific pathology that develops depends on which cells in which tissue receive which mechanical signal, which in turn depends on the specific alteration in scapulothoracic force distribution.

### The Mechanotransduction Laws of Orthopedics

Several classical orthopedic principles are recognized as independent "laws," but they are all manifestations of the same underlying cellular mechanotransduction operating in different tissue types:

| Law | Year | Tissue | Cell Type | Mechanical Input | Cellular Output |
|-----|------|--------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Roux's Functional Adaptation | 1881 | All | Various | Functional demand | Structure adapts to function |
| Wolff's Law | 1892 | Bone | Osteocyte | Strain | Remodeling along stress lines |
| Davis's Law | — | Soft tissue | Fibroblast | Tension | Collagen deposition along load vector |
| Heuter-Volkmann | — | Physis | Chondrocyte | Compression/tension | Growth rate modulation |
| Frost's Mechanostat | 1987 | Bone | Osteocyte | Strain magnitude | Formation (above MES) vs resorption (below MES) vs woven bone (above pathologic threshold) |
| SAID Principle | — | All | Various | Specific demand | Specific adaptation to that demand |

These are not separate phenomena. They are the same Ingber mechanotransduction machinery expressed in different cell types. Each cell type has its own mechanosensitive gene program, so the same force produces different outputs depending on which cell receives it: osteocytes remodel bone (Wolff), fibroblasts lay down collagen (Davis), chondrocytes modulate proliferation (Heuter-Volkmann).

**The critical implication for this framework:** When scapulothoracic mechanics change, they do not trigger just one of these laws. They trigger ALL of them simultaneously, in every tissue that receives the altered force signal. The bone remodels (Wolff), the tendons remodel (Davis), the cartilage remodels (Heuter-Volkmann equivalent in adult tissue), the ligaments remodel (Davis). This is why one upstream variable (scapulothoracic mechanics) can produce multiple distinct downstream pathologies: it is one cause activating one mechanism in multiple tissues, each responding according to its own cell type and its own version of the same fundamental law.

### Specific Examples of Mechanotransduction Producing Clinical Pathology

**Rotator cuff degeneration:** High CSA produces chronic shear at the supraspinatus enthesis (Viehöfer 2016, Gerber 2014). Tenocytes under chronic shear undergo matrix disorganization, collagen turnover disruption, and eventual failure. The macroscopic result is cuff tearing. The cellular process is mechanotransduction-driven matrix degradation.

**Glenohumeral osteoarthritis:** Low CSA produces chronic compression at the articular surface. Chondrocytes under chronic compression undergo a well-characterized degenerative cascade: proteoglycan loss, collagen disruption, subchondral bone remodeling. The macroscopic result is osteoarthritis. The cellular process is the same mechanotransduction pathway responding to a different input signal (compression rather than shear).

**Posterior glenoid wear (B0 cascade):** If chronic scapulothoracic malposition produces functional retroversion (Bryce 2010 geometry), posterior glenoid chondrocytes receive asymmetric compressive loading that anterior chondrocytes do not. Over decades, asymmetric mechanotransduction produces asymmetric cartilage remodeling. The B0 to B2 progression is the macroscopic readout of decades of differential cellular mechanotransduction driven by scapulothoracic positioning.

**Coracoacromial ligament hypertrophy:** In cuff-deficient shoulders, the humeral head migrates superiorly and loads the coracoacromial ligament chronically. The ligament cells respond to this chronic tensile/compressive loading by producing more matrix (hypertrophy) and, in some cases, undergoing fibrocartilaginous metaplasia with calcification. This is the same mechanotransduction pathway producing a tissue-appropriate response: ligament cells subjected to chronic abnormal loading produce more tissue. Kuhn (2016) noted this ligament is unique to hominids and regenerates after resection, suggesting the cells are constitutively responsive to mechanical demand.

**Calcific tendinitis:** The critical zone of the supraspinatus (Codman's zone, Burkhart's rotator crescent) is where the tendon transitions from tensile loading to compressive loading against the humeral head. If scapulothoracic malposition changes the loading vector, tenocytes in this zone may experience chronic compressive stimulus in tissue designed for tension. The cellular response is fibrocartilaginous metaplasia and calcification: the cells differentiate toward a chondrocyte-like phenotype appropriate for compression, not the tenocyte phenotype appropriate for tension.

**Cuff healing after repair:** Thomopoulos showed that controlled mechanical loading during healing produces organized fibrocartilage at the enthesis, while unloading produces disorganized scar. If scapulothoracic position determines the vector and magnitude of loading at the repair site during the healing window, then post-operative scapulothoracic mechanics may influence whether the repair heals with functional tissue or scar. The quality of healing is a mechanotransduction outcome.

### Compressive Tendinopathy: Cook and Purdam's Framework

Cook and Purdam (BJSM, 2012) formally proposed that compressive load is a distinct and underrecognized driver of tendinopathy. Tendons that wrap around bony prominences or are compressed at entheses develop fibrocartilaginous metaplasia at the compression zone: tenocytes shift toward a chondrocyte-like phenotype, producing type II collagen and proteoglycans instead of type I collagen. This is mechanotransduction producing a tissue-appropriate response to the wrong mechanical signal.

Docking et al. (2013, Cook's group, Muscles Ligaments Tendons J) explicitly linked this to Ingber's tensegrity mechanotransduction model, though they noted that how the cytoskeleton detects compressive stress (vs tensile) remains uninvestigated.

**Clinically recognized compressive tendinopathies:**
- Supraspinatus at the greater tuberosity / coracoacromial arch (rotator cuff disease)
- ECRB against the capitellum (lateral epicondylitis)
- Achilles at the calcaneal insertion (insertional Achilles tendinopathy)
- Posterior tibial tendon at the medial malleolus
- Hamstring at the ischial tuberosity

**Gaps identified in the literature (potential publication targets):**
1. Nobody has formally classified lateral epicondylitis as a compressive tendinopathy using Cook/Purdam's framework, despite the anatomical evidence that pathology concentrates on the ECRB undersurface (the capitellum-facing surface).
2. Nobody has connected Uhthoff's precalcific fibrocartilaginous metaplasia in calcific tendinitis to Cook/Purdam's compressive tendinopathy concept. Uhthoff attributed metaplasia to hypoxia; Cook/Purdam would attribute it to compressive loading. The histopathology is identical.
3. Nobody has built the full bridge: compressive load (Cook/Purdam) → mechanotransduction (Ingber) → metaplasia → calcification (Uhthoff).
4. The contact pressure at the supraspinatus-tuberosity interface as a function of CSA or scapulothoracic position has not been measured. Computational modeling (FEA) could test whether scapular morphology determines the compressive load in the critical zone.

### Osteoarthritis as Mechanotransduction: The Broader Principle

OA is not a disease of destruction. It is a disease of mechanotransduction working correctly but to an abnormal mechanical input. Every tissue in the joint responds appropriately to its local mechanical environment. The pathology arises because the adaptive responses of individual tissues create feedback loops that progressively worsen the mechanical environment for neighboring tissues.

**The OA cascade as sequential mechanotransduction:**
1. Altered joint mechanics (from any cause: malalignment, injury, morphology, obesity)
2. Cartilage receives abnormal loading → chondrocyte mechanotransduction (integrins, TRPV4, primary cilia) → catabolic shift (MMP-13, ADAMTS-5, IL-1) → cartilage thinning
3. Cartilage thinning alters load transfer to subchondral bone → osteocyte mechanotransduction → sclerosis (Wolff's Law)
4. Subchondral sclerosis reduces shock absorption → further cartilage overload (feedback loop)
5. Altered joint geometry → osteophyte formation at joint margins (attempted restabilization: increased surface area reduces contact pressure)
6. Capsule and ligaments subjected to altered mechanics → remodel per Davis's Law
7. New equilibrium: remodeled joint that is stiffer, more constrained, and painful, but structurally stable under its altered loading conditions

**Each hallmark of OA is a specific mechanotransduction response:**

| OA Feature | Cell Type | Mechanical Input | Cellular Response | Governing Law |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cartilage degradation | Chondrocyte | Abnormal compression/shear | Catabolic shift, matrix breakdown | Mechanotransduction |
| Subchondral sclerosis | Osteocyte | Increased compressive load | Bone formation | Wolff's Law |
| Osteophytes | Osteoblast/chondrocyte | Altered joint margins | Bone/cartilage growth to increase surface area | Wolff's Law + adaptive restabilization |
| Capsular thickening | Fibroblast | Altered capsular tension | Collagen deposition | Davis's Law |
| Ligament degeneration | Fibroblast | Altered ligament loading | Mucoid degeneration, metaplasia | Davis's Law / compressive tendinopathy |

Osteophytes deserve special attention. van der Kraan and van den Berg argued that osteophytes are an adaptive attempt to redistribute load and restabilize a joint that has lost its normal mechanical equilibrium. They increase joint conformity, reduce contact pressure (force/area), and increase stability. The joint is "trying" to restore homeostasis through the only mechanism available: growing more tissue where it senses mechanical demand.

**The universal principle (not scapula-specific):** Altered mechanics → mechanotransduction → tissue remodeling → pathological phenotype. This operates at every joint:
- Knee: varus/valgus alignment → medial vs lateral compartment OA
- Hip: cam/pincer morphology → labral vs chondral pathology (FAI)
- Spine: segmental mechanics → disc degeneration pattern
- Ankle: tibial malalignment → talar OA location

The scapula is our entry point because it is where the evidence is clearest and most clinically actionable. But the governing principle is universal.

**Honest limitations:** OA in non-weight-bearing joints of obese patients suggests metabolic/inflammatory contributions beyond pure mechanics (adipokines, systemic inflammation). Genetic factors are significant (twin studies show substantial heritability). Synovial inflammation contributes independently. Mechanotransduction is the dominant but not sole driver.

## PART 1: CORE ASSUMPTIONS

These are the foundational beliefs on which the entire framework rests. Each has been evaluated for evidence quality.

### 1. Physical forces govern biological structures.
Tension, compression, shear, and torsion apply to all material objects. Biological structures are not exempt. Bone bears compression. Tendon bears tension. Cartilage bears compression and shear. **Confidence: Absolute.**

### 2. Evolution selects for metabolically efficient solutions, subordinate to reproductive fitness.
Within the space of structures achieving the same functional outcome, natural selection favors lower metabolic cost. However, efficiency is sacrificed when functional advantage demands it (e.g., the depressed greater tuberosity sacrifices supraspinatus mechanical advantage for throwing range of motion). **Confidence: High. Caveat: efficiency is not the sole optimization target.**

### 3. The human shoulder was significantly shaped by the ability to throw.
Supported by Kuhn 2016 (Am J Orthopedics), Roach 2013 (Nature — elastic energy storage), Selby 2017 (scapular evolution from clambering ancestor). Specific anatomical features (depressed tuberosity, coracoacromial ligament unique to hominids, GH cartilage alignment at 90 degrees abduction) are consistent with throwing adaptations. **Confidence: Moderate-high. Caveat: throwing was one of several selection pressures.**

### 4. Similar physical constraints produce similar structural solutions across biological scales.
Ingber demonstrated tensegrity at the cellular level (Science 1993, Annu Rev Physiol 1997, J Cell Sci 2003, Rep Prog Phys 2014 — over 5,000 combined citations). Chen and Ingber extended the principle from cytoskeleton to skeleton (Osteoarthritis Cartilage 1999). Whether this represents conservation of the same developmental program or convergence on similar solutions from different programs is an open question. **Confidence: High for the general principle. Moderate for tensegrity specifically at the musculoskeletal level.**

### 5. The scapulothoracic articulation functions as a tension-dependent system whose morphology and position determine downstream force distribution.
This is the central testable claim of the framework. Supported by CSA data (Moor, Viehöfer, Davide, Garcia, Gerber 2018), posterior acromial morphology (Meyer, Hochreiter), scapulothoracic orientation differences (Akgun 2024), rTSA kinematics (Walker, Kawashima), and persistent dyskinesis data (Mercurio 2026). **Confidence: High. This is what the perspective paper argues.**

## PART 2: THE FRAMEWORK

### The Scapula as an Upstream Determinant

The scapula has no bony articulation with the axial skeleton. It is an unconstrained system stabilized entirely by the balance of tension forces in the surrounding musculature. Its position at any moment is a vector summation of those tensions. Because the scapula positions the glenoid, anchors the rotator cuff, and transmits force from the trunk to the arm, any change in this tension balance changes force distribution across every downstream structure.

The scapula exerts its upstream influence through two mechanistically distinct pathways:

**Static morphology:** The innate bony architecture (CSA, posterior acromial height, acromial coverage, acromial tilt). Does not change over time (Passaplan 2021). Not modifiable through rehabilitation. Determines the baseline force environment.

**Dynamic positioning:** The scapula's orientation during functional movement, governed by periscapular muscle forces. Potentially modifiable through rehabilitation, postural correction, or surgical intervention. Changes the effective force environment in real time.

### The Mechanical Switch: RCT vs OA

Rotator cuff tears and primary glenohumeral osteoarthritis are inversely related (Moor 2013). The biomechanical explanation centers on the shear-to-compression ratio determined by the CSA:

**High CSA:** The deltoid's force vector has a relatively greater superior shear component. The supraspinatus must work against this shear as a stabilizer rather than functioning as an agonist to the deltoid. The cuff is chronically overloaded (13-33% more SSP force needed per Gerber 2014), predisposing to degeneration and tearing. The articular surface is relatively spared from compressive wear. Result: cuff tears without OA.

**Low CSA:** The deltoid's vector is directed more compressively into the glenoid. Cuff and deltoid function as agonists. The cartilage bears greater cumulative compressive load over decades. The cuff is relatively spared. Result: OA without cuff tears.

The scapula's morphology, through a single geometric parameter, acts as a mechanical switch determining which downstream structure is preferentially loaded and which pathology develops. [SUPPORTED — Moor 2013, Viehöfer 2016, Gerber 2014]

### The Self-Reinforcing Cascade

When the scapula shifts to a new position, the consequences extend beyond kinematics through a series of individually well-understood physiological mechanisms:

1. **Positional shift** changes the resting lengths of every attached muscle
2. **Altered resting length** moves each muscle to a different point on its length-tension curve, changing force output for a given neural activation (Gibbons 2016 demonstrated this pathologically in the cuff)
3. **Altered force output** changes the balance of the force couples controlling scapular rotation (Bagg and Forrest 1988, Ludewig 2000)
4. **Altered force couple balance** may disrupt the timing and sequencing of muscle activation as the neuromuscular system recalibrates (Hodges 1996 established feedforward timing disruption at the spinal level; Cools 2007 showed altered trapezius timing in impingement)
5. **Sustained positional change** triggers sarcomere remodeling — muscles add or subtract sarcomeres in series to optimize for the new (suboptimal) position (Williams and Goldspink animal models; Gibbons 2016 human cuff data)
6. **Sarcomere remodeling** structurally commits the system to the dysfunctional position, making it resistant to simple strengthening

What begins as a positional shift becomes a self-reinforcing pattern of altered mechanics, altered motor control, and altered muscle architecture. This explains why scapular dyskinesis persists after cuff repair (Mercurio 2026) and why rehabilitation must be sustained long enough for sarcomere remodeling (weeks to months, per Burkhart 2003 — full repositioning at 3 months).

### The B0 Hypothesis: Dynamic Glenoid Version

Bryce 2010 (JBJS, 147 citations) showed scapular rotation changes measured glenoid version by up to 22.5 degrees. Framed as a CT measurement artifact, we propose reframing as a functional biomechanical variable: a scapula that chronically sits in more internal rotation presents a functionally more retroverted glenoid during loading.

Akgun 2024 showed C1 posterior decentering patients have different scapulothoracic orientation (less upward rotation, less tilt) but also less thoracic kyphosis, suggesting the mechanism is more complex than posture alone.

The cumulative loading argument: the magnitude of functional version change on any given day may be modest, but articular cartilage degeneration is driven by cumulative loading over time. A small persistent asymmetry maintained over years represents a meaningful mechanical dose. The B0 to B2 progression occurs over decades, consistent with cumulative loading.

**Causation is unestablished. This is the most speculative component of the framework.**

### The rTSA Paradox

rTSA converts the shoulder from tension-based stability (cuff providing dynamic centering) to geometry-based constraint (prosthetic ball-and-socket). The scapulothoracic system, still tension-dependent, now contributes approximately half of arm elevation (SHR 1.3:1 vs 3:1 normal, Walker 2015). Better SHR correlates with better outcomes (Kawashima 2025: ASES 94.3 vs 82.1, P=0.007). Moroder 2020 showed scapulothoracic orientation influences optimal component positioning (R=0.91). Shekhbihi 2025 called this "the scapulothoracic conundrum."

The prehab rationale: if periscapular muscles have undergone sarcomere remodeling from years of dysfunction, restoring their architecture before imposing new biomechanical demands (rTSA) should produce better outcomes than rehabilitating afterward. Camargo 2015 RCT supports scapular rehab effectiveness in impingement. Untested in arthroplasty population.

### The Tensegrity Lens

The scapulothoracic articulation fits the definition of tensegrity architecture: a compression element (scapula) floating in a continuous tension network (periscapular muscles) with no bony constraint. Stability emerges from the balance of tension rather than compressive stacking.

Clinical observations consistent with tensegrity predictions:
- Pre-stress determines stiffness (Kebaetse 1999: slouching reduces abduction by 23.6 degrees and force by 16.2%)
- Altered compression element geometry changes force distribution (CSA data, posterior acromial morphology)
- Local perturbation produces global reorganization (Borstad 2005: pec minor length changes kinematics in 3 planes; Burkhart 2003: SICK scapula produces symptoms at multiple anatomic sites)
- System self-stabilizes at new equilibrium after perturbation (Mercurio 2026: dyskinesis persists after repair)

Whether tensegrity is the correct formal model or a useful approximation remains open. We use it as a lens, not a claim. The observations stand regardless of whether the label is accepted.

### Alternative Biomechanical Models

The shoulder can be modeled through multiple frameworks, each capturing different aspects:

- **Lever mechanics:** Calculates joint forces accurately. Standard in implant design. Treats joints in isolation.
- **Linked segment / kinetic chain:** Explains sequential force transmission (Kibler's 51% data). Doesn't explain scapulothoracic stabilization.
- **Tensegrity:** Explains scapulothoracic suspension and global connectivity. Doesn't account for active neural control.
- **Spring-mass-damper:** Handles variable stiffness and dynamic responses. Muscles as variable-stiffness springs tuned by the nervous system. Pre-stress in tensegrity maps onto baseline muscle stiffness.
- **Musculoskeletal computational models (OpenSim, AnyBody):** Most complete. Doesn't assume a structural paradigm. Could validate or refute tensegrity predictions if applied to the scapulothoracic system.
- **Elastic energy storage (Roach 2013):** Explains throwing performance. Subscapularis and biceps as springs. Thorax as torsional spring. Applies specifically to high-energy movements.
- **Optimal control / Bernstein's problem:** The scapula has more degrees of freedom than needed. The CNS solves the redundancy problem. Dyskinesis may represent an energy-minimizing compensation given altered constraints.

No single model is sufficient. The scapulothoracic system likely requires tensegrity (for the structural logic), spring-mass-damper (for the dynamic neural control), and elastic energy (for throwing function) simultaneously.

### Pre-stress vs Triangulation: Two Distinct Tensegrity Principles

Two tensegrity principles apply to the scapulothoracic system, each with different clinical implications:

**Pre-stress** is the baseline tension maintained in a tensegrity structure before any external load is applied. In the scapulothoracic system, this corresponds to resting muscle tone. Pre-stress determines system stiffness: higher pre-stress produces a stiffer, more resilient structure that resists perturbation. This is why Kebaetse (1999) found that slouching (reduced pre-stress) decreased abduction by 23.6 degrees and force by 16.2%. Pre-stress is the more clinically modifiable of the two principles: it can be altered through strengthening, postural training, and motor control work. When we talk about rehabilitating scapular dysfunction, we are largely talking about restoring appropriate pre-stress.

**Triangulation** is the geometric constraint that determines rotational control. A minimum of 3 non-coplanar force vectors is required for full 3D rotational control of a free body. The scapula is controlled by force couples: upper trap / lower trap (upward rotation), serratus anterior / rhomboids (protraction-retraction), and pec minor / lower trap (tilt). These form the triangulated control architecture. Triangulation is an architectural constraint, not easily modifiable: you cannot add a new force vector, only optimize the ones that exist.

**Clinical Relevance:** Pre-stress failure manifests as weakness and kinematic drift. The system is intact but under-tensioned. Strengthening can restore it. Triangulation failure manifests as loss of rotational control in a specific plane. If one vector of a force couple is lost (e.g., serratus anterior weakness from long thoracic palsy), the system cannot control protraction-retraction regardless of how much pre-stress the remaining muscles generate. Treatment requires restoring the specific vector, not just global strengthening.

Both principles operate simultaneously. A well-functioning scapulothoracic system requires adequate pre-stress (baseline stiffness) AND intact triangulation (full 3D rotational control). Dysfunction can arise from failure of either, and the rehabilitation approach depends on identifying which has failed.

### Pain Location as a Diagnostic Indicator of Primary Muscle Deficit

In a triangulated tension system, when one tension element fails, the remaining elements must compensate by increasing their tension to maintain the compression element's position. The compensating elements are loaded beyond their design parameters, and pain develops at the compensating element's insertion rather than at the site of the primary failure.

This generates testable clinical predictions:

**Coracoid pain (anterior):** The pectoralis minor inserts on the coracoid process. When the serratus anterior and/or lower trapezius are weak (primary deficit), the scapula loses its posterior tilt and upward rotation control. The pec minor, as an anterior tilt and downward rotation force, is recruited to compensate for the rotational imbalance. It becomes chronically overloaded, and pain develops at or near the coracoid. The primary problem is posterior (serratus/lower trap weakness), but the pain is anterior (pec minor overload at its insertion).

Physical manifestations: increased scapular anterior tilt, decreased upward rotation, tight pec minor on length testing, weak serratus anterior and lower trap on manual testing.

**Medial upper scapular border pain:** The levator scapulae inserts on the superior medial border. When the middle trapezius and/or rhomboids are weak (primary deficit), the scapula loses retraction control. The levator scapulae compensates by becoming the primary scapular stabilizer against protraction forces. It becomes chronically overloaded, and pain develops at its insertion on the medial superior border. Again, the primary failure is in one force vector (retraction), but pain appears at the compensating element.

Physical manifestations: increased scapular protraction, weak middle trap and rhomboids on manual testing, tender levator scapulae, possible superior medial scapular winging.

This is consistent with Burkhart's (2003) description of the SICK scapula, where he documented pain at the coracoid, superior medial border, and posterosuperior glenoid in patients with scapular malposition, stating that the pain patterns map to specific areas of mechanical overload rather than to the site of primary dysfunction.

**Principle:** In a triangulated system, pain locates to the compensating element, not the failing element. Treatment should target the primary deficit (the weak muscle), not the painful structure (the overloaded compensator). Treating the painful element without addressing the primary deficit will produce temporary relief followed by recurrence.

### Honest Limitations and Competing Explanations

**Intrinsic tendon biology:** Age-related degeneration, vascular insufficiency, oxidative stress, and genetic predisposition contribute to cuff disease independently of scapulothoracic mechanics. Twin studies demonstrate a heritable component. This framework highlights an additional variable, not a replacement for these factors.

**Acromial morphology debate:** Not all studies replicate CSA associations (Arnar 2017: OR 1.12, P=0.63 for RCT). The role of acromial morphology in cuff pathology remains debated. Confounding by shared genetic factors cannot be excluded.

**The Janda comparison:** Vladimir Janda proposed upper/lower cross syndromes based primarily on clinical observation without rigorous biomechanical validation. His framework was intuitive but lacked quantitative evidence. This program differs in that its core claims (CSA predicts pathology, posterior acromial morphology predicts instability) are supported by Level III evidence with biomechanical validation. The tensegrity framing and B0 hypothesis are more speculative and should be held to a higher standard before being presented as established.
