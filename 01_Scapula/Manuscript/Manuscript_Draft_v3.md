# Rethinking the Scapula: How a Single Upstream Structure May Influence the Spectrum of Shoulder Pathology

Brad Roepke, MD

Department of Orthopedic Surgery, University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, Oklahoma City, OK

Corresponding author: Brad Roepke, MD, Department of Orthopedic Surgery, University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, P.O. Box 26901, Oklahoma City, OK 73126. Email: brad.roepke@example.com

## Abstract

The scapula has no bony articulation with the axial skeleton and is stabilized entirely by the balance of tension forces in the surrounding musculature. Its position is therefore a vector summation of the tensions acting upon it. Because the scapula positions the glenoid, anchors the rotator cuff, and transmits force from the trunk to the arm, alterations in this tension balance influence force distribution across every downstream structure. Recent evidence suggests that the scapula exerts this upstream influence through two mechanisms, its static morphology and its dynamic positioning, producing distinct downstream pathologies depending on which structure is overloaded. We present a framework unifying four clinical examples of this relationship and propose testable predictions for clinical investigation.

Level of Evidence: Level V; Expert Opinion / Narrative Review

Keywords: scapula; scapulothoracic; critical shoulder angle; tensegrity; reverse shoulder arthroplasty; scapulohumeral rhythm; posterior instability; glenoid wear

## Introduction

Shoulder pathology encompasses a broad spectrum of conditions, including rotator cuff tears, glenohumeral instability, osteoarthritis, and arthroplasty complications, each of which is typically studied as a distinct entity with a separate etiology. Yet a convergence has emerged in recent literature that is difficult to ignore: investigations of each pathology independently identify the scapula as a significant contributing factor.

The critical shoulder angle, a measurement of scapular morphology, predicts both rotator cuff tears and osteoarthritis.(1-3) Posterior acromial morphology predicts the direction of glenohumeral instability.(4,5) Scapulothoracic orientation differs significantly in patients with constitutional posterior humeral decentering.(6) Scapulohumeral rhythm after reverse total shoulder arthroplasty (rTSA) correlates with functional outcomes.(7,8) And scapular dyskinesis persists after successful rotator cuff repair, raising the possibility that scapular dysfunction may precede rather than follow cuff pathology.(9)

These findings, taken together, raise a question worth considering: are these separate observations, or are they different manifestations of a single biomechanical reality?

The senior author's interest in this question grew out of a personal experience with scapulothoracic dysfunction that was initially misattributed to a neurological deficit. The process of understanding how an abnormal scapular position could alter the mechanical effectiveness of muscles throughout the upper extremity, and ultimately recognizing the resolution of symptoms through targeted scapular retraining, prompted a deeper investigation into the scapula's role as a biomechanical regulator of shoulder function. The literature investigation that followed revealed a convergence that we believe warrants broader discussion.

The purpose of this paper is not to claim that the scapulothoracic articulation explains all shoulder pathology, but to draw attention to a pattern in the evidence that has not been fully appreciated. The scapula, as a bone with no bony connection to the axial skeleton that is stabilized entirely by muscular tension, occupies a unique position in the shoulder girdle. Its morphology and its position influence force distribution to every downstream structure, and the specific pathology that develops in a given patient may depend, at least in part, on which downstream structure bears the consequence of the particular alteration in scapulothoracic mechanics. We present this framework as a starting point for discussion and further investigation, not as a definitive model.

## The Scapulothoracic Articulation: A Summation of Tension Forces

The scapula is the only major bone in the shoulder girdle with no direct bony articulation to the axial skeleton. It has no joint capsule connecting it to the thorax and no ligamentous attachment to the spine or rib cage. From an engineering perspective, this makes the scapulothoracic articulation an unconstrained system: all stability is provided by the muscles that surround it rather than by bony or ligamentous architecture. Its position on the thorax is determined entirely by the balance of tension in these muscles, including the serratus anterior, the three divisions of the trapezius, the rhomboids, the levator scapulae, the pectoralis minor, and the muscles of the rotator cuff and deltoid that originate from its surface. Scapular rotation during arm elevation is produced by coordinated force couples, particularly the upper trapezius acting with the serratus anterior and lower trapezius to generate upward rotation, a relationship that has been validated through three-dimensional electromagnetic tracking and electromyographic analysis.(12,32,34) This arrangement, in which a compression element is suspended within a continuous tension network with no bony constraint, is consistent with what structural engineers and cell biologists describe as tensegrity architecture, a principle validated extensively at the cellular level by Ingber and colleagues(25-27) and extended to the musculoskeletal system by Chen and Ingber.(28)

This anatomical arrangement carries an underappreciated mechanical consequence. The scapula's position at any moment is a vector summation of all the tension forces acting upon it. Altering any single tension element, whether through weakness, tightness, fatigue, neural inhibition, or structural injury, changes the summation and shifts the scapula to a new equilibrium position.

The sensitivity of this system has been quantified in several investigations. Kebaetse et al. demonstrated that a change in thoracic posture alone, simply slouching, reduces active shoulder abduction by 23.6 degrees and abduction muscle force by 16.2%.(10) Borstad showed that differences in pectoralis minor resting length among healthy individuals alter scapular kinematics in three rotational planes simultaneously.(11) These are not pathologic populations; they are healthy subjects in whom a single alteration in the tension balance produces measurable kinematic consequences. The subacromial space, as reviewed by Borstad, has been measured at 4.1 mm in healthy subjects at 90 degrees of elevation and found to narrow to 1.4 mm in subjects with impingement,(11) illustrating how small the margin is between functional and pathologic mechanics, and how directly scapulothoracic positioning influences that margin.

The downstream consequences of a scapular positional change may be more extensive than the kinematic data alone suggest. When the scapula shifts to a new resting position, the resting lengths of every muscle attached to it change accordingly. Because skeletal muscle generates force as a function of sarcomere length, a change in resting position moves each periscapular muscle to a different point on its length-tension curve, altering the force it produces for a given level of neural activation.(24) This in turn changes the balance of the force couples that control scapular rotation,(34) which may alter the timing and sequencing of muscle activation as the neuromuscular system recalibrates to the new mechanical environment.(35,36) If sustained, these positional changes may eventually be reinforced by structural sarcomere remodeling that optimizes the muscle for the new, suboptimal position rather than the original one.(24) What begins as a positional shift can therefore progress, through a series of individually well-understood physiological mechanisms, into a self-reinforcing pattern of altered mechanics, altered motor control, and altered muscle architecture.

Beyond its role as a kinematic regulator, the scapula serves as the sole link between the axial skeleton and the upper extremity in the kinetic chain. Kibler reported that 51% of total kinetic energy and 54% of total force in a tennis serve originate from the legs and trunk, with all of it transmitted through the scapulothoracic articulation.(12) Far from being a passive platform, the scapula is the critical relay station through which the body's entire force production reaches the arm.

## Two Modes of Upstream Influence

Before examining the clinical evidence, an important distinction must be drawn. The scapula exerts its upstream influence through two mechanistically distinct pathways.

The first is static morphology: the innate bony architecture of the acromion and glenoid. This includes the critical shoulder angle, the posterior acromial height, acromial coverage, and acromial tilt. Passaplan et al. showed that the CSA does not change over a decade, even in shoulders that develop new rotator cuff tears during follow-up, establishing these parameters as stable, innate anatomical features.(17) Static morphology is not modifiable through rehabilitation or behavioral change; it is something the patient is born with.

The second is dynamic positioning: the scapula's orientation on the thorax during functional movement, which is governed by the balance of periscapular muscle forces and is potentially modifiable through rehabilitation, postural correction, or surgical intervention. Dynamic positioning is what Kebaetse,(10) Borstad,(11) and Ludewig(32) have measured in their kinematic studies.

Both mechanisms converge at the same outcome: they alter the force environment experienced by downstream structures. The following clinical examples illustrate how each mechanism operates.

## Scapular Morphology and Downstream Pathology

### Rotator Cuff Disease

The relationship between scapular morphology and rotator cuff disease has been progressively clarified over the past two decades. Nyffeler et al. introduced the acromion index, demonstrating that the lateral extension of the acromion is significantly greater in patients with rotator cuff tears (AI 0.73) than in those with intact cuffs (AI 0.64, P<0.0001).(13) Building on this observation, Moor et al. developed the critical shoulder angle (CSA), which combines lateral acromial extension with glenoid inclination into a single measurement (Figure 1). In their study of 298 shoulders, the mean CSA was 33.1 degrees in controls, 38.0 degrees in rotator cuff tears, and 28.1 degrees in osteoarthritis, with an AUC of 0.92 for discriminating cuff tears from controls and 0.90 for osteoarthritis.(1)

[FIGURE 1: Garcia CSA measurement]

A particularly striking feature of Moor's data is that rotator cuff tears and primary glenohumeral osteoarthritis are inversely related: high CSA predicts cuff tears while low CSA predicts OA, and the two conditions rarely coexist. This inverse relationship is itself mechanistically informative. The biomechanical explanation, elucidated through complementary modeling approaches, centers on the shear-to-compression ratio at the glenohumeral joint. Using finite element modeling, Viehöfer et al. demonstrated that a CSA of 38 degrees produces a 23% higher shear-to-compression ratio at 50 degrees of abduction compared to a normal CSA of 33 degrees, requiring supraspinatus forces that exceed physiological values to maintain joint stability.(2) Gerber et al. confirmed these findings on a physical shoulder simulator, showing that supraspinatus tendon load must increase by 13 to 33% (15 to 23 N) for a CSA of 38 degrees compared to 33 degrees.(14) In a high-CSA shoulder, the deltoid generates a relatively greater superior shear vector, and the supraspinatus must work against this shear to keep the humeral head centered. The cuff functions as a stabilizer against shear rather than as an agonist to the deltoid, chronically overloading it and predisposing to degeneration and tearing while relatively sparing the articular surface from compressive wear. In a low-CSA shoulder, the deltoid's force vector is directed more compressively into the glenoid, the cuff and deltoid function as agonists rather than antagonists, and the articular cartilage bears a greater cumulative compressive load over time, predisposing to osteoarthritis while relatively sparing the cuff.(1,2,14) The scapula's morphology, through a single geometric parameter, acts as a mechanical switch that determines which downstream structure is preferentially loaded and which pathology ultimately develops. Blonna et al. further demonstrated a dose-response relationship within the cuff tear population: CSA averaged 34 degrees in controls, 36 degrees with isolated supraspinatus tears, and 40 degrees with combined supraspinatus and infraspinatus tears. After controlling for age, gender, smoking, and BMI, the CSA remained the most relevant risk factor (OR 1.7, CI 1.4 to 2.0).(3)

What elevates these findings from academic observation to clinical significance is the surgical outcome data. Garcia et al. found that a CSA exceeding 38 degrees produces an odds ratio of 14.8 for full-thickness retear after arthroscopic rotator cuff repair (P<0.01).(15) In response to the emerging biomechanical evidence, Gerber et al. showed that arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty (lateral, and not the traditional anterior approach described by Neer) safely reduces the CSA from a mean of 37.5 degrees to 33.9 degrees (P<0.001) without compromising the deltoid origin. Patients whose CSA was corrected to 33 degrees or less had 25% greater abduction strength than those whose postoperative CSA remained at or above 35 degrees (P=0.04), and postoperative CSA was significantly larger in failed repairs than in healed repairs (P=0.026).(16) A systematic review by Docter et al. further examined the relationship between CSA and both cuff tear incidence and repair outcomes, corroborating the association in the majority of included studies while noting the heterogeneity in the literature.(33)

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that not all studies have replicated these findings. Bjarnison et al. found CSA associated with osteoarthritis (OR 2.25, P=0.002) but not with rotator cuff tears (OR 1.12, P=0.63) in their cohort.(18) The weight of evidence tilts toward CSA being clinically relevant, but the debate remains live.

The observation most relevant to the framework we propose is this: the CSA is a measurement of scapular morphology. The acromion is the scapula. An innate morphological feature of the upstream structure influences the force environment of the downstream structure.

### Posterior Glenohumeral Instability

A parallel line of investigation from Gerber's research group has revealed a similar upstream relationship for glenohumeral instability, operating through a different anatomical parameter of the same bone. Meyer et al. found that posterior acromial morphology is significantly associated with posterior shoulder instability (Figure 2, Figure 3). Posterior acromial height (PAH) measured 30.9 mm in patients with posterior instability versus 19.5 mm in those with anterior instability (P<0.001). At a cutoff of PAH of 23 mm or greater, the odds ratio for posterior instability was 39.(4)

[FIGURE 3: Gerber 3D overlay]

Akgun et al. expanded on these morphological findings using three-dimensional computed tomography, demonstrating that multiple scapular parameters, including anterior and posterior acromial coverage, posterior acromial height, posterior acromial tilt, and the CSA, all differ significantly in patients with constitutional posterior decentering compared to healthy controls.(6)

The biomechanical significance of these morphological differences was established by Hochreiter et al., who tested three-dimensional printed scapula models and found that correcting glenoid retroversion alone was not sufficient to restore normal posterior translation. Both glenoid and acromial anatomy must be corrected to normalize posterior stability.(5) This finding establishes the acromion as an active restraint to posterior humeral head translation, not a passive bystander. Gerber et al. have since translated these findings into clinical practice with three-dimensional planned scapular osteotomies, though this approach remains in its early stages and is not yet widely adopted.(19)

The pattern is the same as for cuff disease, but the downstream pathology is different. An innate morphological feature of the scapula shapes the mechanical restraint environment of the glenohumeral joint. Change the scapular morphology, and you change which direction the shoulder fails.

## Scapulothoracic Positioning and Downstream Pathology

### Posterior Glenoid Wear: The Walch B0 Hypothesis

The most speculative extension of this framework, but perhaps its most intellectually compelling application, concerns the Walch B0 glenoid. Domos et al. described this entity as pre-arthritic posterior subluxation of the humeral head, a condition that appears to progress inevitably toward eccentric posterior glenoid erosion through the B1 and B2 stages (Figure 4).(20)

[FIGURE 4: B0 progression]

A key observation provides the geometric foundation for a hypothesis linking scapulothoracic positioning to this progression. Bryce et al. showed that scapular rotation changes measured glenoid version by up to 22.5 degrees, with 0.42 degrees of relative anteversion for every degree of scapular abduction in the coronal plane.(21) Their paper framed this finding as a computed tomography measurement artifact, cautioning clinicians about the influence of scapular position on version calculations. We propose an alternative interpretation: if scapular position dynamically changes effective glenoid version during functional movement, then a scapula that chronically sits in more internal rotation, whether from habitual posture, pectoralis minor tightness, thoracic kyphosis, or individual morphological variation, would present a functionally more retroverted glenoid during loading.

Recent data from Akgun et al. lend support to this concept. Their study found that patients with constitutional posterior humeral decentering (the C1 phenotype, which is analogous to the B0 glenoid) have significantly different scapulothoracic orientation compared to healthy controls, including less scapular upward rotation and less scapular tilt, alongside different posterior acromial morphology and altered muscle volume distribution (Figure 5).(6)

[FIGURE 5: Akgun ST orientation]

Causation has not been established. The scapulothoracic orientation differences that Akgun observed could be a consequence of the posterior decentering rather than its cause. It is also notable that C1 patients in Akgun's cohort exhibited less thoracic kyphosis than controls, not more, suggesting that the scapulothoracic orientation differences in these patients may be driven by intrinsic morphological factors rather than postural habits alone, and that the relationship between scapular position and posterior decentering is likely more complex than a simple posture-driven model would predict. Nonetheless, the geometry is clear: scapulothoracic position changes effective glenoid version,(21) and patients with posterior decentering have different scapulothoracic position.(6) The magnitude of functional version change from scapulothoracic malposition on any given day may be modest. However, articular cartilage degeneration is increasingly understood to be driven by cumulative mechanical loading over time rather than by acute events. A small but persistent asymmetry in posterior glenoid loading, maintained over years of daily functional use, could represent a meaningful cumulative mechanical dose even if the instantaneous force asymmetry on any single day falls within the tolerance of healthy cartilage. This is consistent with the clinical observation that the B0 to B2 progression occurs over decades, not months, and with the broader understanding that osteoarthritis reflects the cumulative history of mechanical loading on a joint surface. Whether chronic dynamic functional retroversion from scapulothoracic malposition contributes to this cumulative loading is a testable question that, to our knowledge, has not yet been asked.

Note that this hypothesis operates through the second mode of upstream influence (dynamic positioning) rather than the first (static morphology). The B0 section thus complements the CSA and posterior acromial sections, which operate through innate morphology. Together, they suggest that the scapula influences downstream pathology through both mechanisms.

### Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty

When a reverse shoulder prosthesis is implanted, the biomechanics of the glenohumeral joint change fundamentally, and the consequences for the scapulothoracic system are significant. Walker et al. measured scapulohumeral rhythm using fluoroscopy with three-dimensional model-image registration across three different implant designs and found that SHR in rTSA is 1.3:1, compared to 3:1 in normal shoulders.(7) This means the scapulothoracic system contributes approximately half of arm elevation after rTSA, a dramatic increase from its normal contribution.

Kawashima et al. found that this altered scapulothoracic contribution has direct clinical consequences in a study of 20 patients with semi-inlay rTSA. Patients with SHR of 2 or greater had significantly higher ASES scores (94.3, SD 4.6) than those with SHR below 2 (82.1, SD 9.4, P=0.007), with a significant positive correlation between SHR and patient-reported outcomes (r=0.452, P=0.045).(8) While the sample size limits the generalizability of this finding, its consistency with the larger Walker dataset and with the broader biomechanical framework presented here suggests that further investigation in a properly powered study is warranted. Moroder et al. showed that scapulothoracic orientation directly influences optimal humeral component retrotorsion in rTSA, with scapular internal rotation correlating with the correction angle at R=0.91 when adjusted for glenoid retroversion.(22) Shekhbihi et al. framed this collective body of work as "the scapulothoracic conundrum," calling for integration of scapulothoracic assessment into rTSA planning and rehabilitation.(23)

The clinical significance of persistent scapulothoracic dysfunction is underscored by kinematic data from Mercurio et al., who prospectively showed that scapular dyskinesis persists after successful arthroscopic rotator cuff repair despite improved range of motion and Constant scores; scapulohumeral rhythm showed no significant change in their cohort (P>0.05) (Figure 6).(9) If dyskinesis were simply a consequence of the cuff tear, one would expect it to resolve after repair. The fact that it does not suggests that the scapulothoracic dysfunction is either independent of, or upstream of, the cuff pathology.

[FIGURE 6: Mercurio kinematic data]

The biological mechanism underlying this persistence may involve structural muscle remodeling. Gibbons et al. showed that massive rotator cuff tears produce sarcomere loss, fiber shortening, and, in massive tears, sarcomere length below the optimal operating range, representing architectural changes that may be irreversible even with successful tendon repair.(24) If similar remodeling occurs in the periscapular muscles of patients with chronic shoulder dysfunction (biologically plausible but unproven), then the scapulothoracic system may be structurally committed to dysfunction before surgery is performed. This reasoning provides a case for scapulothoracic prehabilitation before shoulder surgery: not to strengthen muscles in the conventional sense, but to restore sarcomere architecture and tension balance while remodeling remains possible, before imposing the new biomechanical demands of a prosthesis. Evidence from a randomized trial by Camargo et al. demonstrating that scapular-focused exercise improves kinematics, function, and pain in patients with impingement lends plausibility to the hypothesis that targeted prehabilitation could similarly benefit arthroplasty patients, though this has not been directly tested.(31)

## The Unifying Principle

The examples above involve four different pathologies, four different patient populations, and four different downstream structures failing. In every case, however, the scapula is upstream. In every case, an alteration in the scapula's morphology or position is associated with changed force distribution to a downstream structure. And in every case, the specific pathology that develops depends on which downstream structure bears the consequence.

One mechanism that may explain how the same upstream alteration produces different pathologies in different structures is cellular mechanotransduction. The principle that cells respond to mechanical force through conserved transduction pathways is well established: Ingber demonstrated that forces at the cell surface transmit through the cytoskeleton to the nucleus, influencing gene expression,(25-27) and Engler et al. showed that identical stem cells differentiate into neurons, muscle, or bone depending solely on substrate stiffness.(37) In orthopedics, this principle is already recognized in tissue-specific forms: Wolff's Law (bone remodels along lines of stress), Davis's Law (soft tissue remodels along lines of imposed demand), and the Heuter-Volkmann principle (physeal growth responds to compression and tension). These are not separate phenomena but the same cellular machinery expressed in different cell types. When scapulothoracic mechanics change, they do not trigger one of these responses in isolation; they alter the force environment for bone, tendon, cartilage, and ligament simultaneously, and each tissue responds according to its own mechanosensitive program. The diversity of downstream pathology may therefore reflect not multiple causes but a single upstream alteration activating a single transduction mechanism across multiple tissues over time.

This pattern is also consistent with the structural principle of tensegrity (Figure 7). As introduced earlier, tensegrity describes a system where compression elements float within a continuous tension network and stability emerges from the balance of tension rather than from compressive stacking.(25-27) The scapulothoracic articulation fits this description precisely: a compression element with no bony attachment, suspended entirely within a continuous tension network, with its position determined by the vector summation of forces in that network.

[FIGURE 7: Tensegrity models]

The clinical observations presented in this paper align with several core predictions of tensegrity-organized systems: that pre-stress in the tension network determines system stiffness and function (consistent with the posture-dependent kinematic and strength changes demonstrated by Kebaetse(10)), that alteration of the compression element's geometry changes force distribution throughout the network (consistent with the CSA and posterior acromial morphology data(1-3,14-16)), that local perturbation produces global reorganization (consistent with Borstad's observation that pectoralis minor length changes kinematics in three planes simultaneously(11) and with Burkhart's observation that scapular malposition produces symptoms at multiple anatomic sites(29)), and that the system self-stabilizes at a new equilibrium after perturbation (consistent with the persistence of dyskinesis after cuff repair demonstrated by Mercurio(9)).

Whether tensegrity is the correct structural model for the scapulothoracic system in a formal, quantitative sense remains an open question. Computational modeling of the scapulothoracic articulation as a tensegrity structure has not been performed, and the active neural control of periscapular musculature introduces complexity that passive structural models cannot fully capture. We raise the tensegrity concept here not as a claim to be defended, but as a lens that appears to organize the clinical observations presented in this paper in a way that other models do not. If the analogy is imperfect, the observations themselves remain, and they point consistently toward the scapula as the common upstream variable in shoulder pathology.

## Questions Raised and Directions for Investigation

If the framework presented here has merit, several questions follow naturally, both for clinical practice and for future study.

On the clinical side, the CSA is a measurement that takes 30 seconds on a standard anteroposterior radiograph and has demonstrated excellent reliability.(1) Given the associations with both cuff tear development and retear after repair, routine measurement of the CSA on pre-operative shoulder films seems reasonable and low-cost. Whether lateral acromioplasty should be performed as an adjunct to cuff repair when the CSA exceeds a certain threshold is a question that Gerber's data supports(16) but that has not been evaluated in a randomized fashion. The standard anterior acromioplasty described by Neer does not alter the CSA; only lateral acromioplasty addresses this parameter. The scapular assistance test, in which the examiner manually repositions the scapula during arm elevation, offers a simple way to assess whether scapulothoracic positioning is contributing to a patient's symptoms and may be a useful addition to the clinical examination.(30)

Several lines of investigation emerge from this framework that, to our knowledge, have not been formally explored. The first concerns pre-operative scapulothoracic function as a predictor of surgical outcomes. If the scapulothoracic system bears increasing demands after rTSA, and if its function correlates with outcomes as Kawashima's data suggest,(8) then prospective assessment of scapulothoracic kinematics and periscapular muscle function before surgery could have prognostic value. A second question concerns the role of prehabilitation. The evidence that scapular-focused exercise can improve kinematics and function in patients with impingement(31) raises the possibility that structured scapulothoracic rehabilitation before arthroplasty, initiated early enough to allow for potential sarcomere remodeling,(24) could improve the system's capacity to meet the demands imposed by a prosthesis. This remains untested. A third question concerns the B0 glenoid. If scapulothoracic positioning dynamically alters effective glenoid version during functional loading, as Bryce's geometric data suggest,(21) then measurement of dynamic glenoid version using biplanar fluoroscopy could reveal whether patients who develop posterior wear have greater functional retroversion than their static imaging would indicate. This would require a dedicated study but could fundamentally change how we think about the early stages of posterior glenoid disease.

A fourth question, less explored in the orthopedic literature, concerns the role of motor control timing in scapulothoracic dysfunction. Work by Hodges and Richardson established that deep spinal stabilizers activate in a feedforward pattern, 30 to 100 milliseconds before limb movement, in healthy subjects, and that this anticipatory timing is disrupted by pain and injury.(35) If a similar feedforward mechanism operates at the scapulothoracic level, with the serratus anterior and lower trapezius pre-activating to set the scapular platform before deltoid engagement, then some forms of scapular dyskinesis may represent timing deficits rather than pure force deficits. This distinction has implications for rehabilitation design, as motor control retraining targets a different mechanism than conventional strengthening. Altered trapezius activation timing has been observed in overhead athletes with impingement,(36) suggesting that this line of investigation may be productive.

None of these questions have definitive answers at present. We raise them because the convergence of evidence presented in this paper suggests they are worth asking.

## Limitations

Several limitations of this framework must be acknowledged. This paper emphasizes extrinsic mechanical factors in shoulder pathology. A substantial body of literature implicates intrinsic tendon biology, including age-related degeneration, vascular insufficiency, oxidative stress, and genetic predisposition, as important contributors to rotator cuff disease that operate independently of scapulothoracic mechanics. Twin and family studies have demonstrated a significant heritable component to rotator cuff tears that cannot be explained by mechanical loading alone. The framework presented here is not intended to displace these factors but to highlight an additional variable that may interact with them. The tensegrity model at the musculoskeletal level remains qualitative; while the clinical predictions match observations, quantitative computational modeling of the scapulothoracic system as a tensegrity structure has not been performed. The B0 hypothesis is largely speculative, as causation between scapulothoracic orientation and posterior glenoid wear has not been established. The scapulothoracic system is actively controlled by the nervous system, introducing a level of complexity that static structural models cannot capture. The individual studies cited in this paper range from Level I (randomized controlled trial(31)) to Level IV (case series(19)), and the overall quality of evidence supporting the framework as a whole, rather than its individual components, is limited. Not all evidence supporting the individual components of this framework is consistent; the association between CSA and rotator cuff tears has not been replicated in all cohorts,(18) and other investigators have questioned the role of acromial morphology in cuff disease more broadly. The role of acromial morphology in cuff pathology remains an area of active debate, and the possibility that some morphological associations reflect confounding by shared genetic or developmental factors rather than direct mechanical causation cannot be excluded.

Despite these limitations, the convergence of evidence from morphological, kinematic, arthroplasty, and cellular biology literature on the scapula as a common upstream factor is difficult to dismiss. The predictions generated by this framework are specific and testable, and their investigation has the potential to improve clinical decision-making across the spectrum of shoulder pathology.

## Conclusion

The scapulothoracic articulation is a compression element suspended in a continuous tension network. Its static morphology influences force distribution to the rotator cuff and the glenohumeral joint. Its dynamic positioning influences effective glenoid orientation and subacromial space geometry. When we surgically alter the glenohumeral joint, the demands on this tension-dependent system increase. The specific downstream pathology that develops in a given patient, whether cuff disease, posterior instability, glenoid wear, or suboptimal arthroplasty outcome, depends on which downstream structure bears the consequence of the particular alteration in scapulothoracic mechanics.

Whether this represents a single biomechanical reality producing diverse clinical manifestations, or whether the apparent convergence on the scapula reflects coincidence or confounding, cannot be determined from the existing literature alone. What can be said is that the evidence presented here, drawn from morphological, kinematic, arthroplasty, and cellular biology investigations conducted independently by different research groups, points consistently toward the scapulothoracic articulation as a common and underappreciated variable in shoulder pathology. The questions this framework raises are specific enough to be tested and clinically relevant enough to warrant the effort. We hope this paper contributes to a broader conversation about the scapula's role in shoulder disease and stimulates the kind of investigation that will determine whether the framework holds up or requires revision.
