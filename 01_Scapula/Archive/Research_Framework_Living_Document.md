## 1. The Central Thesis

The scapulothoracic articulation is the biomechanical bottleneck of the shoulder — the point where evolutionary trade-offs, morphological variation, and surgical intervention converge. Multiple independent lines of evidence — from morphology, kinematics, arthroplasty outcomes, and cellular biology — all point to the scapula as a primary, underappreciated driver of shoulder pathology.

This document organizes these lines of evidence and identifies the research questions they generate.

## 2. The Scapula as a Tensegrity System

### 2.1 What Tensegrity Is

Tensegrity (tensional integrity) describes a structural system where compression elements (struts) float within a continuous tension network (cables), and stability emerges from the pre-stressed tension balance rather than from compressive stacking (Fuller 1961; Snelson sculptures).

At the cellular level, this is established science: the cytoskeleton uses microtubules as compression struts and actin filaments as tension cables, with pre-stress governing cell stiffness and mechanical responsiveness (Ingber 1997, Annu Rev Physiol, 1,403 citations; Wang et al. 2001, PNAS, 634 citations; Ingber 2003, J Cell Science, 1,147 citations; Ingber 2014, Rep Prog Phys, 409 citations). [ESTABLISHED]

At the musculoskeletal level, Chen & Ingber (1999, Osteoarthritis & Cartilage, 186 citations) explicitly argued that the same tensegrity principles apply from skeleton to cytoskeleton, and that this architecture minimizes metabolic cost while maximizing multi-directional stability. [SUPPORTED — the framework is published in peer-reviewed orthopedic literature, but quantitative validation at the musculoskeletal scale is incomplete]

### 2.2 Why the Scapulothoracic Articulation is a Tensegrity Structure

The scapula is arguably the most self-evident tensegrity structure in the musculoskeletal system:

- **No bony articulation** to the axial skeleton — the compression element (scapula) floats within the tension network [ESTABLISHED — basic anatomy]
- **~15+ tension elements** (muscles) control one compression element — heavily tension-dominant, consistent with tensegrity cable:strut ratios [ESTABLISHED]
- **Triangulated force couples** (upper trap + lower trap + serratus anterior) provide multi-planar stability — consistent with tensegrity triangulation principles [ESTABLISHED — Bagg & Forrest 1986, Kibler 1998]
- **Pre-stress dependent function** — reducing muscle tone reduces stiffness and function (Kebaetse 1999: slouching → 23.6° less abduction, 16.2% less force; Ludewig 2000: decreased serratus activity → altered kinematics) [ESTABLISHED]
- **Global response to local perturbation** — pec minor shortening alters kinematics in three rotational planes simultaneously (Borstad 2005); SICK scapula produces symptoms at coracoid, levator scapulae, AC joint, and even subclavian space (Burkhart 2003) [ESTABLISHED]
- **Self-stabilization at new equilibrium** — scapular dyskinesis persists after cuff repair despite improved ROM and strength (Mercurio 2026); the system settles into a new, dysfunctional but stable configuration [SUPPORTED]
- **Global restoration from local intervention** — scapular rehabilitation resolved symptoms at multiple anatomic sites; 100% of 96 athletes returned to preinjury performance, directly proportional to scapular repositioning (Burkhart 2003) [SUPPORTED — single-author case series, no control group]

### 2.3 What Tensegrity Predicts and What It Cannot

**Qualitative predictions that match clinical observations:**
1. Reducing pre-stress in any tension element → reduced global stiffness → scapular malposition ✓
2. Local perturbation → global reorganization across the entire scapulothoracic system ✓
3. The system self-stabilizes around a new equilibrium after perturbation ✓
4. Restoring pre-stress at deficient elements → global functional improvement ✓

**What tensegrity cannot currently do at the musculoskeletal level:**
- Quantitatively predict force magnitudes (how much serratus weakness → how much scapular shift)
- Distinguish itself from alternative mechanical models that might produce similar qualitative predictions
- Model the active neural control that continuously tunes muscle tension in living systems

**Open question:** Can computational tensegrity modeling of the scapulothoracic system produce quantitative predictions that match measured clinical data? (See Research Questions #22-25)

## 4. Dynamic Glenoid Version — A Novel Hypothesis

### 4.1 The Concept

Glenoid version is measured statically on CT. But the scapula's position on the thorax — which changes dynamically during movement — alters the effective orientation of the glenoid in space. Bryce et al. (2010, JBJS, 147 citations) showed that scapular rotation changes measured glenoid version by up to 22.5°. They framed this as a CT measurement artifact.

**Reframe:** If scapular position dynamically changes effective glenoid version during functional movement, then scapular dyskinesis doesn't just alter kinematics — it functionally alters the orientation of the socket in real time. A scapula that chronically sits in more internal rotation presents a functionally more retroverted glenoid during loading. [PLAUSIBLE — geometry proven by Bryce, clinical implications untested]

### 4.2 Clinical Implications

- Chronic functional retroversion → asymmetric posterior glenoid loading → B0 → B1 → B2 progression (Domos 2017) [THEORETICAL]
- After glenoid component implantation, scapulothoracic malposition could create dynamic asymmetric loading on the prosthesis [THEORETICAL]
- Akgun 2024: C1 patients (≈B0) have different scapulothoracic orientation vs. controls — consistent with but not proving the mechanism [SUPPORTED — cross-sectional, causation unresolved]
- Moroder 2020 (48 cites): scapulothoracic orientation influences optimal humeral component retrotorsion in rTSA — already being incorporated into planning [SUPPORTED]

### 4.3 Novelty Assessment

Scopus search (March 2026, TITLE-ABS-KEY): "dynamic glenoid version" returns **0 results**. The reframe from measurement artifact to functional biomechanical variable appears genuinely novel. Key adjacent work: Bryce 2010, Akgun 2024, Moroder 2020.

## 5. The rTSA Paradox

### 5.1 The Problem

rTSA converts the shoulder from a tension-dominant stability system (rotator cuff providing dynamic, tensegrity-like stabilization) to a geometry-dominant constraint system (prosthetic ball-and-socket providing mechanical stability). But this conversion INCREASES the tension demands on the scapulothoracic articulation. [ESTABLISHED]

| Study | Finding | Level |
|-------|---------|-------|
| Walker 2015 (JSES, 77 cites) | SHR in rTSA: 1.3:1 vs. normal 3:1. Scapula does ~half the work. | ESTABLISHED |
| Kawashima 2025 (JSES) | SHR ≥2 → ASES 94.3 vs. SHR <2 → 82.1 (p=.007). Better ST function → better outcomes. | SUPPORTED (n=20) |
| Simovitch 2007 (JBJS) | 44% inferior scapular notching rate. Adverse clinical outcomes. | ESTABLISHED |
| Levin 2024 (JBJS) | 486 rTSA configurations modeled. Neck-shaft angle most important for muscle-tendon length. Deltoid originates from scapula. | ESTABLISHED (computational) |
| Shekhbihi 2025 (EFORT) | "The scapulothoracic conundrum in rTSA" — frames this as an open problem. | N/A (review) |
| Moroder 2020, 2022 | Scapulothoracic orientation influences optimal component positioning. Proposes posture types A-C. | SUPPORTED |
| Bauer 2023 | Scapular posture identified as unaddressed variable in rTSA planning software. | N/A (review) |

### 5.2 The "Scapula-Sparing" Concept

If rTSA increases scapulothoracic demands, and pre-operative scapulothoracic function varies between patients, then implant selection should account for the patient's "scapulothoracic reserve." Patients with poor reserve may benefit from designs that demand less scapulothoracic compensation. [THEORETICAL — nobody has proposed this framework formally]

## 6. Persistent Dyskinesis and Sarcomere Remodeling

### 6.1 The Evidence

| Study | Finding | Level |
|-------|---------|-------|
| Mercurio 2026 (JSES Int) | Dyskinesis persists after cuff repair — SHR unchanged despite improved ROM/Constant scores (p>.05) | SUPPORTED (n=21, prospective) |
| Gibbons 2016 (J Orthop Res) | Massive cuff tears: sarcomere loss, fiber shortening, sarcomere length below optimal. May be irreversible. | ESTABLISHED (cadaveric) |
| Burkhart 2003 (Arthroscopy) | 96 athletes, rehab only, 100% returned. Resolution proportional to scapular repositioning. | SUPPORTED (single-author, no control) |
| Camargo 2015 (JOSPT, 106 cites) | RCT: scapular-focused exercise improved kinematics, function, pain in impingement | ESTABLISHED (RCT) |

### 6.2 The Proposed Mechanism

When the scapula sits at an abnormal position chronically:
1. Periscapular muscles at altered resting lengths → sarcomere adaptation (addition/subtraction in series) [ESTABLISHED at the cellular level — Williams & Goldspink; ESTABLISHED in cuff — Gibbons 2016; PLAUSIBLE for periscapular muscles — never directly measured]
2. Length-tension curve shifts → muscles generate optimal force at the WRONG position [ESTABLISHED — basic physiology]
3. Chronically unloaded muscle fibers lose mechanotransduction input → fatty infiltration (Goutallier) [ESTABLISHED for observation; PLAUSIBLE for tensegrity-mechanotransduction mechanism — Ingber]
4. The new equilibrium is structurally committed — not just a motor control problem [PLAUSIBLE — consistent with Mercurio 2026 persistence finding]

### 6.3 Implication: Prehab Before Arthroplasty

If sarcomere remodeling locks in scapular dysfunction, then rehabilitation must start BEFORE surgery (when remodeling is still possible), not after (when new biomechanical demands are already imposed). The typical rTSA patient has had years of dysfunction → years of sarcomere adaptation → structurally compromised scapular stabilizers → then we impose rTSA demands that require MORE from those compromised muscles. [THEORETICAL — compelling logic, completely untested]

## 7. The Evolutionary Context

The shoulder evolved under specific selection pressures (Kuhn 2016, Am J Orthopedics; Selby 2017, Am J Phys Anthropol; Roach 2013, Nature):

- Arboreal locomotion → mobile, adaptable scapulothoracic articulation [ESTABLISHED]
- Throwing → extreme external rotation, elastic energy storage, depressed greater tuberosity, coracoacromial ligament unique to hominids [ESTABLISHED]
- The scapula migrated from lateral thorax (quadrupeds) to posterior thorax (bipeds), losing bony stability [ESTABLISHED]
- The GH cartilage is optimized for load distribution at ~90° abduction (throwing position), not rest [ESTABLISHED — Kuhn 2016]

**The evolutionary mismatch hypothesis:** The shoulder was designed for intermittent, high-performance use in a healthy kinetic chain. Modern humans impose sustained, repetitive loading in a sedentary, kyphotic body — use parameters the system was not selected for. [PLAUSIBLE — logical, consistent with epidemiological data, not directly testable as stated]

**The metabolic efficiency argument for tensegrity:** Evolution selects for metabolic efficiency. Tensegrity structures minimize mass for given structural requirements (Chen & Ingber 1999). The scapulothoracic articulation's pure muscular suspension may represent the metabolically optimal solution to the engineering problem of multi-directional, variable-load stability. [PLAUSIBLE — compelling logic, but evolution optimizes for reproductive fitness not mechanical efficiency per se]

## 10. Beyond the Shoulder: Tensegrity Predictions at the Elbow

### 10.1 The Lateral Epicondyle as a Tensegrity Vulnerability

If the upper extremity functions as a hierarchical tensegrity system, then specific anatomical nodes should be predictably vulnerable based on tensegrity principles. The lateral epicondyle is a compelling test case.

**High-concentration tension node.** The lateral epicondyle is the convergence point for 5-6 tension elements (ECRB, ECRL, EDC, EDM, ECU, supinator) attaching to a single small area of a compression element (distal humerus). In any tensegrity structure, mechanical stress at a node is proportional to the number of tension elements converging there and the magnitude of force they carry. The lateral epicondyle is one of the highest-concentration tension nodes in the upper extremity. [ESTABLISHED — basic anatomy; PLAUSIBLE — tensegrity interpretation]

**Force redirection point.** Wrist extensor tension generated in the forearm must be transmitted to the humerus. The lateral epicondyle is where that force vector changes direction. Points where force changes direction experience stress concentration — this is true in any structural system, not unique to tensegrity. [ESTABLISHED]

**Subsystem interface.** The elbow sits at the junction between the forearm tensegrity subsystem and the upper arm tensegrity subsystem. In hierarchical tensegrity, connections between subsystems are the most mechanically demanding nodes. [PLAUSIBLE]

### 10.2 The ECRB "Dual Loading Violation"

In a tensegrity system, cables should bear tension only — never compression. The ECRB has a unique anatomical vulnerability: its deep surface contacts and slides against the lateral edge of the capitellum during forearm pronation and wrist extension. The ECRB is simultaneously loaded in tension (as a wrist extensor) AND subjected to local compression (against the capitellum).

This "dual loading violation" means the ECRB is operating outside its tensegrity design parameters — it's a cable being used as both a cable and a load-bearing surface. This may explain why the ECRB is specifically and preferentially affected in lateral epicondylitis, rather than the other extensor tendons at the same node that experience tension without compressive contact. [PLAUSIBLE — the anatomy is established (Nirschl), the tensegrity interpretation is novel]

Scarr 2012 (Int J Osteopathic Med, 48 citations) proposed that tensegrity has "relevance to understanding the commonly encountered but vague pathologies such as 'tennis elbow' and 'repetitive strain injury'" but did not elaborate the specific mechanisms described here.

### 10.3 Kinetic Chain Implications

If scapulothoracic dysfunction reduces the efficiency of force transmission through the upper extremity kinetic chain (supported by Kibler 1998, Kebaetse 1999), then downstream nodes must compensate by absorbing more force. The lateral epicondyle — as the highest-concentration tension node at the elbow — would be the predicted first site of failure.

**Testable predictions:**
- Patients with lateral epicondylitis should have a higher prevalence of scapular dysfunction than matched controls [THEORETICAL — testable, not published]
- Treatment addressing upstream kinetic chain dysfunction should reduce epicondylitis recurrence compared to local treatment alone [THEORETICAL — testable]
- Desk workers who develop epicondylitis despite low "overuse" loads may have postural/scapulothoracic dysfunction as the upstream driver [THEORETICAL — explains a clinical observation that pure overuse models cannot]

### 10.4 The Specific Mechanical Pathway: Scapular Protraction → ECRB Dual Loading

A traceable mechanical chain connects scapulothoracic dysfunction to increased ECRB loading:

**Step 1: Scapular protraction alters glenoid orientation.** When the scapula protracts (internally rotates on the thorax), the glenoid faces more anteriorly. To maintain hand position during a constrained task (keyboard, tool, racket), the humerus must internally rotate more to compensate. [ESTABLISHED — Bryce 2010 quantified glenoid version changes from scapular rotation; Ludewig 2000 documented altered scapular position in symptomatic shoulders]

**Step 2: Increased humeral internal rotation increases forearm pronation.** For tasks requiring a fixed hand orientation, compensatory humeral internal rotation shifts the forearm into more pronation. [ESTABLISHED — basic upper extremity kinematics]

**Step 3: Forearm pronation increases ECRB-capitellum contact pressure.** Tanaka et al. (2011, J Hand Surgery, 22 citations) directly measured contact pressure between the extensor origin and the lateral capitellum and showed it varies with forearm position. The radius rotates over the ulna during pronation, shifting the extensor muscle mass and pressing the ECRB origin against the capitellar edge. [SUPPORTED — directly measured]

**Step 4: Chronic dual loading drives tendinopathic changes.** The ECRB now chronically experiences simultaneous tension (wrist extension for task performance) and compression (against the capitellum in the pronated position). Through cellular mechanotransduction (Ingber 1997, 2003, 2014), sustained abnormal mechanical signals alter gene expression in tenocytes. Over months, collagen turnover disrupts, angiofibroblastic changes develop (Nirschl's pathology), and the tissue degenerates. Sasaki et al. (2012, J Hand Surgery, 17 citations) found cartilage damage on the capitellum in tennis elbow patients, confirming that contact forces are large enough to damage tissue on both sides of the interface. [PLAUSIBLE — each component supported, the complete integrated mechanism is untested]

**The desk worker implication:** A sedentary worker with chronic scapular protraction (from forward posture, thoracic kyphosis, serratus weakness) sits with functionally increased forearm pronation for 8+ hours daily. The ECRB is dual-loaded at low intensity but high duration. This provides a specific mechanistic explanation for why desk workers develop lateral epicondylitis despite low peak loads — it is not the MAGNITUDE of force but the DURATION of abnormal loading pattern that exceeds the tissue's mechanotransduction tolerance. [THEORETICAL — explains a clinical observation that conventional overuse models cannot fully account for]

**Additional scapular changes that could compound the effect:**

- **Loss of posterior tilt:** Anteriorly tipped acromion → subacromial symptoms → compensatory avoidance of overhead positions → more forearm-dependent task strategies → more pronation and wrist extension → more ECRB loading [THEORETICAL]
- **Serratus anterior weakness:** Creates a scapula that is passively protracted (gravity + pec minor) but can't upwardly rotate → protraction effect (pronation) PLUS inadequate overhead reach → compensatory distal muscle overuse [PLAUSIBLE — consistent with clinical patterns]
- **Reduced scapular stability as force platform:** If the scapula provides a less stable base for arm function (reduced pre-stress in the scapulothoracic tensegrity), distal muscles including the wrist extensors must generate more force to achieve the same task performance → increased ECRB tension component of the dual load [PLAUSIBLE — consistent with kinetic chain principles, Kibler 1998]

**Honest caveats:**
- The magnitude at each step may be small. A few degrees of scapular protraction → a few degrees of humeral IR → a few degrees of forearm pronation. Whether this produces a clinically meaningful change in ECRB-capitellum contact pressure depends on the slope of Tanaka's pressure-position curve. If the relationship is steep, small positional changes matter. If flat, the mechanism is quantitatively insignificant.
- Even if epicondylitis patients have more scapular protraction, both could be parallel consequences of sedentary posture rather than causally linked.
- The mechanism is most compelling for tasks with fixed hand requirements (keyboard, tool use, racket sports). For unconstrained tasks, the arm can orient differently without changing forearm pronation.

## 11. The Rotator Cable-Crescent as a Tensegrity Subsystem

### 11.1 The Anatomy

Burkhart (1993, Arthroscopy, 384 citations) described the rotator cable as a thick bundle of fibers running perpendicular to the main cuff tendon fibers, spanning from the anterior supraspinatus insertion to the posterior infraspinatus insertion, forming an arc along the greater tuberosity. The rotator crescent is the thinner, relatively avascular tissue between the cable and the bony insertion — overlapping with Codman's "critical zone." Burkhart framed this as a "suspension bridge" — the cable distributes load across its arc, and even when the crescent tears, the cable can still transmit force to the tuberosity. [ESTABLISHED — Burkhart 1993, 384 citations; Burkhart 1992, 274 citations]

Denard et al. (2012, Orthopedics, 79 citations) clinically validated that cable integrity — not tear size — determines whether massive tears cause pseudoparalysis. Patients with massive tears but intact cables maintain function. Patients with cable disruption lose function regardless of tear configuration. [SUPPORTED]

### 11.2 The Tensegrity Interpretation

The rotator cuff is not a homogeneous sheet — it is a structured tension network with elements serving different mechanical functions. The tensegrity framework offers specific explanations for the cable-crescent architecture:

**The cable as a tensegrity cross-link.** In tensegrity, cross-links are tension elements that connect other tension elements to each other, ensuring force applied to one part of the network is shared across the whole system. The rotator cable's fibers run perpendicular to the main tendon fibers — it doesn't pull the humerus toward the glenoid. It redistributes tension WITHIN the cuff. Cross-links are what make tensegrity networks fault-tolerant: local failure (crescent tear) is compensated by the remaining elements because the cross-link (cable) redistributes load. This is precisely what Burkhart observed clinically. [NOVEL interpretation — nobody has framed the cable as a tensegrity cross-link]

**The crescent as an expendable pre-stressed membrane.** The crescent is the thinnest part of the cuff — minimum material, maximum reliance on tension. In tensegrity design, this is optimal: use minimum material where a cross-link (cable) can redistribute load if the membrane fails. Evolution made the crescent thin because it COULD, given the cable's load-redistribution capacity. But this also makes the crescent the most vulnerable element to degradation — thin, avascular, in constant tension. [PLAUSIBLE — consistent with anatomy, novel tensegrity interpretation]

**Tear propagation as tensegrity failure cascade.** When one element fails in a tensegrity network, force redistributes to adjacent elements. If the cable is intact, it absorbs the redistributed load → tear stabilizes. If the cable is attenuated or degenerated, it can't redistribute → force concentrates on adjacent elements → sequential failure → tear propagates. This explains the clinical spectrum:
- Intact cable + crescent tear → functional, stable, often asymptomatic (Burkhart)
- Attenuated cable + crescent tear → progressive tear, worsening function
- Cable failure → catastrophic loss of force distribution → rapid propagation, pseudoparalysis (Denard 2012)

[PLAUSIBLE — maps clinical observations to tensegrity mechanics]

### 11.3 Integration with Scapular Morphology

The rotator cuff (with its cable-crescent architecture) is a nested tensegrity subsystem within the larger scapulothoracic tensegrity. Its function depends on:

1. **Its own internal architecture** — cable integrity, crescent health, tendon quality
2. **The platform it operates from** — scapular position determines cuff muscle resting lengths and force vector directions
3. **The loading it receives** — determined by CSA, acromial morphology, and activity demands

**Gerber 2014 (J Orthop Res):** Directly showed that supraspinatus tendon load during abduction is dependent on the size of the critical shoulder angle. This is the mechanistic link: CSA (scapular morphology) → supraspinatus load → cable-crescent stress distribution → tear risk.

**The complete chain:** CSA determines HOW MUCH load the cuff bears (Gerber 2014, Viehöfer 2016) → the cable-crescent architecture determines HOW the cuff distributes that load internally (Burkhart 1993) → when the load exceeds the cable's capacity to redistribute, the crescent tears and propagates (Burkhart 1992, Denard 2012). The scapula is upstream of the cuff at every level.

### 11.4 Dissolving the Extrinsic-Intrinsic Dichotomy

The current consensus holds that rotator cuff disease results from a combination of extrinsic factors (mechanical compression, altered kinematics) and intrinsic factors (tendon degeneration, vascular insufficiency, aging). The tensegrity framework connects these through hierarchical force transmission:

**Extrinsic → Intrinsic pathway:** Altered scapular kinematics (extrinsic) → altered force distribution through the cuff → altered mechanotransduction signals at the cellular level (Ingber 1997, 2003) → altered gene expression in tenocytes → collagen disorganization, vascular changes, tissue degeneration (intrinsic). The extrinsic factor produces the intrinsic change through the tensegrity-mechanotransduction pathway. [PLAUSIBLE — each link supported individually, the integrated chain is hypothetical]

**Intrinsic → Extrinsic pathway:** Intrinsic tendon degeneration → reduced cable stiffness (cable becomes less effective as cross-link) → altered force distribution across the cuff → changed scapulothoracic demands → compensatory scapular dyskinesis. The intrinsic change drives extrinsic adaptation. [PLAUSIBLE]

**Why some people tear and others don't — despite identical age and activity:** Two patients with the same age, activity level, and even the same CSA may have different cuff outcomes because their scapulothoracic function differs. Patient A with good scapulothoracic function → optimal cuff loading angles → cable loaded within its design parameters → crescent intact. Patient B with scapular dyskinesis → suboptimal cuff loading angles + 23% more shear (Viehöfer) → cable overloaded → crescent fails → tear. The scapulothoracic state is the variable that determines whether the intrinsic vulnerability (thin crescent, critical zone vascularity) becomes a clinical tear. [THEORETICAL — but generates the specific testable prediction below]

### 11.5 Testable Predictions

1. **Cable integrity should correlate with scapulothoracic function.** Patients with poor scapulothoracic kinematics should have more cable attenuation on MRI. [Testable — MRI cable assessment + kinematic analysis]

2. **CSA should predict cable degeneration specifically.** High CSA changes shear-to-compression ratio, which would alter cable loading specifically. [Testable — CSA + MRI cable morphology]

3. **Optimizing scapulothoracic function should improve compensation for cuff tears.** If scapulothoracic pre-stress determines how well the cable redistributes load, scapular rehab should improve function even without surgical cuff repair. [Testable — scapular rehab for symptomatic partial or small full-thickness tears]

4. **Cable integrity should predict retear risk independently of tear size and repair quality.** If the cable is the critical cross-link, its status at time of repair should predict whether the repaired crescent will re-tear. [Testable — prospective study: intraoperative cable assessment + retear follow-up]

### 11.6 Pseudoparalysis as Tensegrity Collapse

Pseudoparalysis — the inability to actively elevate the arm above 90° despite intact neurological function — is one of the most clinically puzzling phenomena in shoulder surgery. Tear size alone does not predict it.

**The clinical evidence:**
- Denard et al. (2012, Orthopedics, 79 citations): Cable integrity determines pseudoparalysis, not tear size. Patients with massive tears but intact cables maintain elevation. Cable disruption → pseudoparalysis. [SUPPORTED]
- Ernstbrunner et al. (2020, AJSM, 18 citations — Gerber group): Scapular morphologic characteristics AND rotator cuff tear pattern are INDEPENDENTLY associated with chronic pseudoparalysis. Scapular morphology is an independent predictor alongside the cuff tear. [SUPPORTED]
- Ernstbrunner et al. (2021, AJSM, 50 citations): Distinguishes pseudoparalysis (complete loss) from pseudoparesis (weakness without complete loss) — structural and biomechanical analysis. [SUPPORTED]

**The tensegrity interpretation:**

Tensegrity systems exhibit a well-known property: they maintain shape and function across a wide range of loads, but when pre-stress drops below a critical threshold, the system doesn't degrade gradually — it loses structural coherence entirely. This is binary collapse, not linear degradation. [ESTABLISHED — engineering tensegrity property]

Pseudoparalysis exhibits exactly this pattern. Patients don't gradually lose 10°, then 20°, then 30° of elevation. They lose the ability to elevate altogether — a binary transition from functional to non-functional. [ESTABLISHED — clinical observation]

**The mechanical explanation:** The GH force couple (cuff + deltoid) is a local tensegrity system where the cuff provides centripetal tension (keeping the head centered) while the deltoid generates elevation force. Their balance maintains head centering during elevation.

- Cable intact after tear → cable redistributes remaining cuff tension → pre-stress stays above critical threshold → head stays centered → functional (pseudoparesis at most) [PLAUSIBLE]
- Cable fails → no cross-link to redistribute force → pre-stress drops below threshold → deltoid pulls the humeral head superiorly instead of rotating it → pseudoparalysis. The motor (deltoid) is fine — the structural system directing its output has collapsed. [PLAUSIBLE]

**The scapulothoracic modulation:** The critical pre-stress threshold is not fixed — it depends on the upstream tensegrity state:

1. **Glenoid orientation:** Optimal scapular position directs more deltoid force into rotational elevation and less into superior shear → LOWERS the threshold [PLAUSIBLE]
2. **Cuff muscle length-tension:** Optimal scapular position places remaining cuff muscles at ideal operating length → INCREASES available pre-stress [PLAUSIBLE]
3. **Deltoid line of action:** Scapular position determines deltoid's force vector direction through the acromial origin [ESTABLISHED — basic anatomy]

**Prediction:** Two patients with identical massive tears and cable disruption could differ in functional status based on scapulothoracic function. Better scapulothoracic function → lower threshold + higher available pre-stress → still above threshold → functional despite cable loss. Poor scapulothoracic function → higher threshold + lower pre-stress → below threshold → pseudoparalysis. [THEORETICAL]

**Clinical implication:** Optimizing scapulothoracic function in patients with massive tears could potentially shift the balance back above the critical threshold — reversing pseudoparalysis without surgical repair. This is actually reported anecdotally: some patients with pseudoparalysis recover active elevation through rehabilitation alone. The tensegrity framework provides the mechanical explanation. [THEORETICAL — consistent with clinical anecdote]

**rTSA connection:** rTSA treats pseudoparalysis by replacing the failed GH tensegrity with a geometric constraint (semiconstrained prosthesis). The deltoid no longer needs a centripetal counterforce. But the scapulothoracic tensegrity — the last remaining tensegrity in the chain — now bears more demand (Walker 2015: SHR 1.3:1). This is why scapulothoracic function predicts rTSA outcomes (Kawashima 2025). [PLAUSIBLE]
