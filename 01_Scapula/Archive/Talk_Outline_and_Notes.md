## Evidence Quality Key
Throughout this document, each claim is tagged with its evidence level:
- **[STRONG]** — Biomechanical measurement data, cadaveric studies, large clinical studies. Hard to dispute.
- **[MODERATE]** — Clinical correlations/associations, systematic reviews, smaller studies with consistent findings.
- **[EMERGING]** — Early evidence, small sample sizes, logical extrapolation from established principles.
- **[THEORETICAL]** — Mechanistically plausible but limited direct empirical support. Acknowledge as such when presenting.

## Slide 2: The Opening Hook (1 min)

**Key Point:** The GH joint gets all the glory, but the scapula makes everything possible.

**Speaker Notes:**
- "How many of you do a focused scapular exam on every shoulder patient?" (Pause — the answer is almost none.)
- 17 muscles attach to the scapula — more than any other bone in the shoulder girdle
- The scapulothoracic "joint" has NO bony articulation — it is stabilized entirely by muscle
- No capsule, no ligaments, no bony constraints
- **Evolutionary context (Kuhn 2016):** Humans are the only species that can throw with speed and accuracy — Darwin himself noted apes "are quite unable...to throw a stone with precision." Throwing affects change at a distance, provided protection and enabled predation — promoted through natural selection. The shoulder evolved FOR throwing, and the scapula is the platform that makes it possible.
- **The evolutionary trade-off:** The same adaptations that gave us throwing ability (depressed greater tuberosity, extreme external rotation capacity) also made us vulnerable to rotator cuff disease (watershed zone, subacromial impingement). Evolution traded long-term tendon health for throwing performance.
- **Selby 2017:** The human scapula evolved from a clambering ancestor (not a brachiator). Two fundamental changes: (1) narrowing of the scapular blade, (2) migration of the glenoid position. The scapula migrated from lateral thorax (quadrupeds) to posterior thorax (bipeds) — losing bony stability and becoming entirely muscle-dependent.
- **The "feet off the ground" moment (Kibler 1998):** When a tennis player is mid-serve with feet off the ground, the scapula IS the only stable base for the entire upper extremity. There is no other foundation. This single image communicates the entire thesis.
- Thesis: The scapula is not a passive platform — it is an active, dynamic regulator of shoulder function, and when it fails, the entire shoulder suffers

**Evidence Level:** [STRONG] — Basic anatomy. Uncontroversial.

**Citations:**
- Kuhn JE. "Throwing, the Shoulder, and Human Evolution." Am J Orthopedics, 2016.
- Selby MS, Lovejoy CO. "Evolution of the hominoid scapula and its implications for earliest hominid locomotion." Am J Phys Anthropol, 2017. DOI: 10.1002/ajpa.23158

## Slides 3-4: Foundational Anatomy — The "Sesamoid Bone" of the Shoulder (2 min)

**Key Point:** The scapula is uniquely vulnerable because it depends entirely on muscular stabilization.

**Speaker Notes:**
- The scapulothoracic articulation: no true joint capsule, no ligamentous attachments between scapula and thorax. Culham 1993 notes the scapula is partly "retained in place by atmospheric pressure" — its stability is that tenuous.
- Muscular stabilization only: serratus anterior, trapezius (upper/middle/lower), rhomboids, levator scapulae, pectoralis minor
- **The force couple concept:** Upward rotation requires coordinated action of upper trap + lower trap + serratus anterior. If any component fails → scapula cannot upwardly rotate properly. **[STRONG]**
  - Bagg & Forrest 1986 defined this force couple
- **The 4 scapular motions:** upward/downward rotation, internal/external rotation, anterior/posterior tilt, protraction/retraction
- **The "sesamoid bone" analogy:** Just as the patella's position is determined by the balance of forces in the extensor mechanism, the scapula's position is determined by the balance of forces in the periscapular musculature. Any imbalance → altered resting position → altered kinematics.
- **Evolutionary depth (Selby 2017):** In quadrupeds, the scapula sits laterally on the thorax and bears compressive load through bony contact. In bipedal humans, it migrated posteriorly — no longer load-bearing, now entirely suspended by muscles. The serratus anterior evolved primarily for stabilization under load (EMG data shows it's most active eccentrically controlling trunk descent during suspension), not just arm-raising. This is why serratus anterior failure is so devastating — it's fighting against the fundamental instability of the evolved scapular position.
- **Key Selby finding:** Only TWO fundamental changes explain virtually ALL hominoid scapular features: (1) reduction in mediolateral breadth, (2) cranial migration of the glenoid. Everything else — spine angle, fossa sizes, inferior angle position — are byproducts. The scapula's form is an integrated system, not a collection of independent parts.

**Evidence Level:** [STRONG] — Established anatomy and biomechanics. Force couple concept well-supported by EMG data.

**Citations:**
- Saha AK. "Dynamic stability of the glenohumeral joint." Acta Orthop Scand, 1971.
- Bagg SD, Forrest WJ. "A biomechanical analysis of scapular rotation during arm abduction in the scapular plane." Am J Phys Med Rehabil, 1986.
- Selby MS, Lovejoy CO. Am J Phys Anthropol, 2017.
- Oxnard CE. "Evolution of the human shoulder: Some possible pathways." Am J Phys Anthropol, 1969.

## Slides 5-6: Normal Scapulohumeral Rhythm (2 min)

**Key Point:** The scapula contributes ~60° of the 180° of arm elevation. Without it, the shoulder is crippled.

**Speaker Notes:**
- **Inman 1944:** Established the classic 2:1 scapulohumeral rhythm (2° GH motion : 1° ST motion). One of the most cited papers in shoulder biomechanics. **[STRONG]**
- **Poppen & Walker 1976:** Refined this — showed the ratio isn't fixed. Early elevation is mostly GH; higher elevation has increasing scapular contribution. **[STRONG]**
- **McClure et al. 2001:** Used **bone pins drilled directly into the scapular spine** in 8 living subjects — the gold standard for eliminating soft tissue artifact. Established that the scapula upwardly rotates (~50°), posteriorly tilts (~30°), and externally rotates (~24°) during full elevation. Mean SHR was **1.7:1** (not 2:1). **[STRONG — invasive, definitive]**
- **Ludewig 2009 JBJS:** Further refined multiplanar motion patterns of the shoulder complex. **[STRONG]**
- **Clinical implication:** If you lose scapulothoracic motion (nerve injury, muscular imbalance), you lose ~1/3 of arm elevation AND the stable platform for force generation. A glenohumeral joint operating without proper scapular motion is like building a house on sand.
- **Honest note:** The 2:1 ratio is an oversimplification — actual SHR varies by plane of elevation, load, speed, and individual anatomy. But the fundamental point stands: the scapula is responsible for a huge proportion of arm motion.

**Evidence Level:** [STRONG] — Measured with 3D electromagnetic tracking, bone pins, and fluoroscopy across multiple labs. Highly reproducible.

## Slides 7-8: The Scapula as Force Platform & Kinetic Chain Link (2 min)

**Key Point:** The scapula is the critical relay station between the trunk and the arm. Force generation for any upper extremity function requires a properly positioned scapula.

**Speaker Notes:**
- **The kinetic chain:** Ground → legs → core/trunk → scapulothoracic joint → glenohumeral joint → arm → hand
- The scapula sits at the critical junction between the axial and appendicular skeleton
- Force generation depends on the scapula being optimally positioned to:
  1. **Provide a stable origin for rotator cuff muscles** — infraspinatus and subscapularis originate from the scapula. If the scapula is malpositioned, these muscles have altered resting lengths and operate at suboptimal points on the length-tension curve. **[STRONG — basic muscle physiology]**
  2. **Maintain the acromion in a position that doesn't impinge the cuff** — posterior tilt and upward rotation clear the subacromial space. **[STRONG]**
  3. **Transmit forces from the trunk** — in throwing, ~50% of ball velocity comes from the trunk and legs. ALL of this energy passes through the scapulothoracic articulation. **[MODERATE — biomechanical modeling data]**
- **Kuhn's key insight:** The glenohumeral joint cartilage distribution is optimized for load distribution at ~90° abduction — the exact position of throwing — NOT the resting position. The joint was literally designed for the throwing position. The scapula must position the glenoid at this precise angle for optimal load distribution.
- **The coracoacromial ligament (Kuhn 2016):** Unique to hominids, not seen in other primates. Positioned to restrain the ~400N anterosuperior shear force during late cocking. It regenerates after resection — the body "wants" it back. This is a scapular structure that evolved specifically for throwing.
- **Kibler's 51% statistic:** In a tennis serve, **51% of total kinetic energy and 54% of total force** come from legs, hips, and trunk. ALL of it is funneled through the scapulothoracic articulation.
- **The "full tank of energy" concept (Kibler 1998):** Scapular retraction during cocking retensions the anterior muscles — storing elastic energy for explosive acceleration. The scapula isn't just a platform; it's an energy reservoir.
- A dyskinetic scapula is an "energy leak" in the kinetic chain — proximal force cannot be efficiently transmitted distally.
- **Kebaetse 1999:** Simply slouching reduces active shoulder abduction by **23.6°** and decreases muscle force by **16.2%** at 90°. Posture alone creates a functional deficit — the scapula is that sensitive to its environment.
- **Borstad 2005:** Normal subacromial space at 90° = **4.1 mm**. In impingement = **1.4 mm**. The margin is measured in MILLIMETERS — even small changes in scapular position matter enormously.
- **Honest caveat:** The "energy leak" concept is mechanistically compelling but direct measurement of energy loss through a dyskinetic scapula in vivo is limited. This is primarily theoretical extrapolation from kinetic chain principles.

**Evidence Level:** [STRONG] for anatomy/muscle physiology; [MODERATE] for kinetic chain quantification; [THEORETICAL] for "energy leak" concept.

## Slides 9-10: When Morphology Goes Wrong — CSA & Posterior Acromial Anatomy (3 min)

**Key Point:** The scapula's SHAPE — not just its motion — directly predicts shoulder pathology. This is objective, radiographic evidence that is difficult to dispute.

### Part A: Critical Shoulder Angle (1.5 min)

**Speaker Notes:**
- **Moor et al. 2013 (BJJ):** Introduced the Critical Shoulder Angle (CSA) — the angle between the glenoid inclination and the most lateral extent of the acromion. **[STRONG — large radiographic study]**
  - CSA >35° → significantly associated with rotator cuff tears
  - CSA <30° → significantly associated with glenohumeral OA
  - This is fundamentally a SCAPULAR measurement — the acromion IS the scapula
- **Viehöfer et al. 2016 (J Orthop Res):** A larger CSA requires MORE rotator cuff activity to maintain joint stability. **[STRONG — biomechanical modeling]**
  - Translation: Unfavorable scapular morphology forces the cuff to work harder → fatigue → degeneration → tear
  - The scapula is the UPSTREAM cause
- **Davide/Blonna 2016 — Dose-response evidence:**
  - CSA 34° in controls, 36° with supraspinatus-only tears, **40° with supraspinatus + infraspinatus tears**
  - Bigger CSA → bigger tears. CSA was the most relevant risk factor (OR 1.7, CI 1.4-2.0), even after controlling for age, gender, smoking, BMI, and occupation.
  - **This is the strongest evidence that CSA is predictive, not just associated.** **[STRONG]**
- **Mantell 2017:** CSA >35° was 90% specific and 52% sensitive for full-thickness RCT in the setting of OA (AUC 0.84). Patients with concurrent OA + RCT had CSA 35° vs. OA alone at 30°. **[MODERATE]**
- **Honest caveats — present these openly:**
  - CSA is a 2D measurement on plain radiographs — subject to positioning variability (though Moor showed robustness to ±20° rotation)
  - Association does not prove causation — we cannot say CSA causes cuff tears
  - **Arnar/Bjarnison 2017:** Found CSA associated with OA (OR 2.25, p=.002) but **NOT** with RCT (OR 1.12, p=.63) in their cohort. Not all studies replicate the cuff tear association. Acknowledging this strengthens your credibility.
  - Some debate about whether CSA changes after cuff tear (cause vs. consequence)
  - However, the biomechanical data (Viehöfer) provides a plausible mechanism, and Davide 2016's dose-response supports it

**Evidence Level:** [STRONG] for the association; [MODERATE] for the causal mechanism.

### Part B: Posterior Acromial Morphology — The Gerber Story (1.5 min)

**Speaker Notes:**
- **Meyer, Ernstbrunner, Gerber et al. 2019 (JBJS Am):** Posterior Acromial Height (PAH) is significantly associated with posterior shoulder instability. **[MODERATE — Level III, retrospective]**
  - n=41 posterior instability vs. 41 anterior instability vs. 53 controls
  - PAH ≥23mm → **OR = 39** for posterior instability (p<0.001)
  - 30.9mm (posterior instability) vs. 19.5mm (anterior instability)
  - A "Swiss chalet roof-type" steep acromion virtually excluded posterior instability
  - **What this means:** A high, horizontal posterior acromion provides LESS osseous restraint against posterior humeral head translation
- **Hochreiter et al. 2023 (J Exp Orthop) — THE BOMBSHELL:** In 3D-printed biomechanical models, correcting the glenoid ALONE was NOT sufficient to restore normal posterior translation. You MUST correct BOTH glenoid AND acromial anatomy. The acromion is an active restraint to posterior translation, not a bystander. **[MODERATE — biomechanical modeling]**
- **Gerber 2025 — The SCOPE Procedure (JSES Int):** 3D-planned Scapular Corrective Osteotomy for Posterior Escape. Uses patient-specific 3D-printed cutting guides. **[EMERGING — Level IV, n=9]**
  - n=9, minimum 2-year follow-up
  - **6 of 9 were REVISION cases** — previous glenoid/soft tissue surgeries had FAILED
  - Median SSV improved 42.5%, Constant Score +18 points
  - 8/9 had improved humeral head centering
  - 1 clinical failure (failed to achieve planned correction intraoperatively)
  - **The paradigm shift:** Traditional approaches focused only on glenoid and soft tissues — and they failed. Gerber concluded that you must reshape the SCAPULA to treat posterior instability.
- **For context on the OR of 39:** Smoking's odds ratio for lung cancer is ~15-30. A posterior acromial height ≥23mm is more strongly associated with posterior instability than smoking is with lung cancer.
- **Honest caveats:**
  - Meyer 2019 is retrospective, relatively small sample
  - Gerber 2025 is a Level IV case series, n=9, preliminary — needs larger studies and longer follow-up
  - The OR of 39 is striking but needs prospective validation
  - One clinical failure reminds us this is still an evolving technique

**Evidence Level:** [MODERATE] for the association (consistent across studies); [EMERGING] for surgical treatment.

## Slides 11-12: When Kinematics Go Wrong — Scapular Dyskinesia (3 min)

**Key Point:** Altered scapular motion patterns are consistently measured in impingement, cuff pathology, and instability populations using objective 3D tracking.

**Speaker Notes:**

**The Biomechanical Evidence (objective, measured, reproducible):**
- **Ludewig & Cook 2000 (JOSPT):** THE landmark paper. Compared 3D scapular kinematics (electromagnetic tracking) in subjects with and without impingement symptoms. Found: **[STRONG]**
  - Decreased upward rotation (scapula doesn't get out of the way)
  - Decreased posterior tilt (acromion tips forward into the cuff)
  - Increased internal rotation
  - Also found increased upper trapezius and decreased serratus anterior activity
- **Lukasiewicz et al. 1999:** Confirmed altered 3D scapular position in impingement. **[STRONG]**
- **McClure et al. 2006:** Largest comparison study (n=45 impingement, 45 matched controls). **[STRONG but nuanced]** Found impingement group had slightly MORE upward rotation and posterior tilt, not less — suggesting these may be **compensatory strategies** for glenohumeral weakness, not primary deficits. Also found decreased ROM and force in all directions. **Honest note:** This finding partially contradicts Ludewig 2000. The discrepancy may reflect different study populations (construction workers vs. mixed), different impingement severity, or genuine variability in how the scapula responds to pathology. Present both sides.
- **Ludewig et al. 2009 (JOSPT):** Review paper linking altered scapular kinematics to multiple GH pathologies. **[STRONG — review of consistent data]**
- **Borstad 2005:** Short pectoralis minor → increased scapular internal rotation and anterior tilt. This is a MODIFIABLE factor — stretching the pec minor can change scapular kinematics. **[STRONG — controlled biomechanical study]**
- **Kebaetse et al. 1999:** Thoracic kyphosis directly affects scapular kinematics — slouched posture → altered scapular position. **[STRONG]**
- **Warner et al. 1992:** Using Moiré topography, showed altered scapulothoracic motion in both unstable AND impingement shoulders compared to normals. **[MODERATE — older methodology but consistent with later 3D studies]**

**The Downstream Pathology Cascade:**
1. Decreased posterior tilt + decreased upward rotation → narrowed subacromial space → external impingement → cuff degeneration **[STRONG — biomechanical mechanism well-supported]**
2. Altered scapular position → altered GH contact mechanics → internal impingement → articular-side cuff tears, SLAP lesions **[MODERATE — logical chain but fewer direct measurements linking each step]**
3. Altered scapular position → reduced dynamic stability → functional instability **[MODERATE]**

**Honest Note:** The biomechanical data is strong and reproducible — multiple labs, multiple methodologies, consistent findings. The leap from "altered kinematics exist" to "altered kinematics cause pathology" is supported but not proven by RCT. This is where we need to be careful about language: these are associations and plausible mechanisms, not proven causal chains.

**Evidence Level:** [STRONG] for the existence of altered kinematics; [MODERATE] for the causal link to specific pathologies.

**The Clinical Framework:**
- **Kibler 2002:** Original 4-type classification of scapular dyskinesis
- **Kibler 2003 (JAAOS):** Scapular dyskinesis and shoulder pain — clinical review
- **Burkhart, Morgan & Kibler 2003 Part III:** The SICK scapula

  - **I**nferior medial border prominence
  - **C**oracoid pain and malposition
  - dys**K**inesis of scapular movement
- **Kibler 2013 (BMJ):** Consensus statement — shifted to simplified yes/no system acknowledging reliability limitations

From Elhassan's group at Harvard/MGH. Worth noting as a sign that the field is actively refining its language and framework.

## Slides 13-14: Leaning Into the Controversy (2-3 min)

**Key Point:** "The problem isn't that scapular dyskinesia doesn't matter — it's that we're bad at detecting it clinically."

**Speaker Notes:**

**Present the criticisms honestly — your audience will respect you more for it:**

1. **High prevalence in asymptomatic populations:**
   - Dyskinesis has been found in asymptomatic overhead AND non-overhead athletes at significant rates
   - Paksoy 2025: Common among asymptomatic professional basketball players
   - If it's present in healthy shoulders, how can it be pathologic?

2. **Poor reliability of clinical assessment:**
   - **Vila-Dieguez et al. 2026 (Shoulder & Elbow):** This is the strongest criticism. **[STRONG study design]**
     - n=104 physical therapists assessed standardized videos of HEALTHY individuals
     - Fleiss' Kappa = **0.12** — very poor inter-rater reliability
     - Clinicians who rated scapular dyskinesis as "more important" were 42% more likely to classify it as present (OR=1.42, p=.0015) — **confirmation bias**
     - **Important nuance the critics miss:** Clinicians seeing MORE shoulder patients/week were LESS likely to identify dyskinesis (OR=0.77, p=.021). This isn't just bias — it's an expertise gradient. The most experienced clinicians are more discerning, not less. This actually argues for MORE specialized attention, not abandonment of the concept.
   - The YES/NO simplification (Kibler 2013 consensus) was a response to the reliability problem — but reliability remains poor

3. **The "so what?" challenge:**
   - Even if dyskinesis exists, does correcting it improve outcomes? Limited RCT evidence for scapular-focused treatment.

**Your counter-arguments (strong but honest):**

1. **"The biomechanics are measured, not observed."**
   - The criticism targets clinical observation reliability. The underlying biomechanical data was measured with 3D electromagnetic tracking, bone pins, and fluoroscopy (Ludewig, McClure, and others). The PHENOMENON is real and reproducible. The clinical DETECTION tool is what's unreliable.
   - *Analogy:* A faulty thermometer doesn't mean fever doesn't exist.

2. **"Asymptomatic dyskinesia may be pre-symptomatic."**
   - High prevalence in asymptomatic athletes doesn't prove dyskinesia is benign — it may represent a population at risk
   - We don't dismiss elevated cholesterol just because many asymptomatic people have it
   - **Honest caveat:** This is a plausible argument but we don't have longitudinal data proving asymptomatic dyskinesis progresses to symptomatic pathology. This is a gap in the evidence. **[THEORETICAL]**

3. **"Observer bias confirms the need for better tools, not abandonment of the concept."**
   - Vila-Dieguez 2026 is actually an argument FOR more attention to the scapula — we need objective measurement tools (wearable sensors, machine learning-based kinematic analysis), not less attention
   - **The field needs to evolve from subjective visual assessment to objective measurement**

4. **"The morphological evidence sidesteps the debate entirely."**
   - CSA and posterior acromial morphology are radiographic measurements — objective, reproducible, not subject to observer bias
   - These have clear associations with pathology. The scapula matters whether or not we can reliably detect dyskinesia on clinical exam. **[STRONG]**

**What we DON'T know (acknowledge openly):**
- We lack longitudinal data showing dyskinesis → progression to pathology
- We lack high-quality RCTs showing that correcting dyskinesis prevents or treats shoulder pathology
- The optimal clinical assessment tool has not been developed
- We don't know the threshold of "how much" dyskinesis matters

**Evidence Level:** [STRONG] for the biomechanical foundation; [MODERATE] for the clinical relevance; [GAPS] in longitudinal and treatment outcome data.

## Slides 15-16: The Throwing Shoulder — A Clinical Case Study (2 min)

**Key Point:** The disabled throwing shoulder is the best clinical example of how scapular dysfunction drives a cascade of pathology.

**Speaker Notes:**

**The Burkhart-Kibler-Morgan Cascade:** **[MODERATE — clinical observation supported by biomechanical reasoning]**
1. Repetitive overhead throwing → posterior capsular contracture → GIRD (Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit)
2. GIRD → posterosuperior shift of GH contact point during late cocking
3. + Scapular dyskinesia (SICK scapula) from repetitive overuse → altered scapular position
4. → Internal impingement → articular-side cuff tears + posterior-superior SLAP lesions
5. The scapula is BOTH a contributor (altered positioning exacerbates the problem) AND a victim (adaptive changes from repeated overuse alter scapular muscle balance)

**Supporting Data:**
- **Wilk et al. 2011:** In professional baseball pitchers, GIRD >20° and total rotational motion deficit correlated with increased shoulder injury. **[MODERATE — Level II, correlation study]**

**Evolutionary Note (Kuhn 2016):** Humans evolved to throw — it gave us a decisive survival advantage. But the depressed greater tuberosity that enables extreme external rotation also creates the watershed zone that predisposes to rotator cuff disease. The subscapularis and long head of biceps act as springs + cables + pulleys storing elastic energy at max external rotation. Our shoulders evolved for intermittent, survival-critical throwing — not 100+ pitches per game. The SICK scapula is what happens when an evolutionary design is pushed beyond its intended use case.

**Burkhart's Treatment Data (from the paper):**
- n=96 overhead athletes treated for SICK scapula with rehab ONLY — no surgery
- **100% returned to preinjury performance level by 4 months**
- Resolution of symptoms was "directly proportional to the ability to reposition the scapula" — a dose-response relationship
- 19 asymptomatic professional pitchers: ALL had SICK scapula scores of 0 — this is NOT adaptive asymmetry, it's pathology
- Most common presenting complaint: anterior coracoid pain (~80%) — easily confused with anterior instability
- **The scapular retraction test:** Manually repositioning the scapula resolved coracoid pain and restored full forward flexion — essentially diagnostic

**The Clinical Pearl:**
- Treating the labrum (SLAP repair) or cuff without addressing the scapular component → high failure rate
- The SICK scapula is often the root cause — surgery fixes the downstream damage but not the upstream driver
- The 100% return rate with rehab alone argues that when you correctly identify the scapula as the problem, the treatment works
- **Honest caveat:** "Root cause" is difficult to prove definitively. The cascade is likely multifactorial, and the relative contribution of scapular dyskinesia vs. GIRD vs. capsular changes vs. overuse alone is debated. The 100% return rate is from a single author's case series without a control group. **[MODERATE]**

**Evidence Level:** [MODERATE] for individual components; [THEORETICAL] for the complete cascade as a causal chain.

## Slides 17-18: The Frontier — Reverse TSA and Scapulothoracic Implications (2 min)

**Key Point:** When we replace the glenohumeral joint with rTSA, the scapula becomes even MORE important — and this is underappreciated.

**Speaker Notes:**

**What rTSA does to the scapulothoracic joint:**
- Grammont design: medialization + distalization of center of rotation → increased deltoid moment arm (Boileau 2005; Gutiérrez 2007)
- The deltoid ORIGINATES FROM THE ACROMION — which is the scapula. Its new mechanical advantage means the scapular stabilizers must provide a DIFFERENT force profile to maintain a stable platform.
- **Walker et al. 2015:** **[STRONG — 3D fluoroscopic measurement, 77 citations]**
  - SHR in rTSA: **1.3:1** vs. normal **3:1** — a dramatic shift
  - This means rTSA shoulders use nearly EQUAL GH and ST motion — the scapula does MORE than half the work
  - n=28, 3 different implant designs, fluoroscopy with model-image registration
  - Walker's conclusion: *"particular attention to the periscapular muscles"* is needed in rTSA rehab
  - **The irony:** We replace the GH joint to improve function, but in doing so we make the patient MORE dependent on their scapulothoracic function, not less
- **Kawashima et al. 2025:** **[EMERGING — small sample but provocative]**
  - n=20 shoulders with semi-inlay rTSA
  - SHR ≥2 group: ASES score **94.3** vs. SHR <2: **82.1** (p=.007)
  - Significant positive correlation between SHR and patient-reported outcomes (r=0.452, p=.045)
  - **Translation:** Better scapulohumeral rhythm → better outcomes after rTSA
  - **Honest caveat:** n=20, single implant design. This needs replication in larger studies.
- **Levin/Jay et al. 2024 (JBJS):** Systematically modeled 486 rTSA configurations. The most important implant-design parameter for restoring native muscle-tendon lengths was neck-shaft angle (135° best). The deltoid originates from the acromion — its length-tension relationship is inextricable from scapular position. Even implant designers are now optimizing for relationships that depend on the scapula.
- **Crosby et al. 2011:** Scapular fractures after rTSA — is this the scapula failing under its new biomechanical demands?
- **Simovitch/Gerber 2007:** **44% inferior scapular notching rate** after Delta III rTSA. Glenosphere-scapular neck angle and craniocaudal position were highly correlated with notching (r=0.65-0.67). Notching adversely affected clinical outcomes. **The prosthesis is literally eroding the scapula** — another way rTSA demands more from scapular anatomy than the native shoulder.
- **Gibbons 2016:** After massive rotator cuff tears, muscle architecture changes fundamentally — sarcomere number decreases, fiber length shortens, and in massive tears sarcomere length itself drops below the optimal length-tension range. These changes may be **irreversible even with successful anatomical repair**. If the cuff muscles can't recover architecturally, the scapular stabilizers become even more critical for shoulder function.

## Slide 20: The Closing Argument (1 min)

**Speaker Notes:**

**The three pillars of scapular importance:**
1. **SHAPE** — CSA predicts cuff tears and OA. Posterior acromial morphology predicts posterior instability. Gerber is reshaping the scapula surgically to treat instability.
2. **MOTION** — The scapula contributes 1/3 of arm elevation, clears the subacromial space, and transmits all force from trunk to arm. When it moves wrong, impingement, cuff pathology, and instability follow.
3. **MUSCLES** — The scapula is stabilized entirely by muscle. These muscles can be measured with EMG, they show consistent patterns of imbalance in pathologic shoulders, and they can be specifically targeted with rehabilitation.

**And even when we replace the GH joint:** Scapulohumeral rhythm after rTSA is dramatically altered, the scapula works harder, and better SHR correlates with better outcomes.

**What we still don't know:**
- Does correcting dyskinesis prevent pathology? (No RCTs)
- Does pre-op scapular function predict surgical outcomes?
- What's the best way to objectively assess the scapula clinically?

**The Call to Action:**
- Examine the scapula on EVERY shoulder patient
- Measure the CSA on every shoulder radiograph
- Consider the scapular component of every shoulder pathology
- Think about how your surgical interventions change scapulothoracic demands

**Closing Line:**
*"We've spent decades focused on the glenohumeral joint — and for good reason. But the scapula has been quietly running the show all along. It's time we gave it the attention it deserves."*

## ANTICIPATED QUESTIONS & RESPONSES

**Q: "If dyskinesis is so common in asymptomatic people, how can it be pathologic?"**
A: "Great question. I'd make two points. First, the biomechanical data showing altered kinematics in symptomatic shoulders is measured objectively — it's not the same as clinical observation of dyskinesis in healthy people. Second, asymptomatic dyskinesis may represent a pre-symptomatic state — similar to how asymptomatic rotator cuff tears exist but some progress to symptomatic pathology. We don't have the longitudinal data yet, and I want to be honest about that gap."

**Q: "Isn't the CSA debate settled? Some papers argue it changes after cuff tear."**
A: "You're right that there's debate about whether CSA is a cause or consequence. The Viehöfer 2016 biomechanical data suggests a plausible causal mechanism — higher CSA requires more cuff activity, which could lead to fatigue and failure. But I agree we can't definitively prove directionality from the current evidence. What IS clear is that it's a scapular measurement that correlates with two of the most common shoulder pathologies."

**Q: "How do you examine the scapula clinically if the visual assessment is unreliable?"**
A: "Honestly, I don't think we have a great clinical tool yet — and that's part of the problem. The scapular assistance test is one option. Observation of winging, SICK scapula features, and comparing bilateral symmetry are reasonable but imperfect. The real need is for objective measurement tools — wearable sensors, motion capture in clinical settings. This is an area that needs innovation."

**Q: "What about the evidence for scapular-focused rehab? Does it actually work?"**
A: "The EMG data is strong for targeting specific muscles. The evidence that scapular rehab improves shoulder pathology outcomes is more limited — we don't have large RCTs comparing scapular-focused rehab to standard protocols. I believe it should be part of our approach based on the biomechanical rationale, but I want to be transparent that the treatment outcome data is an evidence gap."

**Q: "Is the rTSA/scapular connection just speculation?"**
A: "The Walker 2015 data showing dramatically altered SHR after rTSA is strong — it's 3D fluoroscopic measurement, not speculation. The Kawashima 2025 data linking better SHR to better outcomes is provocative but preliminary — small sample, needs replication. I present this as an emerging area with strong biological plausibility and early supporting data, not as established fact."
