# The Scapulothoracic Articulation as an Upstream Determinant of Shoulder Pathology: A Biomechanical Framework

## Abstract

The scapulothoracic articulation is unique in the musculoskeletal system. The scapula has no bony articulation with the axial skeleton and is stabilized entirely by the balance of tension forces in the surrounding musculature, making its position at any moment a vector summation of the tensions acting upon it. Because the scapula positions the glenoid, anchors the rotator cuff, and transmits force from the trunk to the arm, any change in this tension balance necessarily changes force distribution across every downstream structure. Recent evidence demonstrates that this upstream relationship produces at least four distinct clinical consequences depending on which downstream structure is overloaded: rotator cuff disease, posterior glenohumeral instability, progressive posterior glenoid wear, and variable outcomes after reverse total shoulder arthroplasty. These diverse pathologies are currently studied in separate literature silos, yet they may share a common biomechanical origin at the scapulothoracic articulation. In this paper, we present a framework that unifies these observations and identifies testable predictions for clinical investigation.

Level of Evidence: Level V; Expert Opinion / Narrative Review

Keywords: scapula; scapulothoracic; critical shoulder angle; tensegrity; reverse shoulder arthroplasty; scapulohumeral rhythm; posterior instability; glenoid wear

## Introduction

Shoulder pathology encompasses a broad spectrum of conditions, including rotator cuff tears, glenohumeral instability, osteoarthritis, and arthroplasty complications, each of which is typically studied as a distinct entity with a separate etiology. Yet a striking convergence has emerged in recent literature: investigations of each pathology independently identify the scapula as a significant contributing factor.

The critical shoulder angle, a measurement of scapular morphology, predicts both rotator cuff tears and osteoarthritis.^1-3^ Posterior acromial morphology predicts the direction of glenohumeral instability.^4,5^ Scapulothoracic orientation differs significantly in patients with constitutional posterior humeral decentering.^6^ Scapulohumeral rhythm after reverse total shoulder arthroplasty (rTSA) predicts functional outcomes.^7,8^ And scapular dyskinesis persists after successful rotator cuff repair, raising the possibility that scapular dysfunction may precede rather than follow cuff pathology.^9^

These findings, taken together, raise a fundamental question: are these separate observations, or are they different manifestations of a single biomechanical reality?

The purpose of this paper is to propose a unifying framework. We argue that the scapulothoracic articulation, as a bone with no bony connection to the axial skeleton that is stabilized entirely by muscular tension, functions as the upstream determinant of shoulder mechanics. Its morphology and its position govern force distribution to every downstream structure, and the specific pathology that develops in a given patient depends on which downstream structure is overloaded by the particular alteration in scapulothoracic force distribution.

## The Scapulothoracic Articulation: A Summation of Tension Forces

The scapula is the only major bone in the shoulder girdle with no direct bony articulation to the axial skeleton. It has no joint capsule connecting it to the thorax and no ligamentous attachment to the spine or rib cage. Its position on the thorax is determined entirely by the balance of tension in the muscles that surround it: the serratus anterior, the three divisions of the trapezius, the rhomboids, the levator scapulae, the pectoralis minor, and the muscles of the rotator cuff and deltoid that originate from its surface.

This anatomical arrangement carries an underappreciated mechanical consequence. The scapula's position at any moment is a vector summation of all the tension forces acting upon it. Altering any single tension element, whether through weakness, tightness, fatigue, neural inhibition, or structural injury, changes the summation and shifts the scapula to a new equilibrium position.

The sensitivity of this system has been quantified in several investigations. Kebaetse et al. demonstrated that a change in thoracic posture alone, simply slouching, reduces active shoulder abduction by 23.6° and abduction muscle force by 16.2%.^10^ Borstad showed that differences in pectoralis minor resting length among healthy individuals alter scapular kinematics in three rotational planes simultaneously.^11^ These are not pathologic populations; they are healthy subjects in whom a single alteration in the tension balance produces measurable kinematic consequences. Perhaps most striking is the subacromial space data: measured at 4.1 mm in healthy subjects at 90° of elevation, the space narrows to 1.4 mm in subjects with impingement.^11^ The margin between functional and pathologic is measured in millimeters, and scapulothoracic positioning determines where that margin falls.

Beyond its role as a kinematic regulator, the scapula serves as the sole link between the axial skeleton and the upper extremity in the kinetic chain. Kibler demonstrated that 51% of total kinetic energy and 54% of total force in a tennis serve originate from the legs and trunk, with all of it transmitted through the scapulothoracic articulation.^12^ Far from being a passive platform, the scapula is the critical relay station through which the body's entire force production reaches the arm.

## Scapular Morphology Determines Downstream Pathology

### Rotator Cuff Disease

The relationship between scapular morphology and rotator cuff disease has been progressively clarified over the past two decades. Nyffeler et al. introduced the acromion index, demonstrating that the lateral extension of the acromion is significantly greater in patients with rotator cuff tears (AI 0.73) than in those with intact cuffs (AI 0.64, P<0.0001).^13^ Building on this observation, Moor et al. developed the critical shoulder angle (CSA), which combines lateral acromial extension with glenoid inclination into a single measurement (Figure 1). In their study of 298 shoulders, the mean CSA was 33.1° in controls, 38.0° in rotator cuff tears, and 28.1° in osteoarthritis, with an AUC of 0.92 for discriminating cuff tears from controls and 0.90 for osteoarthritis.^1^

[FIGURE 1: Garcia CSA measurement]

The biomechanical mechanism underlying this association has been elucidated through complementary approaches. Viehöfer et al., using finite element modeling, demonstrated that a CSA of 38° produces a 23% higher shear-to-compression ratio at 50° of abduction compared to a normal CSA of 33°, requiring supraspinatus forces that exceed physiological values to maintain joint stability.^2^ Gerber et al. confirmed these findings on a physical shoulder simulator, showing that supraspinatus tendon load must increase by 13 to 33% (15 to 23 N) for a CSA of 38° compared to 33° to stabilize the arm during abduction.^14^ Blonna et al. extended this further by demonstrating a dose-response relationship: CSA averaged 34° in controls, 36° with isolated supraspinatus tears, and 40° with combined supraspinatus and infraspinatus tears. The CSA proved to be the most relevant risk factor (OR 1.7, CI 1.4 to 2.0) even after controlling for age, gender, smoking, and BMI.^3^

What elevates these findings from academic observation to clinical significance is the surgical outcome data. Garcia et al. found that a CSA exceeding 38° produces an odds ratio of 14.8 for full-thickness retear after arthroscopic rotator cuff repair (P<0.01).^15^ In response, Gerber et al. demonstrated that arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty (lateral, not the traditional anterior approach) safely reduces the CSA from a mean of 37.5° to 33.9° (P<0.001) without compromising the deltoid origin. Patients whose CSA was corrected to 33° or less had 25% greater abduction strength than those whose postoperative CSA remained at or above 35° (P=0.04). Notably, postoperative CSA was significantly larger in failed repairs than in healed repairs (P=0.026).^16^ Passaplan et al. added an important piece to this puzzle by demonstrating that the CSA does not change over a decade, even in shoulders that develop new cuff tears during follow-up, establishing it as an innate anatomical parameter rather than a consequence of pathology.^17^

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that not all studies have replicated these findings. Bjarnison et al. found CSA associated with osteoarthritis (OR 2.25, P=0.002) but not with rotator cuff tears (OR 1.12, P=0.63) in their cohort.^18^ The weight of evidence tilts toward CSA mattering, but the debate remains live.

The observation most relevant to the framework we propose is this: the CSA is a measurement of scapular morphology. The acromion is the scapula. A morphological feature of the upstream structure determines the force environment of the downstream structure.

### Posterior Glenohumeral Instability

A parallel line of investigation from Gerber's research group has revealed a similar upstream relationship for glenohumeral instability, but operating through a different anatomical parameter of the same bone. Meyer et al. demonstrated that posterior acromial morphology is significantly associated with posterior shoulder instability (Figure 2, Figure 3). Posterior acromial height (PAH) measured 30.9 mm in patients with posterior instability versus 19.5 mm in those with anterior instability (P<0.001). At a cutoff of PAH of 23 mm or greater, the odds ratio for posterior instability was 39.^4^ For context, this odds ratio exceeds the association between cigarette smoking and lung cancer.

[FIGURE 2: Akgun 3D measurements]

[FIGURE 3: Gerber 3D overlay]

Akgun et al. expanded on these morphological findings using three-dimensional computed tomography, demonstrating that multiple scapular parameters, including anterior and posterior acromial coverage, posterior acromial height, posterior acromial tilt, and the CSA, all differ significantly in patients with constitutional posterior decentering compared to healthy controls.^6^

The biomechanical significance of these morphological differences was established by Hochreiter et al., who tested three-dimensional printed scapula models and found that correcting glenoid retroversion alone was not sufficient to restore normal posterior translation. Both glenoid and acromial anatomy must be corrected.^5^ This is a paradigm-shifting finding: the acromion is an active restraint to posterior humeral head translation, not a passive bystander. Gerber et al. have since translated these findings into clinical practice with three-dimensional planned scapular osteotomies, though this approach remains early and is not yet widely adopted.^19^

The pattern is the same as for cuff disease, but the downstream pathology is different. A morphological feature of the scapula determines the mechanical restraint environment of the glenohumeral joint. Change the scapular morphology, and you change which direction the shoulder fails.

### Posterior Glenoid Wear: The Walch B0 Hypothesis

Perhaps the most intellectually compelling extension of this framework, though also the most speculative, concerns the Walch B0 glenoid. Domos et al. described this entity as pre-arthritic posterior subluxation of the humeral head, a condition that appears to progress inevitably toward eccentric posterior glenoid erosion through the B1 and B2 stages (Figure 4).^20^

[FIGURE 4: B0 progression]

A key observation provides the geometric foundation for this hypothesis. Bryce et al. demonstrated that scapular rotation changes measured glenoid version by up to 22.5°, with 0.42° of relative anteversion for every degree of scapular abduction in the coronal plane.^21^ Their paper framed this finding as a computed tomography measurement artifact, cautioning clinicians about the influence of scapular position on version calculations. We propose an alternative interpretation: if scapular position dynamically changes effective glenoid version during functional movement, then a scapula that chronically sits in more internal rotation, whether from habitual posture, pectoralis minor tightness, thoracic kyphosis, or individual morphological variation, presents a functionally more retroverted glenoid during loading.

Recent data from Akgun et al. lend support to this concept. Their study demonstrated that patients with constitutional posterior humeral decentering (the C1 phenotype, which is analogous to the B0 glenoid) have significantly different scapulothoracic orientation compared to healthy controls, including less scapular upward rotation and less scapular tilt, alongside different posterior acromial morphology and altered muscle volume distribution (Figure 5).^6^

[FIGURE 5: Akgun ST orientation]

We cannot yet establish causation. The scapulothoracic orientation differences that Akgun observed could be a consequence of the posterior decentering rather than its cause. But the geometry is clear: scapulothoracic position changes effective glenoid version,^21^ and patients with posterior decentering have different scapulothoracic position.^6^ Whether chronic dynamic functional retroversion from scapulothoracic malposition initiates the posterior wear cascade that Domos described is a testable question that, to our knowledge, has not yet been asked.

### Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty

When a reverse shoulder prosthesis is implanted, the biomechanics of the glenohumeral joint change fundamentally, and the consequences for the scapulothoracic system are significant. Walker et al. measured scapulohumeral rhythm using fluoroscopy with three-dimensional model-image registration across three different implant designs and found that SHR in rTSA is 1.3:1, compared to 3:1 in normal shoulders.^7^ This means the scapulothoracic system contributes approximately half of arm elevation after rTSA, a dramatic increase from its normal contribution.

Kawashima et al. demonstrated that this altered scapulothoracic contribution has direct clinical consequences. In a study of semi-inlay rTSA, patients with SHR of 2 or greater had significantly higher ASES scores (94.3, SD 4.6) than those with SHR below 2 (82.1, SD 9.4, P=0.007), with a significant positive correlation between SHR and patient-reported outcomes (r=0.452, P=0.045).^8^ Moroder et al. showed that scapulothoracic orientation directly influences optimal humeral component retrotorsion in rTSA, with scapular internal rotation correlating with the correction angle at R=0.91 when adjusted for glenoid retroversion.^22^ Shekhbihi et al. framed this collective body of work as "the scapulothoracic conundrum," calling for integration of scapulothoracic assessment into rTSA planning and rehabilitation.^23^

The clinical significance of persistent scapulothoracic dysfunction is underscored by kinematic data from Mercurio et al., who prospectively demonstrated that scapular dyskinesis persists after successful arthroscopic rotator cuff repair despite improved range of motion and Constant scores; scapulohumeral rhythm showed no significant change in their cohort (P>0.05) (Figure 6).^9^ If dyskinesis were simply a consequence of the cuff tear, it should resolve after repair. The fact that it does not suggests that the scapulothoracic dysfunction is either independent of, or upstream of, the cuff pathology.

[FIGURE 6: Mercurio kinematic data]

The biological mechanism underlying this persistence may involve structural muscle remodeling. Gibbons et al. showed that massive rotator cuff tears produce sarcomere loss, fiber shortening, and, in massive tears, sarcomere length below the optimal operating range, representing architectural changes that may be irreversible even with successful tendon repair.^24^ If similar remodeling occurs in the periscapular muscles of patients with chronic shoulder dysfunction (biologically plausible but unproven), then the scapulothoracic system may be structurally committed to dysfunction before surgery is performed. This reasoning provides the strongest case for scapulothoracic prehabilitation before shoulder surgery: not to strengthen muscles in the conventional sense, but to restore sarcomere architecture and tension balance while remodeling remains possible, before imposing the new biomechanical demands of a prosthesis.

## The Unifying Principle

The four examples above involve four different pathologies, four different patient populations, and four different downstream structures failing. In every case, however, the scapula is upstream. In every case, a change in the scapula's morphology or position changes force distribution to a downstream structure. And in every case, the specific pathology that develops depends on which downstream structure bears the consequence.

This pattern is consistent with a structural principle that has been extensively validated at the cellular level: tensegrity (Figure 7). Tensegrity, short for tensional integrity, describes a system where compression elements float within a continuous tension network, and stability emerges from the balance of tension rather than from compressive stacking.^25-27^ Ingber and colleagues have demonstrated, through several decades of experimental work, that living cells use tensegrity architecture. Microtubules serve as compression elements, actin filaments form the tension network, and the cell's shape, stiffness, and mechanotransduction responses are all governed by the pre-stress in this network.^25-27^ Chen and Ingber extended this principle from the cytoskeleton to the skeleton, arguing that the musculoskeletal system uses the same organizational logic: bones as compression elements, muscles and fascia as the continuous tension network.^28^

[FIGURE 7: Tensegrity models]

The scapulothoracic articulation may be the clearest musculoskeletal example of this architecture. The scapula is a compression element with no bony attachment to the axial skeleton, suspended entirely within a continuous tension network of periscapular musculature. Its position is a pure summation of the tension vectors in that network. The clinical observations presented in this paper align with several core tensegrity predictions: that pre-stress in the tension network determines system stiffness and function (consistent with the data of Kebaetse^10^), that alteration of the compression element's geometry changes force distribution throughout the network (consistent with the CSA and posterior acromial morphology data^1-3,14-16^), that local perturbation produces global reorganization (consistent with the findings of Borstad^11^ and Burkhart^29^), and that the system self-stabilizes at a new equilibrium after perturbation (consistent with the persistence of dyskinesis demonstrated by Mercurio^9^).

We propose this framework not as a theoretical assertion, but as a biomechanical lens that organizes existing clinical evidence and generates testable predictions. It does not replace existing understanding of individual pathologies. It contextualizes them within a system where the scapula is the common upstream variable.

## Clinical Implications and Testable Predictions

This framework has immediate clinical implications and generates several predictions suitable for prospective investigation.

From a practical standpoint, we recommend that the CSA be measured on every pre-operative shoulder radiograph. The measurement takes 30 seconds, has excellent inter- and intra-observer reliability,^1^ and may influence surgical planning. For rotator cuff repair in the setting of CSA exceeding 35°, arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty should be considered as an adjunct based on current evidence.^16^ Additionally, scapulothoracic function should be assessed in every shoulder patient. The scapular assistance test, in which the examiner manually repositions the scapula into posterior tilt and upward rotation during arm elevation, changes subacromial space dimensions and can identify a scapulothoracic contribution to symptoms in seconds.^30^

Beyond these immediate applications, the framework generates predictions that warrant formal investigation. First, we predict that pre-operative scapulothoracic function should predict surgical outcomes independently of surgical variables. For both rotator cuff repair and rTSA, prospective assessment of scapulothoracic kinematics and periscapular muscle function should correlate with post-operative outcomes. To our knowledge, this has not been studied. Second, we predict that scapulothoracic prehabilitation before shoulder arthroplasty should improve outcomes. If scapulothoracic pre-stress determines the system's ability to accommodate the biomechanical demands of a prosthesis, then restoring that pre-stress before surgery, while sarcomere remodeling is still possible, should produce better outcomes than rehabilitation initiated after surgery. A randomized pilot comparing standard perioperative rehabilitation to a protocol that includes 6 to 12 weeks of scapulothoracic prehabilitation would be a reasonable first step. Third, we predict that dynamic glenoid version measurement would identify patients at risk for posterior glenoid wear before irreversible erosion develops. Biplanar fluoroscopy during functional tasks could measure effective glenoid version as a function of scapulothoracic position, revealing whether patients with the B0 phenotype have greater dynamic functional retroversion than their static computed tomography measurements suggest.

## Limitations

Several limitations of this framework must be acknowledged. The tensegrity model at the musculoskeletal level remains qualitative; while the predictions match clinical observations, quantitative computational modeling of the scapulothoracic system as a tensegrity structure has not been performed. The B0 hypothesis is largely speculative, as causation between scapulothoracic orientation and posterior glenoid wear has not been established. The scapulothoracic system is actively controlled by the nervous system, introducing a level of complexity that static tensegrity models cannot capture. And not all evidence supporting the individual components of this framework is consistent; the association between CSA and rotator cuff tears, in particular, has not been replicated in all studies.^18^

Despite these limitations, the convergence of evidence from morphological, kinematic, arthroplasty, and cellular biology literature on the scapula as a common upstream factor is difficult to dismiss. The predictions generated by this framework are specific and testable, and their investigation has the potential to improve clinical decision-making across the spectrum of shoulder pathology.

## Conclusion

The scapulothoracic articulation is a compression element suspended in a continuous tension network. Its morphology determines force distribution to the rotator cuff and the glenohumeral joint. Its position determines effective glenoid orientation and subacromial space geometry. When we surgically alter the glenohumeral joint, the tension demands on this system increase. The specific downstream pathology that develops in a given patient, whether cuff disease, posterior instability, glenoid wear, or suboptimal arthroplasty outcome, depends on which downstream structure bears the consequence of the particular alteration in scapulothoracic force distribution.

This is, we believe, a single biomechanical reality producing diverse clinical manifestations. The framework we propose, rooted in established clinical evidence and consistent with the structural principle of tensegrity, unifies observations that currently reside in separate literature silos and generates predictions that, if validated, could change how we assess, plan for, and rehabilitate patients across the full spectrum of shoulder pathology.
