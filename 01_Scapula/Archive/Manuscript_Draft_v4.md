## Abstract

The scapula has no bony articulation with the axial skeleton and is stabilized entirely by the balance of tension forces in the surrounding musculature. Its position is therefore a vector summation of the tensions acting upon it. Because the scapula positions the glenoid, anchors the rotator cuff, and transmits force from the trunk to the arm, alterations in this tension balance influence force distribution across every downstream structure. Recent evidence suggests that the scapula exerts this upstream influence through two mechanisms, its static morphology and its dynamic positioning, producing distinct downstream pathologies depending on which structure is overloaded. We present a framework unifying four clinical examples of this relationship and propose directions for future investigation.

Level of Evidence: Level V; Expert Opinion / Narrative Review

Keywords: scapula; scapulothoracic; critical shoulder angle; tensegrity; reverse shoulder arthroplasty; scapulohumeral rhythm; posterior instability; glenoid wear

## Introduction

Shoulder pathology encompasses a broad spectrum of conditions, including rotator cuff tears, glenohumeral instability, osteoarthritis, and arthroplasty complications, each typically studied as a distinct entity with a separate etiology. Yet a convergence has emerged in recent literature that is difficult to ignore: investigations of each pathology independently identify the scapula as a significant contributing factor. The critical shoulder angle predicts both rotator cuff tears and osteoarthritis.(1-3) Posterior acromial morphology predicts the direction of glenohumeral instability.(4,5) Scapulothoracic orientation differs in patients with constitutional posterior humeral decentering.(6) Scapulohumeral rhythm after reverse total shoulder arthroplasty (rTSA) correlates with functional outcomes.(7,8) And scapular dyskinesis persists after successful rotator cuff repair, raising the possibility that scapular dysfunction may precede rather than follow cuff pathology.(9)

The senior author's interest in this convergence grew out of a personal experience with scapulothoracic dysfunction that was initially misattributed to a neurological deficit. The process of understanding how an abnormal scapular position could alter the mechanical effectiveness of muscles throughout the upper extremity, and recognizing the resolution of symptoms through targeted scapular retraining, prompted the literature investigation that follows.

The purpose of this paper is not to claim that the scapulothoracic articulation explains all shoulder pathology, but to draw attention to a pattern in the evidence that has not been fully appreciated. The scapula's morphology and its position influence force distribution to every downstream structure, and the specific pathology that develops in a given patient may depend, at least in part, on which downstream structure bears the consequence. We present this framework as a starting point for discussion, not as a definitive model.

## The Scapulothoracic Articulation: A Summation of Tension Forces

The scapula is the only major bone in the shoulder girdle with no direct bony articulation to the axial skeleton. It has no joint capsule connecting it to the thorax and no ligamentous attachment to the spine or rib cage, making it an unconstrained system in which all stability is provided by the muscles that surround it. Its position on the thorax is determined entirely by the balance of tension in these muscles, including the serratus anterior, the three divisions of the trapezius, the rhomboids, the levator scapulae, the pectoralis minor, and the muscles of the rotator cuff and deltoid that originate from its surface. Scapular rotation during arm elevation is produced by coordinated force couples, particularly the upper trapezius acting with the serratus anterior and lower trapezius to generate upward rotation, a relationship validated through three-dimensional electromagnetic tracking and electromyographic analysis.(12,32,34) This arrangement, in which a compression element is suspended within a continuous tension network with no bony constraint, is consistent with what structural engineers and cell biologists describe as tensegrity architecture, a principle validated extensively at the cellular level by Ingber and colleagues(25-27) and extended to the musculoskeletal system by Chen and Ingber.(28)

The scapula's position at any moment is a vector summation of all the tension forces acting upon it. Altering any single tension element changes the summation and shifts the scapula to a new equilibrium. The sensitivity of this system has been quantified: Kebaetse et al. showed that slouching alone reduces active shoulder abduction by 23.6 degrees and abduction muscle force by 16.2%.(10) Borstad showed that pectoralis minor resting length differences in healthy individuals alter scapular kinematics in three rotational planes simultaneously.(11) The subacromial space, as reviewed by Borstad, measures 4.1 mm in healthy subjects at 90 degrees of elevation and narrows to 1.4 mm in impingement,(11) illustrating how directly scapulothoracic positioning influences the margin between functional and pathologic mechanics.

The downstream consequences of a positional shift extend beyond kinematics. When the scapula moves to a new resting position, the resting lengths of every attached muscle change, moving each to a different point on its length-tension curve and altering the force it generates for a given neural activation.(24) This changes the balance of the force couples controlling scapular rotation,(34) which may alter the timing and sequencing of muscle activation as the neuromuscular system recalibrates.(35,36) If sustained, these changes may be reinforced by structural sarcomere remodeling that optimizes the muscle for the new, suboptimal position.(24) What begins as a positional shift can therefore progress, through individually well-understood physiological mechanisms, into a self-reinforcing pattern of altered mechanics, altered motor control, and altered muscle architecture.

The scapula also serves as the sole link between the axial skeleton and the upper extremity in the kinetic chain. Kibler reported that 51% of total kinetic energy and 54% of total force in a tennis serve originate from the legs and trunk, all transmitted through the scapulothoracic articulation.(12)

The scapula exerts its upstream influence through two distinct pathways: static morphology (the innate bony architecture of the acromion and glenoid, which Passaplan et al. showed does not change over a decade(17)) and dynamic positioning (the scapula's orientation during functional movement, which is governed by periscapular muscle forces and is potentially modifiable). Both alter the force environment experienced by downstream structures, but through different mechanisms. The following clinical examples illustrate each.

## Scapular Morphology and Downstream Pathology

### Rotator Cuff Disease

Nyffeler et al. showed that lateral acromial extension is significantly greater in patients with rotator cuff tears (acromion index 0.73) than in those with intact cuffs (0.64, P<0.0001).(13) Moor et al. refined this into the critical shoulder angle (CSA), combining acromial extension with glenoid inclination (Figure 1). The mean CSA was 33.1 degrees in controls, 38.0 degrees in rotator cuff tears, and 28.1 degrees in osteoarthritis, with an AUC of 0.92.(1)

[FIGURE 1: Garcia CSA measurement]

A striking feature of Moor's data is that rotator cuff tears and primary glenohumeral osteoarthritis are inversely related, and the biomechanical explanation centers on the shear-to-compression ratio at the glenohumeral joint. Viehöfer et al. demonstrated that a CSA of 38 degrees produces a 23% higher shear-to-compression ratio at 50 degrees of abduction, requiring supraspinatus forces exceeding physiological values to maintain stability.(2) Gerber et al. confirmed this on a shoulder simulator, showing supraspinatus load must increase by 13 to 33% for a CSA of 38 degrees compared to 33 degrees.(14) In a high-CSA shoulder, the deltoid generates a relatively greater superior shear vector, and the cuff must work against this shear to center the humeral head, chronically overloading it and predisposing to degeneration while relatively sparing the articular surface. In a low-CSA shoulder, the deltoid's vector is directed more compressively into the glenoid, the cuff and deltoid function as agonists, and the cartilage bears greater cumulative compressive load, predisposing to osteoarthritis while relatively sparing the cuff.(1,2,14) The scapula's morphology, through a single geometric parameter, acts as a mechanical switch determining which downstream structure is preferentially loaded. Blonna et al. demonstrated a dose-response within the cuff tear population: CSA averaged 34, 36, and 40 degrees with increasing tear severity, and CSA was the most relevant risk factor (OR 1.7, CI 1.4 to 2.0) after controlling for age, gender, smoking, and BMI.(3)

Garcia et al. found that CSA exceeding 38 degrees produces an odds ratio of 14.8 for full-thickness retear after cuff repair (P<0.01).(15) Gerber et al. showed that arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty (lateral, not the traditional anterior approach) reduces the CSA from 37.5 to 33.9 degrees (P<0.001) without compromising the deltoid. Patients corrected to 33 degrees or less had 25% greater abduction strength (P=0.04), and postoperative CSA was larger in failed than healed repairs (P=0.026).(16) A systematic review by Docter et al. corroborated the association in most included studies while noting heterogeneity.(33) Not all studies agree: Bjarnison et al. found CSA associated with osteoarthritis but not rotator cuff tears in their cohort.(18) The debate remains live.

### Posterior Glenohumeral Instability

Meyer et al. found that posterior acromial height (PAH) measured 30.9 mm in posterior instability versus 19.5 mm in anterior instability (P<0.001), with an odds ratio of 39 at a PAH cutoff of 23 mm (Figure 2, Figure 3).(4) Akgun et al. confirmed that multiple scapular parameters, including acromial coverage, PAH, acromial tilt, and CSA, all differ significantly in constitutional posterior decentering (Figure 2).(6) Hochreiter et al. tested three-dimensional printed scapula models and found that correcting glenoid retroversion alone was insufficient; both glenoid and acromial anatomy must be corrected to normalize posterior stability.(5) Gerber et al. have translated these findings into three-dimensional planned scapular osteotomies, though this approach remains early.(19)

An innate morphological feature of the scapula shapes the mechanical restraint environment of the glenohumeral joint. A different parameter of the same bone, operating through a different mechanism, produces a different downstream pathology.

## Scapulothoracic Positioning and Downstream Pathology

### Posterior Glenoid Wear: The Walch B0 Hypothesis

Domos et al. described the Walch B0 glenoid as pre-arthritic posterior subluxation that progresses inevitably toward posterior glenoid erosion (Figure 4).(20) Bryce et al. showed that scapular rotation changes measured glenoid version by up to 22.5 degrees.(21) Their paper framed this as a measurement artifact. We propose an alternative interpretation: if scapular position dynamically changes effective glenoid version during functional movement, then a scapula that chronically sits in more internal rotation would present a functionally more retroverted glenoid during loading.

[FIGURE 4: B0 progression]

Akgun et al. found that patients with constitutional posterior decentering have significantly different scapulothoracic orientation, including less upward rotation and less tilt, alongside different acromial morphology (Figure 5).(6) It is notable that these patients exhibited less thoracic kyphosis than controls, suggesting the orientation differences may be driven by intrinsic morphological factors rather than postural habits.

[FIGURE 5: Akgun ST orientation]

Causation has not been established. But the magnitude of functional version change need not be large on any given day to be clinically meaningful: articular cartilage degeneration is driven by cumulative loading over time, and a small but persistent asymmetry maintained over years of daily use could represent a meaningful mechanical dose even if the instantaneous asymmetry falls within the tolerance of healthy cartilage. This is consistent with the B0 to B2 progression occurring over decades. Whether chronic dynamic functional retroversion from scapulothoracic malposition contributes to this cumulative loading is a testable question that has not been asked.

### Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty

Walker et al. found that SHR in rTSA is 1.3:1 compared to 3:1 in normal shoulders, meaning the scapulothoracic system contributes approximately half of arm elevation after rTSA.(7) Kawashima et al. found that patients with SHR of 2 or greater had ASES scores of 94.3 compared to 82.1 for those below 2 (P=0.007) in a study of 20 semi-inlay rTSA patients, though the sample size limits generalizability.(8) Moroder et al. showed scapulothoracic orientation influences optimal component retrotorsion (R=0.91).(22) Shekhbihi et al. framed this as "the scapulothoracic conundrum."(23)

Mercurio et al. prospectively showed that scapular dyskinesis persists after cuff repair despite improved range of motion and Constant scores (P>0.05 for SHR change) (Figure 6).(9) If dyskinesis were caused by the cuff tear, it should resolve after repair. Gibbons et al. showed that massive cuff tears produce sarcomere remodeling that may be irreversible,(24) and if similar changes occur in the periscapular muscles of chronically dysfunctional shoulders, the system may be structurally committed to dysfunction before surgery. This provides a rationale for scapulothoracic prehabilitation: restoring muscle architecture while remodeling remains possible, before imposing the demands of a prosthesis. Evidence that scapular-focused exercise improves outcomes in impingement(31) lends plausibility to this hypothesis, though it has not been tested in the arthroplasty population.

## The Unifying Principle

Four different pathologies, four different downstream structures failing, and in every case the scapula is upstream. This pattern is consistent with tensegrity architecture (Figure 7), in which compression elements float within a continuous tension network and stability emerges from tension balance rather than compressive stacking.(25-28) The scapulothoracic articulation fits this description: a compression element with no bony attachment, suspended in a continuous tension network, with its position determined by the vector summation of forces.

[FIGURE 7: Tensegrity models]

The clinical observations align with tensegrity predictions: pre-stress determines stiffness (Kebaetse(10)), altered geometry changes force distribution (CSA and PAH data(1-3,14-16)), local perturbation produces global reorganization (Borstad(11), Burkhart(29)), and the system self-stabilizes at new equilibria (Mercurio(9)). Whether tensegrity is the correct formal model remains open. We raise it as a lens that organizes these observations, not as a claim requiring defense. If the analogy is imperfect, the observations remain.

## Questions Raised and Directions for Investigation

The CSA takes 30 seconds to measure on a standard radiograph with excellent reliability.(1) Routine measurement seems reasonable. Whether lateral acromioplasty should adjunct cuff repair when CSA exceeds 35 degrees is supported by Gerber's data(16) but untested in randomized fashion. The scapular assistance test offers a simple way to assess scapulothoracic contribution to symptoms.(30)

Several questions emerge. Pre-operative scapulothoracic function may predict surgical outcomes for both cuff repair and rTSA; this has not been studied. Structured prehabilitation before arthroplasty, initiated early enough for sarcomere remodeling,(24) could improve outcomes; this has not been tested. Dynamic glenoid version measurement via biplanar fluoroscopy could reveal whether B0 patients have greater functional retroversion than static imaging suggests. And if feedforward motor control timing(35) operates at the scapulothoracic level as it does at the spine, some forms of dyskinesis may represent timing rather than force deficits, with distinct rehabilitation implications.(36)

## Limitations

This framework emphasizes extrinsic mechanical factors. A substantial body of literature implicates intrinsic tendon biology, including age-related degeneration, vascular insufficiency, and genetic predisposition, as contributors to rotator cuff disease independent of scapulothoracic mechanics. Twin studies have demonstrated a heritable component that cannot be explained by loading alone. This framework is not intended to displace these factors but to highlight an additional variable that may interact with them. The tensegrity model remains qualitative at the musculoskeletal level. The B0 hypothesis is speculative. The nervous system introduces complexity that static models cannot capture. Not all evidence is consistent: the CSA-cuff tear association has not replicated in all cohorts,(18) and the role of acromial morphology in cuff pathology remains debated. Confounding by shared genetic or developmental factors cannot be excluded.

Despite these limitations, the convergence across morphological, kinematic, arthroplasty, and cellular biology literature on the scapula as a common upstream factor is difficult to dismiss. The questions this framework raises are specific and testable.

## Conclusion

The scapulothoracic articulation is a compression element suspended in a continuous tension network. Its morphology influences force distribution to the cuff and glenohumeral joint. Its positioning influences effective glenoid orientation and subacromial geometry. When we alter the glenohumeral joint surgically, the demands on this system increase. The pathology that develops depends on which downstream structure bears the consequence.

Whether this represents a single biomechanical reality producing diverse clinical manifestations cannot be determined from existing literature alone. What can be said is that evidence from independent investigations by different research groups points consistently toward the scapulothoracic articulation as a common and underappreciated variable. We hope this paper stimulates the kind of investigation that will determine whether the framework holds up or requires revision.
