# The Scapulothoracic Articulation as an Upstream Determinant of Shoulder Pathology: A Biomechanical Framework

## Abstract

The scapulothoracic articulation is unique in the musculoskeletal system: the scapula has no bony articulation with the axial skeleton and is stabilized entirely by the balance of tension forces in the surrounding musculature. Its position at any moment is therefore a summation of the tension vectors acting upon it. Because the scapula positions the glenoid, anchors the rotator cuff, and transmits force from the trunk to the arm, any change in this tension balance — whether from morphological variation, postural alteration, muscular imbalance, or surgical intervention — changes force distribution across every downstream structure. Recent evidence demonstrates that this upstream relationship produces at least four distinct clinical consequences depending on which downstream structure is overloaded: rotator cuff disease, posterior glenohumeral instability, progressive posterior glenoid wear, and variable outcomes after reverse total shoulder arthroplasty. These diverse pathologies, currently studied in separate literature silos, may share a common biomechanical origin at the scapulothoracic articulation. We present a framework that unifies these observations and identifies testable predictions for clinical investigation.

**Level of Evidence:** Level V; Expert Opinion / Narrative Review

## Introduction

Shoulder pathology encompasses a spectrum of conditions — rotator cuff tears, glenohumeral instability, osteoarthritis, and arthroplasty complications — that are typically studied as distinct entities with separate etiologies. Yet a striking convergence has emerged in recent literature: investigations of each pathology independently identify the scapula as a significant contributing factor.

The critical shoulder angle, a measurement of scapular morphology, predicts both rotator cuff tears and osteoarthritis.^1-3^ Posterior acromial morphology predicts the direction of glenohumeral instability.^4,5^ Scapulothoracic orientation differs in patients with constitutional posterior humeral decentering.^6^ Scapulohumeral rhythm after reverse total shoulder arthroplasty (rTSA) predicts functional outcomes.^7,8^ And scapular dyskinesis persists after successful rotator cuff repair, suggesting that scapular dysfunction may precede rather than follow cuff pathology.^9^

These findings raise a fundamental question: are these separate observations, or are they different manifestations of a single biomechanical reality?

The purpose of this paper is to propose a unifying framework. The scapulothoracic articulation — a bone with no bony connection to the axial skeleton, stabilized entirely by muscular tension — functions as the upstream determinant of shoulder mechanics. Its morphology and position govern force distribution to every downstream structure. The specific pathology that develops in a given patient depends on which downstream structure is overloaded by the specific alteration in scapulothoracic force distribution.

This framework is consistent with a structural principle well-established in cellular biomechanics: tensegrity. In this paper, we present the clinical evidence that supports this framework, identify the structural principle that explains it, and propose specific, testable predictions for clinical investigation.

## The Scapulothoracic Articulation: A Summation of Tension Forces

The scapula is the only major bone in the shoulder girdle with no direct bony articulation to the axial skeleton. It has no joint capsule connecting it to the thorax. It has no ligamentous attachment to the spine or rib cage. Its position on the thorax is determined entirely by the balance of tension in the muscles that surround it — the serratus anterior, the trapezius (upper, middle, and lower divisions), the rhomboids, the levator scapulae, the pectoralis minor, and the muscles of the rotator cuff and deltoid that originate from its surface.

This anatomical arrangement has a mechanical consequence that is underappreciated: the scapula's position at any moment is a vector summation of all tension forces acting upon it. Change any single tension element — through weakness, tightness, fatigue, neural inhibition, or structural injury — and the summation changes. The scapula shifts to a new equilibrium position.

The sensitivity of this system has been quantified. Kebaetse et al. demonstrated that thoracic posture change alone — slouching — reduces active shoulder abduction by 23.6° and abduction muscle force by 16.2%.^10^ Borstad demonstrated that pectoralis minor resting length differences in healthy individuals alter scapular kinematics in three rotational planes simultaneously.^11^ The subacromial space, measured at 4.1mm in healthy subjects at 90° of elevation, narrows to 1.4mm in subjects with impingement.^11^ The margin between functional and pathologic is measured in millimeters, and scapulothoracic positioning determines where the margin falls.

The scapula also serves as the sole link between the axial skeleton and the upper extremity in the kinetic chain. Kibler demonstrated that 51% of the total kinetic energy and 54% of the total force in a tennis serve originate from the legs and trunk, all transmitted through the scapulothoracic articulation.^12^ The scapula is not merely a passive platform — it is the critical relay station through which the body's force production reaches the arm.

## Scapular Morphology Determines Downstream Pathology

### Rotator Cuff Disease

Nyffeler et al. introduced the acromion index, demonstrating that the lateral extension of the acromion is significantly greater in patients with rotator cuff tears (AI 0.73) than in those with intact cuffs (AI 0.64, p<0.0001).^13^ Moor et al. refined this into the critical shoulder angle (CSA), combining lateral acromial extension with glenoid inclination. In their study of 298 shoulders, the mean CSA was 33.1° in controls, 38.0° in rotator cuff tears, and 28.1° in osteoarthritis, with an AUC of 0.92 for discriminating cuff tears from controls.^1^

The mechanism has been established biomechanically. Viehöfer et al. used finite element modeling to show that a CSA of 38° produces a 23% higher shear-to-compression ratio at 50° of abduction compared to a CSA of 33°, requiring supraspinatus forces exceeding physiological values to maintain joint stability.^2^ Gerber et al. confirmed this on a shoulder simulator, showing that supraspinatus tendon load must increase by 13-33% (15-23N) for a CSA of 38° compared to 33° to stabilize the arm during abduction.^14^ Davide et al. demonstrated a dose-response relationship: CSA was 34° in controls, 36° with isolated supraspinatus tears, and 40° with supraspinatus and infraspinatus tears, with CSA being the most relevant risk factor (OR 1.7, CI 1.4-2.0) after controlling for age, gender, smoking, and BMI.^3^

Critically, this morphological parameter also predicts surgical failure. Garcia et al. found that CSA >38° produces an odds ratio of 14.8 for full-thickness retear after arthroscopic rotator cuff repair (p<0.01).^15^ Gerber et al. demonstrated that arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty — not anterior, but lateral — safely corrects the CSA from a mean of 37.5° to 33.9° (p<0.001) without compromising the deltoid. Patients corrected to CSA ≤33° had 25% greater abduction strength than those with CSA ≥35° (p=0.04), and postoperative CSA was significantly larger in failed repairs than in healed repairs (p=0.026).^16^ Passaplan et al. confirmed that CSA is a stable anatomical parameter that does not change over a decade, even in shoulders that develop new cuff tears, establishing it as an innate risk factor rather than a consequence of pathology.^17^

Not all studies agree — Bjarnison et al. found CSA associated with osteoarthritis (OR 2.25, p=0.002) but not with rotator cuff tears (OR 1.12, p=0.63) in their cohort.^18^ The evidence tilts toward CSA mattering, but the debate is live, and intellectual honesty requires acknowledging the inconsistency.

The key observation for our framework: the CSA is a measurement of scapular morphology. The acromion IS the scapula. A morphological feature of the scapula — the upstream structure — determines the force environment of the rotator cuff — the downstream structure.

### Posterior Glenohumeral Instability

Meyer et al. demonstrated that posterior acromial morphology is significantly associated with posterior shoulder instability. Posterior acromial height (PAH) was 30.9mm in posterior instability versus 19.5mm in anterior instability (p<0.001). With a cutoff of PAH ≥23mm, the odds ratio for posterior instability was 39.^4^

Hochreiter et al. tested this biomechanically using 3D-printed scapula models and found that correcting glenoid retroversion alone was not sufficient to restore normal posterior translation — both glenoid and acromial anatomy must be corrected.^5^ This finding is paradigm-shifting: it means the acromion is an active restraint to posterior humeral head translation, not a passive bystander.

Gerber et al. have translated this into clinical practice with the SCOPE procedure (Scapular Corrective Osteotomy for Posterior Escape) — 3D-planned osteotomies using patient-specific cutting guides to reshape the acromion and glenoid. In their initial series of 9 patients (6 of whom were revisions from failed prior approaches), median subjective shoulder value improved by 42.5%, and 8 of 9 showed improved humeral head centering.^19^ This is early data with inherent limitations. But the direction is significant: one of the most experienced shoulder surgeons in the world concluded that treating posterior instability requires reshaping the scapula.

Again: a morphological feature of the scapula — the posterior acromion — determines the mechanical restraint environment of the glenohumeral joint. Different scapular morphology, different downstream pathology. Same upstream structure.

### Posterior Glenoid Wear: The Walch B0 Hypothesis

The most speculative — but perhaps most intellectually compelling — extension of this framework concerns the Walch B0 glenoid, described by Domos et al. as pre-arthritic posterior subluxation of the humeral head that inevitably progresses to eccentric posterior glenoid erosion (B1, B2).^20^

Bryce et al. demonstrated that scapular rotation changes measured glenoid version by up to 22.5°, with 0.42° of relative anteversion per degree of scapular abduction in the coronal plane.^21^ This was framed as a CT measurement artifact. We propose an alternative interpretation: if scapular position dynamically changes effective glenoid version during functional movement, then a scapula that chronically sits in more internal rotation — from habitual posture, pectoralis minor tightness, thoracic kyphosis, or individual morphological variation — presents a functionally more retroverted glenoid during loading.

Akgun et al. recently provided support for this concept, showing that patients with constitutional posterior humeral decentering (the C1 phenotype, analogous to the B0 glenoid) have significantly different scapulothoracic orientation compared to healthy controls — including less scapular upward rotation and less scapular tilt — along with different posterior acromial morphology and muscle volume distribution.^6^

We cannot yet establish causation. The scapulothoracic orientation differences Akgun observed could be a consequence of the posterior decentering rather than its cause. But the geometry is clear: scapulothoracic position changes effective glenoid version (Bryce), and patients with posterior decentering have different scapulothoracic position (Akgun). Whether chronic dynamic functional retroversion from scapulothoracic malposition initiates the posterior wear cascade that Domos described is a testable question that has not been asked.

### Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty

When a reverse shoulder prosthesis is implanted, the biomechanics of the glenohumeral joint change fundamentally. Walker et al. measured scapulohumeral rhythm using fluoroscopy with 3D model-image registration across three different implant designs and found that SHR in rTSA is 1.3:1, compared to 3:1 in normal shoulders.^7^ The scapulothoracic system contributes approximately half of arm elevation after rTSA — dramatically more than in a native shoulder.

Kawashima et al. demonstrated the functional consequence: in semi-inlay rTSA, patients with SHR ≥2 had significantly higher ASES scores (94.3 ±4.6) than those with SHR <2 (82.1 ±9.4, p=0.007), with a significant positive correlation between SHR and outcomes (r=0.452, p=0.045).^8^ Better scapulothoracic function produces better arthroplasty outcomes.

Moroder et al. showed that scapulothoracic orientation directly influences optimal humeral component retrotorsion in rTSA, with scapular internal rotation correlating with the correction angle at R=0.91 when adjusted for glenoid retroversion.^22^ Shekhbihi et al. framed this as "the scapulothoracic conundrum," calling for integration of scapulothoracic assessment into rTSA planning and rehabilitation.^23^

The framework interpretation: rTSA replaces the glenohumeral force couple — a local tension-compression balance between the rotator cuff and deltoid — with a fixed geometric constraint. The scapulothoracic articulation, which remains a tension-dependent system, must now compensate. Patients whose scapulothoracic tension network can accommodate the increased demand do well. Those whose network is compromised — from years of deconditioning, chronic pain-related inhibition, or structural muscle remodeling — plateau.

This raises a clinically important question: does persistent scapular dysfunction limit arthroplasty outcomes? Mercurio et al. prospectively demonstrated that scapular dyskinesis persists after successful arthroscopic rotator cuff repair despite improved range of motion and Constant scores — scapulohumeral rhythm showed no significant change (p>0.05).^9^ Gibbons et al. showed that massive rotator cuff tears produce sarcomere loss, fiber shortening, and in massive tears, sarcomere length below the optimal operating range — architectural changes that may be irreversible even with successful tendon repair.^24^ If similar remodeling occurs in the periscapular muscles of patients with chronic shoulder dysfunction — which is biologically plausible but unproven — then the scapulothoracic system may be structurally committed to dysfunction before surgery is even performed.

This provides the strongest argument for scapulothoracic prehabilitation before shoulder surgery: not to "strengthen muscles" in the conventional sense, but to restore sarcomere architecture and pre-stress distribution while remodeling remains possible, before imposing the new biomechanical demands of a prosthesis.

## The Unifying Principle

The four examples above involve four different pathologies, four different patient populations, and four different downstream structures failing. But in every case, the scapula is upstream. In every case, a change in the scapula's morphology or position changes force distribution to a downstream structure. In every case, the specific pathology that develops depends on which downstream structure bears the consequence.

This pattern is consistent with a structural principle that has been extensively validated at the cellular level: tensegrity. Tensegrity (tensional integrity) describes a system where compression elements float within a continuous tension network, and stability emerges from the balance of tension rather than from compressive stacking.^25-27^ Ingber and colleagues demonstrated that living cells use tensegrity architecture — microtubules as compression elements, actin filaments as the tension network — and that the cell's shape, stiffness, and mechanotransduction responses are governed by the pre-stress in this network.^25-27^ Chen and Ingber extended this principle from cytoskeleton to skeleton, arguing that the musculoskeletal system uses the same organizational logic: bones as compression elements, muscles and fascia as the continuous tension network.^28^

The scapulothoracic articulation may be the clearest musculoskeletal example of this architecture. The scapula is a compression element with no bony attachment to the axial skeleton, suspended entirely within a continuous tension network of periscapular musculature. Its position is a pure summation of the tension vectors in that network. The clinical observations presented in this paper are consistent with tensegrity predictions: pre-stress in the tension network determines system stiffness and function (Kebaetse^10^), alteration of the compression element's geometry changes force distribution throughout the network (CSA data^1-3,14-16^, posterior acromial morphology^4,5^), local perturbation produces global reorganization (Borstad^11^, Burkhart^29^), and the system self-stabilizes at a new equilibrium after perturbation (Mercurio^9^).

We propose this not as a theoretical assertion, but as a biomechanical framework that organizes existing clinical evidence and generates testable predictions. The framework does not replace existing understanding of individual pathologies — it contextualizes them within a system where the scapula is the common upstream variable.

## Clinical Implications and Testable Predictions

This framework has immediate clinical implications and generates specific predictions suitable for prospective investigation:

**Measure the CSA on every pre-operative shoulder radiograph.** CSA measurement takes 30 seconds, has excellent inter- and intra-observer reliability,^1^ and may change surgical planning. For cuff repair with CSA >35°, arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty should be considered as an adjunct based on current evidence.^16^

**Assess scapulothoracic function in every shoulder patient.** The scapular assistance test — manual repositioning of the scapula into posterior tilt and upward rotation — changes subacromial space and can be performed in 10 seconds.^30^ If repositioning the scapula reduces symptoms, the scapulothoracic system is contributing to the pathology.

**Prediction: Pre-operative scapulothoracic function should predict surgical outcomes.** For both cuff repair and rTSA, prospective assessment of scapulothoracic kinematics and periscapular muscle function should correlate with post-operative outcomes independently of the surgical variables. This has not been studied.

**Prediction: Scapulothoracic prehabilitation before shoulder arthroplasty should improve outcomes.** If scapulothoracic pre-stress determines the system's ability to accommodate the biomechanical demands of a prosthesis, then restoring pre-stress before surgery — while sarcomere remodeling is still possible — should produce better outcomes than rehabilitation initiated after surgery. A randomized pilot comparing standard perioperative rehabilitation to a protocol that includes 6-12 weeks of scapulothoracic prehabilitation is warranted.

**Prediction: Dynamic glenoid version measurement would identify patients at risk for posterior glenoid wear.** Biplanar fluoroscopy during functional tasks could measure effective glenoid version as a function of scapulothoracic position, revealing whether patients with the B0 phenotype have greater dynamic functional retroversion than their static CT measurements suggest. This could enable early identification and potentially early intervention before irreversible glenoid erosion develops.

## Limitations

Several limitations of this framework must be acknowledged. First, the tensegrity model at the musculoskeletal level remains qualitative. While the predictions match clinical observations, quantitative computational modeling of the scapulothoracic system as a tensegrity structure has not been performed. Second, the B0 hypothesis is largely speculative — causation between scapulothoracic orientation and posterior glenoid wear has not been established. Third, the scapulothoracic system is actively controlled by the nervous system, introducing a level of complexity that static tensegrity models cannot capture. Fourth, not all evidence supporting the individual components of this framework is consistent — the CSA-rotator cuff tear association, in particular, has not been replicated in all studies.^18^

Despite these limitations, the convergence of evidence from morphological, kinematic, arthroplasty, and cellular biology literature on the scapula as a common upstream factor is difficult to dismiss. The predictions generated by this framework are specific and testable, and their investigation has the potential to improve clinical decision-making across the spectrum of shoulder pathology.

## Conclusion

The scapulothoracic articulation is a compression element suspended in a continuous tension network. Its morphology determines force distribution to the rotator cuff and the glenohumeral joint. Its position determines effective glenoid orientation and subacromial space. When we surgically alter the glenohumeral joint, the tension demands on this system increase. The specific downstream pathology that develops in a given patient — cuff disease, posterior instability, glenoid wear, or suboptimal arthroplasty outcome — depends on which downstream structure bears the consequence of the specific alteration in scapulothoracic force distribution.

This is a single biomechanical reality producing diverse clinical manifestations. The framework we propose — rooted in established clinical evidence and consistent with the structural principle of tensegrity — unifies observations that currently reside in separate literature silos and generates predictions that, if validated, could change how we assess, plan, and rehabilitate across the full spectrum of shoulder pathology.
