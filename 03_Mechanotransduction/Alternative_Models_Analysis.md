# Alternative Models Analysis

*Steelmanning competing explanations. If the mechanical adaptation model is right, it should survive comparison with the best alternatives.*
*2026-03-25*

---

## Observations Any Model Must Explain

1. CSA correlates with disease type (AUC 0.92)
2. OA and cuff tears are nearly mutually exclusive
3. Dose-response: bigger angle, bigger tear
4. CSA is congenital and stable
5. Tissue changes show zero inflammation
6. Degeneration worst on articular side
7. Tears equal in dominant/non-dominant arms
8. Not more common in laborers
9. Age is strongest single risk factor
10. Genetic heritability exists (18%, siblings 2.85x)
11. Gerber's traction reverses architectural changes

---

## Five Alternative Models

### 1. Genetic Programming Model
CSA is a visible biomarker of a genetic program that also determines tissue quality. Same genes → scapular shape AND tendon/cartilage properties.
- Strongest competitor. Explains correlation, heritability, bilateral symmetry.
- Cannot explain: regional patterning (articular > bursal), dose-response for tear size, mechanical reversibility with traction.
- Distinguishing test: Does changing CSA (lateral acromioplasty) change outcomes? If yes → mechanical causation. If no → genetic marker.

### 2. Vascular Watershed Model
Hypovascularity in supraspinatus critical zone drives ischemic degeneration. Morphology affects vascular anatomy, not force.
- Explains articular-side predominance and zero inflammation well.
- Cannot explain: CSA-disease TYPE correlation, mutual exclusivity, mechanical reversibility.
- Distinguishing test: Perfusion imaging (CE-US or MRI) in high vs low CSA shoulders.

### 3. Senescence Model
Aging drives everything. CSA determines which tissue is closest to the age-related cliff.
- Explains age dependence perfectly.
- Too simple: can't explain AUC of 0.92 (too clean for age + noise), dose-response, or traction reversibility.
- Distinguishing test: Senescence markers (p16, telomere length) articular vs bursal side.

### 4. Neural/Proprioceptive Model
Neuromuscular dysfunction is the primary cause. Scapular malposition from poor motor control drives abnormal loading.
- Explains why PT works and why dyskinesis is universal in shoulder pathology.
- Cannot explain: fixed bony CSA correlating with disease (CSA is morphology, not kinematics).
- Distinguishing test: Does normalizing kinematics eliminate the CSA-disease correlation?

### 5. Epigenetic Drift Model
Stochastic epigenetic accumulation drives tissue-specific degeneration over time.
- Explains age dependence and stochastic variation.
- Cannot explain: AUC 0.92 (too clean for stochastic process), mechanical reversibility.

## Most Likely Truth: The Integrated Model

Genetics loads the gun. Morphology aims it. Cumulative loading pulls the trigger.

Genetic programming determines:
- Tissue susceptibility (collagen quality, vascular density, cellular repair capacity)
- Morphology (CSA, PAH, glenoid version)
- Mechanostat thresholds (set points are genetically encoded per Frost)

Morphology determines:
- Force distribution (which tissue receives what type and magnitude of load)

Force determines:
- Which genetically susceptible tissue crosses its threshold first
- The pattern and location of adaptation within each tissue

Time determines:
- Whether cumulative dose reaches the threshold during the patient's lifetime

## The Deeper Integration: The Genetics-Mechanics Loop

Neither genetics nor mechanics is upstream of the other. They are reciprocal processes operating on different timescales.

**Over evolutionary time:** Mechanics shaped genetics. Natural selection favored organisms that could detect and respond to mechanical forces. This produced integrins, Piezo channels, Rho/ROCK pathways, the entire mechanotransduction toolkit.

**Over developmental time:** Genetics shapes mechanics. Genes build morphology (CSA, PAH, glenoid version). Morphology determines force distribution. Mammoto showed mechanical forces regulate gene expression at every stage of embryogenesis.

**Over a lifetime:** Mechanics shapes gene expression. Engler showed substrate stiffness alone determines stem cell lineage. After 3 weeks, the commitment is irreversible. The mechanical environment instructs which genetic programs run, in which cells, continuously.

**Over a disease course:** Gene expression shapes mechanics. Tissue remodeling (matrix changes, fatty infiltration, bone remodeling) changes the local force environment. Changed forces change gene expression further. The loop either maintains homeostasis or spirals.

### The Integrated Model

The genome builds the morphology. The morphology determines the force distribution. The forces regulate gene expression in every cell continuously. When the force environment drives gene expression past tissue-specific thresholds, the resulting program produces what clinicians recognize as disease.

The tissue is executing the gene expression program that its mechanical environment has selected. The program was optimized by evolution for the ancestral mechanical environment. It may be maladaptive in the modern one.

### Intervention Points in the Loop

- **Genetics:** Cannot change (yet)
- **Fixed morphology:** Cannot change without surgery (CSA, PAH, version)
- **Dynamic force environment:** CAN change (scapulothoracic function, posture, conditioning, load management)
- **Threshold modulators:** CAN change (metabolic health, smoking, diabetes management)
- **Surgical boundary correction:** Can change morphology when disease warrants (lateral acromioplasty, SCOPE, HTO, realignment osteotomies)

The framework is not "mechanical model vs genetic model." It is a model of the mechanics-genetics feedback loop applied to musculoskeletal disease. Genetics loads the gun. Morphology aims it. Cumulative loading pulls the trigger. The question is whether we can keep the safety on by managing the modifiable parts of the loop.
