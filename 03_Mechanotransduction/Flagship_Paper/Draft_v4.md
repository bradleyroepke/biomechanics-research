# The Mechanical Environment and Shoulder Disease: How Scapular Morphology Determines Tissue Fate

---

## Abstract

Glenohumeral osteoarthritis and rotator cuff tears are the two most common degenerative conditions of the shoulder, yet they rarely coexist in the same joint. No mechanistic explanation for this mutual exclusivity has been proposed. We argue that both conditions represent predictable tissue responses to the force environment created by scapular morphology. The critical shoulder angle, a congenital and stable radiographic measure combining glenoid inclination and lateral acromial extension, determines the ratio of compressive to shear force at the glenohumeral joint. A low angle produces a compression-dominant environment in which articular cartilage bears supraphysiological load, driving the changes recognized as osteoarthritis. A high angle produces a shear-dominant environment in which the supraspinatus is chronically overloaded, driving the changes recognized as rotator cuff disease. Each pathway, once established, alters joint geometry in ways that reduce the likelihood of the other. We extend this reasoning to posterior acromial morphology, introduce the concept of dynamic glenoid version as a modifiable biomechanical variable, and propose six testable predictions, including the hypothesis that surgical over-correction of the critical shoulder angle below 28 degrees should paradoxically increase osteoarthritis risk. This framework is falsifiable: if correcting morphological parameters does not alter tissue adaptation trajectories in prospective study, the mechanical causation claim fails.

## 1. A Clinical Observation Without a Mechanism

Between 200,000 and 400,000 rotator cuff repairs are performed annually in the United States, at a cost exceeding $10,000 per case.¹ Despite three decades of technical evolution, from open to arthroscopic, from single-row to suture bridge, from simple suture to biologic augmentation, a systematic review of over 8,000 shoulders found "little evidence that the results of rotator cuff repair are improving."² Long-term retear rates approach 41% at 14 years.³

Gerber raised a set of questions about rotator cuff disease that current models do not answer.⁴ Why does a professional tennis player who loads his shoulder thousands of times weekly not develop a rotator cuff tear? Why are supraspinatus tears equally frequent in the dominant and non-dominant arm? Why are they not more common in manual laborers than in office workers? And why does a patient develop either a rotator cuff tear or glenohumeral osteoarthritis, but almost never both?

Moor and colleagues provided the observation that makes these questions answerable.⁵ The critical shoulder angle, measured on a standard anteroposterior radiograph from the inferior glenoid margin to the lateral acromial edge, separated rotator cuff tears from osteoarthritis with an area under the curve of 0.92. Patients with rotator cuff tears had a mean angle of 38 degrees (SD 2.8). Patients with osteoarthritis had a mean of 28 degrees (SD 3.3). Controls fell between them at 33 degrees (SD 2.1). Patients with a critical shoulder angle above 35 degrees were in the rotator cuff tear group 84% of the time. Those below 30 degrees were in the osteoarthritis group 93% of the time.

Blonna and colleagues confirmed the association and added a dose-response relationship.⁶ Isolated supraspinatus tears averaged 36 degrees. Large tears involving the supraspinatus and infraspinatus averaged 40 degrees. On multivariate analysis controlling for age, sex, smoking, BMI, and occupation, the critical shoulder angle remained the most statistically significant risk factor for rotator cuff tears (OR 1.7, 95% CI 1.4-2.0, p < 0.001). Smoking had a higher raw odds ratio (4.0) but wider confidence intervals.

Passaplan and colleagues followed 47 shoulders for a mean of twelve years and found no significant change in the critical shoulder angle over time (34 degrees at first assessment, 34 degrees at second, p = 0.597), even in the ten shoulders that developed new rotator cuff tears during follow-up.⁷ The critical shoulder angle is congenital and stable. It does not change with age or disease.

A single radiographic measurement, determined at birth and unchanging over a lifetime, predicts with high discriminatory power both the type and severity of degenerative shoulder disease. The critical shoulder angle is not likely the sole morphological parameter that matters. It is one of several, including posterior acromial height, glenoid version, greater tuberosity geometry, and scapulothoracic posture, each of which modifies the force environment the shoulder operates in. The critical shoulder angle is used throughout this paper because it is the most extensively studied and quantified of these parameters. The principle it illustrates, that skeletal morphology determines the mechanical environment and that tissue adapts to that environment, applies regardless of which specific morphological feature is measured.

The question this paper addresses is why a morphological parameter that varies continuously produces two discrete pathological conditions rather than a smooth gradient of mixed pathology.

## 2. Mechanical Adaptation as a General Principle

The answer builds on principles that are individually well established in orthopedic training. What has not been articulated is what happens when they are taken together.

Wolff recognized in 1892 that bone remodels in response to its mechanical environment.⁸ Trabeculae align with principal stress directions. Cortical bone thickens in response to habitual loading. Frost formalized this with the mechanostat, demonstrating that bone does not respond to load on a smooth continuum but instead switches between qualitatively different biological programs at specific strain thresholds: disuse-mode resorption below approximately 50-100 microstrain, adaptive maintenance up to 1,000-1,500 microstrain, active modeling and bone formation above that range, and pathological microdamage accumulation above approximately 3,000 microstrain.⁹ These are set points, not gradients. Frost recognized in his 2003 update that the same threshold-based logic likely applies to "fascia, joint, ligament, tendon, or growth plates," though the specific threshold values for non-osseous tissues remain unknown.

The Hueter-Volkmann principle demonstrates the same behavior in growth plates: sustained compression inhibits longitudinal growth while tension stimulates it, at a rate of approximately 17% per 0.1 MPa across multiple species and anatomic sites.¹⁰ This is not a failure of the growth plate. It is a calibrated response to the mechanical environment. Angular deformity progresses because asymmetric loading drives asymmetric growth, each increment of deformity increasing the asymmetry that drives the next increment.

Davis's Law describes soft tissue remodeling along lines of imposed demand. Stress shielding beneath a plate or prosthesis demonstrates that bone correctly reads a reduced mechanical signal and adjusts its mass accordingly. Articular cartilage requires cyclic loading to maintain its matrix; immobilize it and it degrades. In each case, the tissue is reading its mechanical environment and adapting its composition to match.

The cellular machinery for this process is well characterized. Integrins spanning the cell membrane physically link the extracellular matrix to the cytoskeleton and nucleus, transmitting mechanical forces to the site of gene transcription in milliseconds, faster than chemical diffusion.¹¹ Engler and colleagues demonstrated that mesenchymal stem cells plated on substrates of varying stiffness differentiate into neurons on soft substrates (0.1-1 kPa, matching brain tissue stiffness), muscle cells on intermediate substrates (8-17 kPa, matching muscle), and osteoblasts on stiff substrates (25-40 kPa, matching osteoid), using substrate stiffness alone with identical chemical conditions.¹² After three weeks of culture, the mechanically directed commitment becomes irreversible: soluble differentiation factors for alternative lineages can no longer override what the substrate stiffness has specified. Full differentiation requires both the correct mechanical environment and appropriate chemical signals acting in synergy, but the mechanical commitment precedes and constrains the chemical response.

These observations span bone, cartilage, growth plate, soft tissue, and undifferentiated stem cells. Taken together, they describe a single principle: living tissue continuously reads its mechanical environment and adapts its structure and composition accordingly. The adaptation is directional, threshold-dependent, and, within the parameters each tissue has evolved to handle, mechanically appropriate. When the mechanical environment deviates from what the tissue was designed for, the adaptation produces changes that clinicians recognize as disease.

## 3. Threshold Behavior in the Shoulder

Degenerative shoulder disease does not develop as a linear function of time or loading. A patient functions well for years, then tips into symptomatic disease. A partial rotator cuff tear is stable on serial imaging, then enlarges. These observations suggest the system has mechanical thresholds where tissue transitions between qualitatively different states.

Parsons and colleagues provided the most precise demonstration of this.¹³ Using a dynamic shoulder testing apparatus with an instrumented prosthesis, they sequentially sectioned the rotator cuff and measured glenohumeral joint reaction forces at each stage. A complete, isolated supraspinatus tear produced no significant change in force magnitude or direction compared to the intact state. The transverse force couple, subscapularis balanced against infraspinatus and teres minor, maintained humeral head centering through concavity compression.

When the sectioning extended to include the infraspinatus, disrupting the transverse force couple, the joint reaction force dropped by 61 percent (from 44 +/- 8 N/kg to 19 +/- 5 N/kg) and its elevation angle shifted by 15 degrees. Maximum abduction collapsed from 85 degrees to 41 degrees. This was not a proportional worsening along a continuum. It was an abrupt mechanical state change at a specific structural threshold: loss of the transverse force couple.

Burkhart described this clinically as the distinction between functional and dysfunctional rotator cuff tears.¹⁴ A massive tear with an intact transverse force couple maintains concavity compression and the patient retains function. A tear of identical size that disrupts the force couple produces pseudoparalysis. What changed was not the quantity of tissue lost but whether the loss crossed a specific mechanical threshold.

Gerber's work on muscle degeneration after tendon release in sheep demonstrates that the tissue changes traditionally considered irreversible are themselves threshold-dependent.¹⁵ After infraspinatus release, muscle architecture changed progressively over 16 weeks: pennation angle increased from 29 to 55 degrees as fibers shortened and reoriented, and fatty infiltration developed to Goutallier stage 2. These changes do not reverse with standard surgical repair.

However, when Gerber applied slow, continuous musculotendinous traction at one millimeter per day over several weeks, the pennation angle normalized completely (to 29.8 +/- 7.5 degrees, not significantly different from the contralateral control at 30 +/- 6 degrees, p = 0.575).¹⁵ New sarcomeres were added in series, with fibers becoming longer and thinner as contractile protein was added longitudinally rather than in cross-section. Fatty infiltration did not reverse but its progression was arrested (Goutallier stage 1 in the traction group versus stage 2 in the failed-traction group, p = 0.016). The muscle fibers showed no degeneration on histology or electron microscopy; they were viable, innervated, and electrically excitable. The architectural changes had been the tissue's response to an absence of longitudinal tension, not irreversible degeneration. Restore the tension at the appropriate rate, and the architecture restored itself.

This finding has a specific implication for the argument that follows. If muscle architecture after chronic cuff tear represents a mechanical adaptation rather than permanent damage, then the tissue changes seen across the spectrum of shoulder disease may similarly represent adaptations to specific mechanical environments rather than irreversible pathological processes.

## 4. Two Pathways from One Parameter

The critical shoulder angle integrates two features of scapular morphology: glenoid inclination and lateral acromial extension. Beeler and colleagues decomposed it and found that the lateral acromial roof extension had the largest effect size (Cohen's d = 1.36 on radiograph) for distinguishing rotator cuff tears from osteoarthritis, greater than glenoid inclination (d = 0.60).¹⁶ Together, these features determine the line of action of the middle deltoid relative to the glenohumeral joint center.

In a shoulder with a low critical shoulder angle, a short acromion and less-inclined glenoid direct the deltoid force vector more into the glenoid face. The deltoid and rotator cuff act synergistically in compression. Concavity compression is high. The articular cartilage bears a chronically elevated compressive load.

In a shoulder with a high critical shoulder angle, a long lateral acromion and more-inclined glenoid direct the deltoid vector more superiorly. The shearing component along the glenoid face increases. The supraspinatus must generate more force to counteract this shear and maintain joint stability through concavity compression.

Viehofer and colleagues quantified this relationship on a robotic shoulder simulator.¹⁷ Reducing the critical shoulder angle by five degrees (from 33 to 28 degrees, into the osteoarthritis-associated range) increased the glenohumeral joint reaction force by a maximum of 8.5 percent at 55 degrees of abduction, with the compression-to-shear ratio averaging approximately 1.8:1 and shifting predictably with the angle. In a complementary study using a three-dimensional finite element model with 27 muscle elements, the same group showed that a critical shoulder angle of 38 degrees (rotator cuff tear range) required up to 33 percent more supraspinatus force to maintain joint stability compared to a normal angle of 33 degrees, with effects most pronounced between 30 and 55 degrees of abduction, the range of daily activities.¹⁸

These biomechanical data provide the mechanism linking one specific morphological parameter to tissue loading. The critical shoulder angle is not the only variable in this system. Glenoid version, acromial morphology in the sagittal plane, humeral head geometry, and the dynamic positioning of the scapula on the thorax all contribute to the total force environment the glenohumeral joint experiences. A patient's disease trajectory is determined by the composite effect of these parameters acting together over a lifetime, modulated by tissue susceptibility, activity level, and systemic health. The model that follows uses the critical shoulder angle as the primary variable because it is the most studied, but the logic applies to any morphological feature that alters the mechanical environment of the joint.

**The compression pathway.** A chronically compression-dominant environment loads articular cartilage beyond its maintenance threshold. Chondrocytes respond by altering matrix composition. Cartilage thins. Subchondral bone, receiving increased compressive load, stiffens and scleroses, the mechanostat responding as Wolff's Law predicts. The joint medializes as the articular surface remodels toward a new geometric equilibrium under the altered load. Each tissue change is the expected response to the mechanical signal that tissue receives. Together, they constitute glenohumeral osteoarthritis.

**The shear pathway.** A chronically shear-dominant environment overloads the supraspinatus, the smallest and most mechanically vulnerable of the rotator cuff muscles, with a work capacity of 4.0 compared to 15.1 for the subscapularis.¹⁹ The fiber length is the shortest (4.5 cm) and the sarcomere resting length the longest (3.23 micrometers) of any cuff muscle, placing it on the descending limb of its length-tension curve at rest and making it exquisitely sensitive to perturbation.

Hashimoto's histological study of 80 torn rotator cuff stumps found degeneration in 100% of specimens and inflammation in 0%.²⁰ The degenerative changes, including collagen disorientation, myxoid degeneration, and chondroid metaplasia, were significantly more pronounced in the deep (articular) layer than in the superficial (bursal) layer (p < 0.01). If extrinsic impingement from the acromion above were the primary mechanism, the bursal surface should show the worst changes. It does not. The pattern is consistent with intrinsic stress concentration on the articular surface, where the tendon is compressed against the humeral head by the very forces it generates to resist superior shear.

As cumulative loading exceeds the tissue's repair capacity, partial tearing begins at the site of maximal stress concentration, consistently 13-17 mm posterior to the biceps tendon at the supraspinatus-infraspinatus junction.²¹ When the tear propagates to disrupt the transverse force couple, the system crosses the threshold Parsons and Burkhart described: superior migration, accelerated overload, and progressive failure. Fatty infiltration of the detached muscle follows, reaching moderate levels within approximately four years and severe levels by six years.²²

**Why the two pathways rarely coexist.** Once the osteoarthritis pathway is established, medialization of the joint reduces the effective moment arm of the deltoid, which decreases the superior shear component that would otherwise overload the cuff. The rotator cuff is spared not by a protective mechanism but by a geometric consequence of the disease process itself. Once the rotator cuff tear pathway is established, superior migration of the humeral head shifts the contact pattern away from the central articular surface, reducing the compressive loading that drives cartilage degeneration (until the late stage of cuff tear arthropathy, which represents a subsequent threshold crossing).

This is not mutual protection. It is pathway selection by a shared morphological parameter. The critical shoulder angle determines which tissue's mechanical threshold is reached first. The tissue changes that follow alter the joint geometry in ways that move the other tissue further from its threshold. The two conditions diverge because the first pathway's consequences are geometrically incompatible with the second pathway's prerequisites.

Mantell and colleagues provide indirect evidence for this model.²³ In patients who had both osteoarthritis and a full-thickness rotator cuff tear, a relatively uncommon combination, the mean critical shoulder angle was 35 degrees, falling in the zone between the typical osteoarthritis and cuff tear ranges. Nearly half of the concurrent tears occurred at critical shoulder angles below 35 degrees. This is consistent with a boundary region where both pathways are accessible and factors beyond the critical shoulder angle, including tissue susceptibility, activity level, scapulothoracic function, and systemic health, determine which threshold is crossed first.

**Engaging the counterevidence.** The association between the critical shoulder angle and osteoarthritis is robust and replicated across every study that has examined it, including those that challenge the cuff tear association. The association between the critical shoulder angle and rotator cuff tears is supported by the majority of studies but is not universal. Bjarnison and colleagues, in a methodologically rigorous case-control study with 3:1 matched controls, found no significant association between the critical shoulder angle and rotator cuff tears (OR 1.12, 95% CI 0.70-1.81, p = 0.63).²⁴ Their cuff tear group mean was 33.9 degrees, essentially identical to controls at 33.6 degrees.

This finding cannot be dismissed. However, it should be interpreted in context. The study could not stratify by tear size; Blonna's data demonstrate the association is strongest for large tears.⁶ The malrotation threshold for radiograph inclusion was more liberal than in other studies (11 mm glenoid overlap versus 5 mm), potentially introducing measurement noise. Case identification relied on ICD-10 diagnostic codes rather than imaging confirmation. A meta-analysis of the CSA-cuff tear literature found significant heterogeneity (I² = 93%), with the mean difference of approximately 3.3 degrees trending larger in smaller, higher-risk-of-bias studies.²⁵ This pattern suggests a real but potentially modest effect that is susceptible to methodological variation.

The measurement itself presents a practical challenge. A five-degree scapular positioning error changes the measured critical shoulder angle by more than two degrees, and up to 76% of clinical radiographs do not meet established quality standards for reliable measurement.²⁶ The differences between disease groups (typically 3-5 degrees) overlap with the range of measurement error from patient positioning. The biomechanical logic is not in question: the critical shoulder angle determines the force vector, and five degrees of change produces a measurable 8.5% change in joint reaction force.¹⁷ What is in question is whether the clinical measurement tool has sufficient precision to apply this logic at the individual patient level.

Additionally, genetic factors contribute independently to rotator cuff disease risk. Twin studies estimate heritability at approximately 18%, with shared environment contributing 44% and unique environment 38%.²⁷ Siblings of patients with rotator cuff tears have a 2.85 times higher risk of developing tears themselves. Specific genetic variants in collagen, matrix metalloproteinase, and growth factor genes have been identified. The critical shoulder angle may in part reflect a genetic program that also determines intrinsic tissue properties, making it difficult to separate the morphological effect on force distribution from the genetic effect on tissue susceptibility without interventional data.

The biomechanical relationship between the critical shoulder angle and glenohumeral force distribution is established experimentally.¹⁷ ¹⁸ The clinical association between the angle and disease type is strong for osteoarthritis and present but contested for rotator cuff tears. The causal claim, that the angle drives disease through force distribution rather than serving as a genetic marker, can only be resolved by prospective interventional study.

## 5. Beyond the Critical Shoulder Angle

If scapular morphology determines glenohumeral force distribution in the coronal plane through the critical shoulder angle, the same logic should apply through other morphological parameters in other planes. Two extensions are supported by current data.

**Posterior acromial morphology and instability.** Meyer and colleagues found that the posterior acromial height in patients with posterior shoulder instability was 30.9 mm compared to 19.5 mm in patients with anterior instability, with an odds ratio of 39 for posterior instability when the height exceeded 23 mm (95% CI 10-155, p < 0.001).²⁸ A high, flat posterior acromion provides less bony coverage of the posterolateral humeral head, reducing the passive restraint against posterior translation.

Hochreiter and colleagues confirmed this biomechanically using three-dimensional printed scapula models derived from patient-specific CT data.²⁹ A high, flat acromial configuration reduced the force required for posterior humeral displacement by 23-60% compared to normal acromial anatomy. Correction of acromial alignment increased posterior stability by up to 122%. Glenoid version correction alone was insufficient to restore normal stability; only combined correction of both glenoid and acromial morphology approximated normal.

Gerber applied this clinically with a combined glenoid and acromial osteotomy (the SCOPE procedure), planned using three-dimensional statistical shape models of normal scapular anatomy.³⁰ In nine consecutive patients, six of whom had failed prior soft-tissue stabilization procedures, combined scapular correction improved the subjective shoulder value from a median of 40% to 87.5%. Eight of nine shoulders were recentered, and no progressive osteoarthritis was observed at a mean follow-up of 29 months. The scapular morphology, not the soft tissue, had been the primary problem.

The critical shoulder angle and posterior acromial height together define a two-dimensional morphological parameter space. Different regions of this space predict different pathological endpoints: rotator cuff tears, osteoarthritis, or posterior instability. These parameters are determined at birth and do not change over time.

**Scapulothoracic posture: a modifiable parameter.** The critical shoulder angle and posterior acromial height are fixed features of the scapular skeleton. They cannot be changed without surgery. However, one parameter that modifies the effective force environment can be changed: scapulothoracic posture.

Bryce and colleagues demonstrated that scapular rotation changes the two-dimensionally measured glenoid version by up to 22.5 degrees, with every degree of scapular abduction producing 0.42 degrees of relative anteversion and every degree of internal rotation producing 0.2 degrees of version change.³¹ Their study framed this as a source of CT measurement error. It is also a functional biomechanical reality. A scapula that chronically sits in more internal rotation or less posterior tilt presents a functionally more retroverted glenoid during loading.

Akgun and colleagues found that patients with constitutional posterior shoulder instability had significantly different scapulothoracic orientation compared to healthy controls: less scapular upward rotation (9.6 versus 12.2 degrees, p = 0.03), markedly less scapular tilt (7.9 versus 16.7 degrees, p < 0.001), and less thoracic kyphosis (24.9 versus 34.7 degrees, p = 0.005).³²

We propose the term dynamic glenoid version to describe the effective version of the glenoid as determined by the scapula's position on the thorax during loading. This concept does not appear in the existing literature under this or equivalent terminology. Unlike the critical shoulder angle or posterior acromial height, dynamic glenoid version is modifiable through scapulothoracic rehabilitation: strengthening of the serratus anterior and lower trapezius, mobilization of the thoracic spine, and correction of pectoralis minor tightness can change how the scapula presents the glenoid to the humeral head.

After reverse total shoulder arthroplasty, scapulothoracic function becomes the dominant kinematic variable. Walker and colleagues showed the scapulohumeral rhythm drops to 1.3:1 compared to the normal 3:1, meaning the patient depends far more on scapulothoracic motion for arm elevation.³³ Kawashima and colleagues found that patients with a scapulohumeral rhythm of 2 or greater had ASES scores of 94.3 compared to 82.1 for those below 2 (p = 0.007), exceeding the minimal clinically important difference.³⁴ Mercurio and colleagues demonstrated that scapular dyskinesis persists at 12 months after successful rotator cuff repair, with no significant change in scapulohumeral rhythm despite improvements in range of motion and Constant score.³⁵

No current clinical protocol screens for scapulothoracic function before shoulder arthroplasty or targets it specifically in post-arthroplasty rehabilitation.

## 6. Predictions

This framework generates predictions that differ from those of current models. Each is testable with existing or feasible study designs. The framework's value depends on whether these predictions are confirmed or refuted.

**Prediction 1.** Patients with concurrent glenohumeral osteoarthritis and rotator cuff tears should have critical shoulder angles concentrated in the 30-35 degree boundary zone. Isolated osteoarthritis should cluster below 30 degrees and isolated rotator cuff tears above 35 degrees. A retrospective radiographic study of 500 or more shoulders classified by pathology type can test this.

**Prediction 2.** Rotator cuff repair combined with lateral acromioplasty reducing the critical shoulder angle to 33 degrees or below should demonstrate lower structural retear rates than repair alone at long-term follow-up.

**Prediction 3.** Surgical reduction of the critical shoulder angle well below 28 degrees should increase the long-term risk of glenohumeral osteoarthritis. No current clinical model generates this prediction. A retrospective review of patients who have undergone lateral acromioplasty, stratified by post-operative critical shoulder angle with 10 or more years of follow-up, can test it.

**Prediction 4.** Baseline scapulothoracic posture should predict progression from Walch B0 (posterior subluxation without erosion) to B1/B2 glenoid morphology on serial imaging over time.

**Prediction 5.** Pre-operative scapulothoracic kinematics should independently predict functional outcomes after reverse total shoulder arthroplasty, after controlling for implant type, pre-operative diagnosis, and tear pattern.

**Prediction 6.** If the same mechanical logic applies beyond the shoulder, rehabilitation protocols that reduce compressive loading on the ECRB against the capitellum should outperform standard eccentric loading protocols for lateral epicondylitis.

**Falsification criterion.** If prospective study demonstrates that correcting morphological parameters in patients with high-risk scapular anatomy does not alter disease development or tissue adaptation trajectories, the mechanical causation claim at the center of this framework fails.

## 7. Limitations and Boundaries

This paper presents a framework for thinking about degenerative shoulder disease, not proof of mechanism. Several limitations require explicit statement.

The competing pathway model for osteoarthritis and rotator cuff tear mutual exclusivity is a mechanistic hypothesis. It is consistent with the available biomechanical and clinical data but has not been directly tested. The mutual exclusivity pattern itself might reflect ascertainment bias: osteoarthritis patients are older and less likely to present with symptomatic cuff tears, while cuff tear patients are younger and have not yet developed radiographic osteoarthritis. Examination of asymptomatic rotator cuff tear prevalence in total shoulder arthroplasty populations and radiographic osteoarthritis prevalence in cuff repair populations would help distinguish genuine biological competition from selection artifact.

Garcia's widely cited odds ratio of 14.8 for retear with a critical shoulder angle above 38 degrees is based on eight full-thickness retears.³⁶ It should be treated as hypothesis-generating, not as a robust effect estimate.

No controlled trial has demonstrated that surgically reducing the critical shoulder angle improves patient-reported or structural outcomes compared to repair alone. Gerber's lateral acromioplasty data are consistent with this hypothesis (post-operative angle of 33 degrees in healed repairs versus 36 degrees in failures, p = 0.026) but represent a phase 1 safety study without a comparison group.³⁷

The framework applies to the subset of shoulder conditions where the mechanical environment is the plausible primary driver: degenerative rotator cuff disease, primary glenohumeral osteoarthritis, and instability associated with morphological variants. It does not apply to acute trauma, inflammatory arthritis, infection, or conditions with primary genetic, vascular, or metabolic etiologies. In inflammatory conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis, the mechanical environment may influence the pattern and distribution of joint destruction but is not the primary pathological mechanism.

The claim that tissue changes in degenerative shoulder disease represent mechanically determined responses rather than random degeneration is supported by the regional specificity of changes (articular-side predominance in cuff disease, subchondral sclerosis along loading axes in osteoarthritis) and by the demonstration that restoring mechanical signals can reverse architectural changes in animal models.¹⁵ However, framing every tissue change as an appropriate response to mechanical input risks making the framework unfalsifiable. Genuinely maladaptive responses to mechanical loading exist and should be acknowledged.

The observations and data cited in this paper belong to the investigators who generated them. What is offered here is a synthesis: a way of seeing the work of Gerber, Moor, Frost, Burkhart, Hashimoto, Engler, and many others as related expressions of a single principle rather than as independent findings.

---

## 8. A Different Question

This paper began with a clinical frustration: rotator cuff repairs that fail despite improving technique. It ends with a reframe.

The critical shoulder angle is congenital, stable, and measurable. It determines the ratio of compression to shear at the glenohumeral joint through its effect on the deltoid force vector. Tissue adapts to whichever force dominates. When the cumulative adaptation crosses a mechanical threshold, the result is what clinicians diagnose as disease. Low angles drive compression and osteoarthritis. High angles drive shear and rotator cuff disease. The two conditions rarely coexist because the geometric consequences of each pathway move the joint away from the conditions required for the other.

If the force environment drives the tissue response, then repairing tissue without addressing the force environment treats the downstream effect while the upstream cause persists. Assessing and potentially correcting the critical shoulder angle at the time of rotator cuff repair, optimizing scapulothoracic function to modulate the dynamic force environment, and measuring scapulothoracic kinematics before reverse shoulder arthroplasty are clinical applications that follow from this reasoning. Each can be tested prospectively.

The tissue is not failing. It is responding to the mechanical environment that scapular morphology has created. The clinical question worth asking is not only why the tissue changed, but what mechanical information it received that prompted the change.
