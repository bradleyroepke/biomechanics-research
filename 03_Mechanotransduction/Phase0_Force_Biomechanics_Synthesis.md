## Key Quantitative Data for the Flagship Paper

### GH Joint Reaction Forces
- Peak magnitude: 0.89× body weight at 90° abduction (Poppen 1978), up to 2.4× BW during rehab (Goetti 2021)
- Direction: nearly perpendicular to glenoid (80-91°) in midrange, shifting superiorly at 0-30°
- ~300-400 N at 80° thoracohumeral abduction (Viehofer 2016)

### Shear-to-Compression Ratio
- Average compression-to-shear: ~1.8:1, modulated by CSA and glenoid tilt (Viehofer 2016)
- 5° CSA change (33→28°) = 8.5% increase in joint reaction force (Viehofer 2016)
- Without supraspinatus: shear component rises from ~10% to ~40% of total (Dvir & Berme 1978)
- Shear vulnerability zone: 0-30° abduction (deltoid vector primarily superior)

### Tipping Points / Thresholds (CRITICAL for Chapter 3)
- **Parsons 2002 (THE threshold paper):** Isolated complete SS tear → no significant force change. SS+IS tear → 61% force drop + 15° directional shift. THIS IS THE TIPPING POINT.
- **Burkhart 1994:** Transverse force couple disruption = threshold between functional massive tear and pseudoparalysis
- **Lippitt & Matsen 1993:** Stability ratio (shear/compression for subluxation) = 0.56-0.64 depending on direction. Labrum removal → 20% reduction.
- **Metcalf 1999:** 20-25% glenoid bone loss = posterior stability failure threshold
- **Nyffeler 2004:** 5-10mm COR elevation can convert abductor → adductor (phase transition)

### Morphology Determines Force
- **Saha 1973:** Glenoid inclination >15° superiorly predisposes to superior subluxation. First to quantify glenoid inclination as shear-to-compression determinant. Anticipated CSA concept by decades.
- **Lippitt & Matsen 1993:** Stability ratio = function of concavity depth. Linear with compression. Glenoid depth ~5mm (with labrum).
- **Buchler & Farron 2002 (FEA):** 10-15° increased retroversion → peak contact stress shifts from 2.5-3.5 MPa to 5-6 MPa posteriorly. Self-reinforcing cycle: posterior loading → more erosion → more posterior loading.
- **Boileau 2005 (Grammont):** 19mm COR medialization converts deltoid shear to compression, recruits additional deltoid segments, increases lever arm 42%
- **Hochreiter 2024:** Acromial morphology changes posterior stability force by 23-122%. High flat acromion = 52N for displacement vs 130N for normal. Acromion is a posterior buttress, not just an impingement structure.

### Tissue Adaptation Evidence
- **Carpenter 2005:** "Pathologic" biceps tendons maintain normal material properties (tensile strength 303 vs 305N). Gross changes are adaptive, not degenerative.
- **Ward 2006:** RC muscles have resting sarcomere lengths on descending limb of length-tension curve → generate passive stabilizing tension at rest. Architecture = morphology determining force.
- **Soslowsky 1997:** Internal impingement is normal contact at end-range, not pathology.

### Novel Insights for Framework
1. **Saha 1973** was first to quantify the shear-to-compression concept — 40 years before Moor's CSA
2. **Parsons 2002** provides the most quantitative threshold demonstration: continuous tear size produces discontinuous force change at the SS→SS/IS boundary
3. **Nyffeler 2004** 5mm COR shift converting abductor to adductor = the clearest example of a biomechanical phase transition
4. **Buchler 2002 FEA** self-reinforcing positive feedback (version→loading→erosion→version) is the clearest demonstration of the framework's adaptive cycle
5. **Hochreiter 2024** reframes the acromion from purely negative (impingement) to positive (posterior stabilizer) — supports framework's challenge to simplistic pathology labels
