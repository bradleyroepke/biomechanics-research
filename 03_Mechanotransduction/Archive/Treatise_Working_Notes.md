## Core Argument (Reformulated After Bayesian Assessment)

### The claim, stated precisely:

1. Living tissues are dissipative structures maintained far from thermodynamic equilibrium by continuous energy throughput (metabolism, mechanical loading).

2. Mechanical forces constitute a necessary (though not sole) information channel through which cells regulate their behavior, including differentiation, matrix production, and remodeling.

3. The mechanical environment — determined by morphological parameters like CSA, posterior acromial morphology, and scapulothoracic posture — sets the boundary conditions of this dissipative system.

4. When morphological parameters drive the mechanical environment past critical thresholds (bifurcation points), tissues transition to qualitatively different but internally consistent stable states.

5. What clinicians call "pathology" (OA, cuff tears, tendinopathy) often represents these alternative stable states — correct cellular responses to the mechanical information they receive.

6. The specific morphological starting conditions determine which bifurcation branch the system takes, explaining why certain pathologies are associated with certain morphotypes and why some pathologies appear "mutually protective."

### What this is NOT claiming:

- That mechanical forces override chemical/genetic signaling (they don't — it's an integrated mechanochemical system)
- That tensegrity is the only viable structural model (it's one model with known limitations)
- That a single universal scaling law governs all tissues (similar principles recur, but each tissue has its own parameters)
- That all pathology is mechanically determined (many pathologies have chemical, genetic, infectious, or traumatic origins)

## The CSA Bifurcation Model (Primary Clinical Application)

### Low CSA pathway (compression-dominant):
- Short acromion → deltoid vector more compressive → increased concavity compression
- Chronically elevated compressive load on articular cartilage
- Tissue responses (each individually rational):
  - Cartilage thinning (chondrocytes responding to supraphysiological compression)
  - Subchondral sclerosis (Wolff's Law — bone stiffens under increased load)
  - Medialization (joint seeking new geometric equilibrium)
- Clinical endpoint: glenohumeral osteoarthritis

### High CSA pathway (shear-dominant):
- Long lateral acromion → deltoid vector has more shear → supraspinatus overloaded
- Chronic tensile/shear overload on cuff tendon
- Tissue responses:
  - Tendinopathic changes (altered matrix, increased cellularity, neovascularization)
  - Progressive tearing (partial → full-thickness)
  - Fatty infiltration, muscle atrophy
- Clinical endpoint: rotator cuff tear

### Key insight:
OA and cuff tears are not "mutually protective." They represent **different bifurcation branches from the same parameter space.** The morphological starting conditions (CSA) determine which tissue hits its bifurcation threshold first. Once the system commits to one branch, the mechanical environment shifts in ways that make the other branch less likely.

## Structure Ideas for the Treatise

Inspired by Thompson's *On Growth and Form* — not a literature review but an *argument from first principles* illustrated with clinical evidence.

Possible chapter structure:
1. **The Question** — Why do certain morphologies predict certain pathologies? (Codman's observations, Gerber's CSA work, the clinical puzzle)
2. **The Physics** — Dissipative structures, bifurcation theory, far-from-equilibrium systems (Prigogine, accessible to clinicians)
3. **The Biology** — Mechanotransduction as information channel; how cells read force (Ingber, Engler, Mammoto)
4. **The Architecture** — Hierarchical structure in biological materials; why design principles recur (Fratzl, Thompson)
5. **The Shoulder** — CSA bifurcation model; posterior acromial morphology; dynamic glenoid version; scapulothoracic context
6. **The Tendon** — Compressive tendinopathy as another instance of the framework (Cook, application to elbow/shoulder)
7. **The Implications** — What changes if we view pathology as adaptive response rather than failure? (treatment philosophy, research directions)
