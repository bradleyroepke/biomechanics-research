# "Why You Should Pay More Attention to the Scapula"
## 3-Thread Format — 15-20 Minutes

---

## Thesis
*The scapula is not a passive platform — it is the primary determinant of shoulder function, and we have been treating its consequences instead of its causes.*

---

## THREAD 1: The Scapula is Upstream of Everything (4 min)

**Goal:** Establish that the scapula is the foundation — conceptual setup + 1 actionable item.

### Evolutionary Context (1 min)
- Humans are the only species that can throw with speed and accuracy. Darwin noted apes "are quite unable...to throw a stone with precision." (Kuhn 2016)
- The shoulder evolved FOR throwing — promoted through natural selection for predation, protection, resource competition. (Kuhn 2016)
- The GH joint cartilage is optimized for load distribution at ~90° abduction — the throwing position, NOT the resting position. (Kuhn 2016)
- The scapula migrated from lateral thorax (quadrupeds) to posterior thorax (bipeds) — losing bony stability, becoming entirely muscle-dependent. (Selby 2017)
- The same evolutionary adaptations that gave us throwing (depressed greater tuberosity, extreme external rotation) also made us vulnerable to rotator cuff disease (watershed zone, subacromial narrowing). (Kuhn 2016)

### The Platform Argument (2 min)
- The scapulothoracic "joint" has NO bony articulation — no capsule, no ligaments. Stabilized entirely by muscle.
- Scapulohumeral rhythm: the scapula contributes ~60° of 180° of arm elevation. (Inman 1944; McClure 2001 — bone pin study confirmed SHR of 1.7:1, with 50° upward rotation, 30° posterior tilt, 24° external rotation)
- The kinetic chain: 51% of total kinetic energy and 54% of total force in a tennis serve originate from legs/trunk — ALL funneled through the scapulothoracic articulation. (Kibler 1998)
- "In many tennis serving motions, the feet and body are actually off the ground...the entire stable base of the arm rests on the scapula rather than on the feet or the ground." (Kibler 1998 — direct quote)
- The subacromial space at 90° elevation is 4.1mm in healthy subjects. In impingement, it is reduced to 1.4mm. The margin between healthy and pathologic is measured in millimeters. (Borstad 2005, citing prior measurement studies)
- Simply slouching reduces active shoulder abduction by 23.6° (±10.7°) and decreases abduction muscle force by 16.2% at 90°. (Kebaetse 1999)

### Actionable Takeaway
- **Use the Scapular Assistance Test (SAT) on every shoulder patient.** Manually repositioning the scapula changes subacromial space. If it reduces pain or increases ROM, the scapula is contributing to the problem. (Seitz 2012)

## THREAD 2: The Scapula's Shape Predicts Pathology — AND Surgical Failure (7 min)

**Goal:** The hardest evidence, the most paradigm-shifting implications. This is the centerpiece.

### The Nyffeler-Moor-Gerber Lineage (2 min)
- **Nyffeler 2006 (JBJS, 384 citations):** Introduced the Acromion Index — ratio of glenoid-to-acromion distance over glenoid-to-humeral-head distance. AI was 0.73 in cuff tears, 0.60 in OA with intact cuff, 0.64 in normal controls (p<.0001). A large lateral acromial extension is associated with cuff tears.
- **Moor 2013 (BJJ):** Built on Nyffeler to introduce the CSA — combining acromial cover AND glenoid inclination into one measurement. CSA 33.1° in controls, 38.0° in RCT, 28.1° in OA. AUC of 0.92 for discriminating RCT from controls. CSA >35° → 84% were in the RCT group. CSA <30° → 93% were in the OA group.
- **Viehöfer 2016 (J Orthop Res):** Used finite element modeling to show WHY — a higher CSA produces a 23% greater joint shear-to-compression ratio at 50° abduction. The rotator cuff must generate forces exceeding physiological values to maintain stability with a high CSA. The cuff is being overworked by the scapula's shape.

### Dose-Response and the Debate (1.5 min)
- **Davide/Blonna 2016 (JSES):** CSA 34° ±3° in controls, 36° ±3° with isolated supraspinatus tears, 40° ±3.5° with supraspinatus + infraspinatus tears. Bigger CSA → bigger tears. CSA was the most relevant risk factor (OR 1.7, CI 1.4-2.0), even after controlling for age, gender, smoking, BMI, occupation.
- **Honest caveat — Arnar 2017 (JSES):** Found CSA associated with OA (OR 2.25, p=.002) but NOT with RCT (OR 1.12, p=.63). Not all studies replicate the cuff tear association. Present this openly — it strengthens your credibility.

### CSA Predicts Surgical Failure (1.5 min)
- **Garcia 2017 (JSES):** n=76 after arthroscopic cuff repair. CSA in no-retear group: 34.3° ±2.9°. CSA in full-thickness retear group: 38.6° ±3.5° (p<.01). If CSA >38°, OR of 14.8 for full-thickness retear (p<.01). Higher CSA also inversely correlated with ASES scores (p<.03).
- **Gerber 2018 (Arthroscopy, 112 citations):** Performed arthroscopic LATERAL acromioplasty (NOT anterior) to correct CSA at time of cuff repair. n=49. Mean CSA reduced from 37.5° to 33.9° (p<.001). Only ~6mm of lateral acromion resected. No deltoid dehiscence or atrophy. Postoperative CSA was significantly LARGER in failed than healed repairs (p=.026). Patients with CSA corrected to ≤33° had 25% more abduction strength than those with CSA ≥35° (p=.04).
- **The key insight:** Standard anterior acromioplasty (Neer-style) doesn't change CSA. Only LATERAL acromioplasty addresses the morphological driver. We've been shaving the wrong part of the acromion for decades.

### Posterior Acromial Morphology — The Gerber Story (2 min)
- **Meyer 2019 (JBJS, 72 citations):** Posterior Acromial Height (PAH) is associated with posterior instability. PAH in posterior instability: 30.9mm. In anterior instability: 19.5mm (p<.001). PAH ≥23mm → OR of 39 for posterior instability. A "Swiss chalet roof-type" steep acromion virtually excluded posterior instability.
- **Hochreiter 2023 (J Exp Orthop):** Biomechanical testing on 3D-printed scapula models showed correcting the glenoid ALONE was NOT sufficient to restore normal posterior translation. You must correct BOTH glenoid AND acromial anatomy. The acromion is an active restraint.
- **Gerber 2025 (JSES Int):** The SCOPE procedure — 3D-planned scapular corrective osteotomies for posterior instability. n=9, minimum 2-year follow-up. 6 of 9 were REVISION cases (previous approaches had failed). Median SSV improved 42.5%, Constant Score +18 points. 8/9 improved humeral head centering. 1 failure.
- **The paradigm shift:** Gerber is reshaping the scapula in TWO contexts — lateral acromioplasty for cuff disease (2018) and SCOPE osteotomies for posterior instability (2025). Previous approaches that ignored scapular morphology failed.

### Actionable Takeaways
- **Measure CSA on every pre-operative shoulder radiograph.** It takes 30 seconds and may change your surgical plan.
- **If CSA >35° at time of cuff repair, consider arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty** as an adjunct (Gerber 2018). You're treating the morphological driver, not just the tear.

## THREAD 3: Treating Upstream Changes Outcomes (5 min)

**Goal:** Clinical evidence that addressing the scapula works + research questions for the future.

### The rTSA Paradox (2 min)
- **Walker 2015 (JSES, 77 citations):** SHR in rTSA is 1.3:1, vs. normal 3:1. The scapula does MORE than half the work after rTSA, not less. n=28, 3 implant designs, fluoroscopy with 3D model-image registration.
- **Kawashima 2025 (JSES):** In semi-inlay rTSA, SHR ≥2 group had ASES 94.3 ±4.6 vs. SHR <2 group at 82.1 ±9.4 (p=.007). Significant positive correlation between SHR and ASES scores (r=0.452, p=.045). Better scapular function → better arthroplasty outcomes.
- **Simovitch/Gerber 2007 (JBJS):** 44% inferior scapular notching rate after Delta III rTSA. The prosthesis erodes the scapula under its new biomechanical demands.
- **The irony:** We replace the GH joint to help patients, but in doing so we make them MORE dependent on the very structure we've been ignoring.
- **Honest caveat:** Kawashima is n=20, single implant design. Promising but needs replication.

### Dyskinesis Persists After Surgery (1.5 min)
- **Mercurio 2026 (JSES Int):** Prospective study, n=21, 3D kinematic tracking with magnetic inertial sensors. After arthroscopic cuff repair, ROM and Constant scores improved significantly. But SHR showed NO significant change pre- vs. post-operatively (p>.05). Scapular dyskinesis persists despite successful cuff repair.
- **Implication:** If the dyskinesis was caused by the cuff tear, it should resolve after repair. It doesn't. This suggests the dyskinesis may have been the CAUSE, not the consequence — or at minimum, it's an independent problem that surgery doesn't fix.
- **Burkhart 2003 Part III (Arthroscopy):** 96 overhead athletes with SICK scapula treated with rehabilitation ONLY. 100% returned to preinjury performance by 4 months. Resolution of symptoms was "directly proportional to the ability to reposition the scapula." 19 asymptomatic pro pitchers all scored 0 — SICK scapula is pathology, not adaptation.
- **Camargo 2015 (JOSPT, 106 citations):** RCT showing stretching + strengthening exercises improved scapular kinematics, function, and pain in individuals with impingement.

### Actionable Takeaways
- **Prescribe scapular-focused prehab/rehab** (serratus anterior, lower trapezius targeting) for every cuff repair and rTSA. There are now RCTs supporting this (Camargo 2015).
- **Assess and document scapular function pre- and post-operatively.** If dyskinesis persists after cuff repair, the scapula may have been the driver — consider targeted scapular rehab.

## CLOSING (2 min)

### Three Pillars
1. **SHAPE** — CSA predicts cuff tears AND cuff retears. Posterior acromial morphology predicts instability. Gerber is reshaping the scapula surgically in both contexts.
2. **MOTION** — The scapula contributes 1/3 of arm elevation, transmits all force from trunk to arm, and clears the subacromial space by millimeters. When it moves wrong, everything downstream suffers.
3. **SURGERY** — Even when we replace the GH joint, scapular function determines outcomes. And fixing the cuff doesn't fix the dyskinesis.

### 4 Things You Can Do Monday Morning
1. **SAT on every shoulder patient** — if repositioning the scapula reduces pain, the scapula is contributing
2. **Measure CSA on every shoulder radiograph** — 30 seconds, may change your plan
3. **Consider lateral acromioplasty** for CSA >35° at time of cuff repair
4. **Prescribe scapular-focused rehab** before and after shoulder surgery

### Research Opportunities
- Does pre-operative scapular dyskinesia predict worse surgical outcomes? (Nobody has published this)
- Should scapular prehab be standard before rTSA?
- Can we develop objective, reliable scapular assessment tools?

### Closing Line
*"We've spent decades focused on the glenohumeral joint — and for good reason. But the scapula has been quietly running the show all along. It's time we gave it the attention it deserves."*

## ANTICIPATED Q&A

**Q: "If dyskinesis is so common in asymptomatic people, how can it be pathologic?"**
A: "The biomechanical data was measured with 3D electromagnetic tracking — it's not the same as clinical observation. And the morphological data — CSA, posterior acromial morphology — doesn't depend on clinical observation at all. These are radiographic measurements."

**Q: "Isn't the CSA debate settled? Arnar didn't find a cuff tear association."**
A: "You're right, not all studies replicate it. But Garcia showed an OR of 14.8 for retear if CSA >38°, and Gerber showed that insufficiently corrected CSA after repair correlates with both retear and weaker abduction. The weight of evidence tilts toward CSA mattering, but I acknowledge the inconsistency."

**Q: "How do you examine the scapula clinically?"**
A: "Honestly, our clinical tools are poor — Vila-Dieguez 2026 showed a Kappa of 0.12 for visual assessment. The SAT is the most practical test we have. For morphology, measure CSA on radiographs. The field needs better objective assessment tools."

**Q: "Is lateral acromioplasty the answer?"**
A: "Gerber's data is promising — safe, effective, no deltoid compromise. But it can't correct CSA above 40° (Olmos 2021). And we don't have long-term RCTs. I think it should be considered as an adjunct to cuff repair when CSA is elevated, but it's not standard of care yet."
