# rTSA Deep Read — 6 Papers Synthesis

*2026-03-25*

## The Central Insight

rTSA converts the scapula from a passive platform into the sole load-bearing structure. The entire mechanical chain runs through the scapula: thorax → scapulothoracic articulation → scapula/baseplate → glenosphere → humeral cup → deltoid. No one is measuring whether the scapula can do the job being asked of it.

---

## Key Numbers

### Grammont Design (Boileau 2005)
- COR medialized 19.0 mm (SD 9.9) onto glenoid surface
- Humerus medialized 16 mm, lowered 15 mm
- Deltoid lever arm increased up to 42%
- Recruits deltoid segments I-IV (vs normal II-III only)
- Elevation: 55° → 121° (p < .001)
- ER did NOT improve (7° → 11°, NS)
- Teres minor status: Constant 66 (intact) vs 46 (torn/fatty), p = .007

### Scapular Notching (Sirveaux 2004)
- 63.6% notching rate (49/77)
- Grade 3-4 notching significantly decreased Constant scores
- Glenoid erosion type predicted notch severity (E2/E3 worse)
- Glenoid loosening: 6.25%; glenosphere dissociation: 8.75%
- Survivorship: 91.3% at 5yr, 74.6% at 7yr, 29.8% at 8yr

### Glenosphere Position (Nyffeler 2005)
- 2-4 mm distal repositioning: adduction deficit from -25° to -1°
- Inferior overhang (Config C) significantly superior (p < .001)
- Scapular neck geometry is the rate-limiting factor for ROM

### Muscle Architecture (Ward 2006)
- Total cuff PCSA lost in CTA: 36.07 cm² of stabilizer muscle
- Supraspinatus sarcomere length at rest: 3.23 μm (descending limb = passive tension)
- Small changes in muscle length → large functional changes
- Cuff muscles designed for force, not excursion (Lf/Lm = 0.45-0.57)

### Lateralized vs Medialized (Frankle 2006)
- Glenoid bone quality determines COR position compromise
- Suboptimal bone → must medicalize → sacrifices ER and cuff tension
- Optimal bone → can lateralize → better function but higher bone stress

## The Gap Our Framework Fills

None of these papers:
- Measure scapulothoracic kinematics after rTSA
- Report periscapular muscle strength as outcome variable
- Correlate scapular position with functional outcomes
- Ask whether the scapula can do the job being demanded of it

Combined with Walker 2015 (SHR = 1.3:1) and Kawashima 2025 (better SHR = better ASES), the argument is clear: scapulothoracic function is a critical determinant of rTSA outcomes that nobody is systematically measuring or accounting for in surgical planning.
