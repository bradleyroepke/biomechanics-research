# Form, Force, and Adaptation: A First-Principles Framework for Understanding Shoulder Pathology

Brad Roepke, MD

Department of Orthopedic Surgery, University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, Oklahoma City, OK

---

> "The form of an object is a diagram of forces."
> — D'Arcy Thompson, *On Growth and Form* (1917)

---

## Abstract

Rotator cuff tears, glenohumeral osteoarthritis, posterior instability, and cuff tear arthropathy are treated as distinct conditions with separate etiologies. Yet each can be traced to the same underlying principle: a tissue receiving a force type it was not designed to bear. Bone and cartilage are compression elements. Tendons and ligaments are tension elements. The shoulder's architecture distributes these forces through a tensegrity system in which the rotator cuff compresses the humeral head into the glenoid concavity while the scapula positions that concavity to receive the load. When scapular morphology or position is altered, the ratio of compression to shear at the glenohumeral joint changes, and the tissue that receives the inappropriate force is the one that fails. The critical shoulder angle governs this ratio in the coronal plane: a high angle produces shear that overloads the cuff, while a low angle produces compression that overloads cartilage. Glenoid version governs it in the axial plane: retroversion converts centered compression into posterior shear, driving posterior instability and progressive glenoid erosion. In every case, the tissue response is predictable from first principles and consistent with established adaptation laws. What clinicians call pathology may represent correct cellular responses to the mechanical information received. If so, durable treatment requires correcting the mechanical environment, not simply repairing the tissue that adapted to it. This framework is falsifiable: if correcting morphological parameters does not alter tissue adaptation trajectories in prospective study, the mechanical causation claim fails.

Level of Evidence: Level V; Expert Opinion

Keywords: tensegrity; mechanotransduction; critical shoulder angle; concavity compression; scapula; shoulder biomechanics; rotator cuff; osteoarthritis; posterior instability

## 1. Four Observations Without a Unifying Mechanism

Glenohumeral osteoarthritis and rotator cuff tears are among the most common degenerative conditions of the shoulder, yet they almost never coexist in the same joint. Moor and colleagues demonstrated that a single radiographic measurement, the critical shoulder angle, separates the two conditions with an area under the curve of 0.92.(1) Patients with cuff tears had a mean angle of 38 degrees. Those with osteoarthritis had a mean of 28 degrees. The measurement is congenital and does not change over time.(2) A morphological parameter determined at birth predicts which degenerative disease develops decades later. No existing model explains why.

Articular-side partial-thickness rotator cuff tears outnumber bursal-side tears by roughly two to one. If acromial impingement were the primary mechanism, one would expect the opposite: the bursal surface faces the acromion and should fail first. Codman described the "rim rent" in 1934, noting that cuff tears begin on the deep surface at the tuberosity insertion.(3) He observed that "it would be hard to explain this by erosion from contact with the acromion process." Ozaki and colleagues confirmed this in 200 cadaver shoulders: cuff tears that did not extend to the bursal surface were associated with normal acromial histology, and acromial changes appeared only after the tear reached the bursal side.(4) The acromion responds to the tear. It does not cause it.

Posterior instability produces worse arthritis than anterior instability. Samilson and Prieto found that shoulders with posterior instability had a significantly higher incidence of moderate or severe glenohumeral arthritis compared to those with anterior instability.(5) The posterior glenoid has thinner cartilage and less subchondral bone stock than the anterior glenoid, yet current models do not explain why the direction of instability determines the severity of degeneration.

The supraspinatus is the first cuff tendon to fail in the majority of patients, despite not being the thinnest or having the smallest cross-sectional area. The subscapularis is larger. The infraspinatus inserts over a broader footprint. Yet the supraspinatus tears first, and it tears at its anterior insertion near the biceps tendon, in a location Matsen described as where "the loads are presumably the greatest."(6) The biology of the supraspinatus is not fundamentally different from its neighbors. Its mechanical environment is.

These are four observations from four different research groups studying four different conditions. Each is well established. Each is currently treated as a separate finding with a separate explanation. Taken together, they point toward a single principle.

## 2. Tissues Are Designed for Specific Force Types

Every force acting on the shoulder can be decomposed into three components: compression, tension, and shear. Each tissue in the musculoskeletal system is designed to bear one of these.

Bone resists compression. Trabecular architecture aligns along principal stress directions. Cortical thickness increases where compressive loads concentrate. Wolff recognized this in 1892, and Frost formalized it with the mechanostat: bone does not adapt on a smooth continuum but switches between qualitatively different biological programs at specific strain thresholds.(7,8) Below approximately 100 microstrain, it resorbs. Between 100 and 1,500 microstrain, it maintains. Above 1,500, it actively models and forms. These are set points, not gradients.

Articular cartilage distributes compression. Its layered structure, with tangential collagen fibers at the surface transitioning to radial fibers at depth, is optimized to spread compressive load across the subchondral plate. It requires cyclic loading to maintain its matrix. Immobilize it and it degrades.

Tendons resist tension. Collagen fibers align along the tensile vector. They are strong in tension and weak in compression. When a tendon is subjected to sustained compression, it undergoes fibrocartilaginous metaplasia: the tissue forms cartilage-like cells and matrix in the compressed zone. This was described by Riley and colleagues in the supraspinatus and characterized as "an adaptation to mechanical forces, including compression."(9) The tendon is not failing. It is adapting to an environment it was not designed for, by becoming a tissue that handles compression better and tension worse.

Ligaments resist tension at end range. The capsular ligaments of the shoulder are lax throughout most of the arc of motion and exert stabilizing force only when they come under tension near the limits of rotation.(10) They are designed to be loaded intermittently and briefly.

The labrum is a hybrid. It deepens the glenoid concavity, functioning as a compression element, while anchoring the glenohumeral ligaments, functioning as a tension element. It is fibrocartilage: a tissue engineered for the interface between compression and tension.

The tendon-bone insertion embodies this principle in miniature. Clark and Harryman showed that the rotator cuff insertion transitions through four zones: tendon (pure tension), nonmineralized fibrocartilage, mineralized fibrocartilage, and bone (pure compression).(11) The transition is gradual. There is no abrupt change in material properties. The insertion is avascular by design: direct insertions are not traversed by blood vessels.(12) The zone where tension transitions to compression is mechanically optimized but biologically vulnerable. It is where cuff tears begin.

The cellular machinery that reads these forces is well characterized. Integrins spanning the cell membrane link the extracellular matrix to the cytoskeleton, transmitting mechanical forces to the nucleus in milliseconds, faster than any chemical signal can diffuse.(13) Engler and colleagues showed that mesenchymal stem cells plated on substrates of varying stiffness differentiate into neurons on soft substrates, muscle on intermediate substrates, and osteoblasts on stiff substrates, using substrate stiffness alone.(14) After three weeks, the mechanically directed commitment becomes irreversible. Full differentiation requires both the correct mechanical environment and appropriate chemical signals, but the mechanical commitment precedes and constrains the chemical response.

This is not a new principle. Wolff's Law, Davis's Law, the Hueter-Volkmann principle, and the mechanostat are all taught in orthopedic training. Individually, each describes a tissue adapting to its mechanical environment. Taken together, they describe a single reality: living tissue continuously reads its mechanical environment and adapts its structure and composition accordingly. When the environment delivers a force the tissue was designed for, the adaptation is homeostatic. When the environment delivers the wrong force, the adaptation produces what we call disease.

## 3. The Shoulder as a Tension-Compression System

### The Evolutionary Trade-Off

The shoulder sacrificed bony stability for range of motion. In reptiles, the glenohumeral joint achieves stability through osseous congruity: the humeral head sits within a deep, conforming socket. In humans, the relationship is a golf ball on a tee.(15) Matsen described this evolutionary trajectory: "the inherent osseous articular congruity of the upper limbs was sacrificed for soft tissue stability to achieve a greater degree of mobility."(15) Three diarthrodial joints, each with minimal bony conformity, stabilized by a continuous network of capsule, ligaments, and muscle.

This architecture has a name. A system in which rigid compression elements are suspended within a continuous tension network is a tensegrity structure. The term was coined by Buckminster Fuller. Ingber demonstrated that cells themselves are tensegrity structures: a prestressed cytoskeleton in which microtubules resist compression while actin filaments and intermediate filaments bear tension.(13,16) Whether the musculoskeletal shoulder meets the strict mathematical definition of tensegrity is debatable. That it operates on the same principle, compression elements stabilized by a continuous tension field, is not.

### Concavity Compression

The central stability mechanism of the glenohumeral joint is concavity compression. The rotator cuff compresses the humeral head into the glenoid concavity. The deeper the concavity and the greater the compressive force, the more translational force is required to dislocate the joint.(17) Matsen quantified this as the stability ratio: the peak translational force divided by the applied compressive force. In normal shoulders, this ratio is 50 to 60 percent in the superior-inferior direction and 30 to 35 percent in the anterior-posterior direction.(17)

Matsen made a critical distinction: the cuff's primary stability function is compression, not depression. "Although in the past the cuff muscles were referred to as head depressors, it is evident that the inferiorly directed components of cuff muscle force are small; instead, the primary stabilizing function of the cuff muscles is through head compression into the glenoid."(6) The cuff is a compressor. The glenoid is a compression target.

The balance stability angle formalizes this. The glenohumeral joint remains stable as long as the net humeral joint reaction force, the resultant of all muscular, ligamentous, gravitational, and external forces, is directed within the effective glenoid arc.(10) The balance stability angle is the maximum angle this force vector can make with the glenoid centerline before dislocation occurs. Anything that reduces the concavity (labral tears, bone loss, cartilage erosion) or redirects the force vector (altered muscle balance, changed glenoid orientation) narrows this margin.

### The Scapula Moves the Target

Matsen described two complementary functions: "the glenohumeral muscles strive to direct the joint reaction vector toward the glenoid and the muscles controlling the scapula move the glenoid toward the force."(12) The first is the cuff compressing the head into the socket. The second is the scapulothoracic system positioning the socket to receive the compression.

The scapula is the only major bone in the shoulder girdle with no bony articulation to the axial skeleton. It is suspended entirely by the surrounding musculature. Its position at any moment is a vector summation of the tension forces in the trapezius, serratus anterior, rhomboids, levator scapulae, pectoralis minor, and the muscles originating from its surfaces. A change in any single tension element shifts the summation and moves the scapula to a new equilibrium position.

This sensitivity has been quantified. A change in thoracic posture alone, simply slouching, reduces active shoulder abduction by 23.6 degrees and abduction muscle force by 16.2 percent.(18) The subacromial space measures 4.1 mm in healthy subjects at 90 degrees of elevation and narrows to 1.4 mm in subjects with impingement.(19) The margin between functional and pathologic mechanics is less than three millimeters.

Stability depends on the joint reaction force vector crossing the joint through "cartilage and bone, the tissues designed for compression stress."(12) When the scapula positions the glenoid correctly, the force vector hits the target. Compression passes through compression-tolerant tissue. When the scapula is malaligned, by morphology or by dynamic malposition, the force vector misses the target. Compression becomes shear. And the tissue that receives that shear is the one that fails.

The system even has pre-stress. A negative pressure normally exists within the glenohumeral joint. If this pressure is equalized by arthrotomy or even a small puncture, inferior subluxation occurs.(17) Pre-stress, a baseline compressive force that holds the system together before any muscular engagement, is a defining feature of tensegrity architecture.

## 4. Pathology as Adaptation

If the principle stated above is correct, then specific alterations in scapular morphology should produce specific force-type shifts, and those shifts should drive specific tissue adaptations that we recognize as specific diseases. The following five examples test this logic.

### 4a. High Critical Shoulder Angle and Rotator Cuff Disease

The critical shoulder angle combines lateral acromial extension with glenoid inclination. A high angle means a longer lateral acromion and a more superiorly inclined glenoid.(1) What changes biomechanically is the deltoid's force vector.

With a high CSA, the deltoid generates a greater superiorly directed shear component at the glenohumeral joint. Viehöfer and colleagues demonstrated that a CSA of 38 degrees produces a 23 percent higher shear-to-compression ratio compared to 33 degrees.(20) The supraspinatus must generate greater force to counteract this shear and maintain concavity compression. Gerber confirmed this on a physical simulator: supraspinatus tendon load increases 13 to 33 percent for a CSA of 38 degrees compared to 33 degrees.(21)

The supraspinatus is now caught between two forces. It must generate more tension to counteract deltoid shear. Simultaneously, it is compressed between the humeral head below and the acromion above. Subacromial pressure rises from 8 mm Hg at rest to 56 mm Hg during loaded elevation.(22) The tendon responds to this compression by forming fibrocartilage, an adaptation to compression that weakens the tissue in tension.(9) The tendon is being asked to do more tension work while being remodeled into a tissue less capable of bearing tension.

Where does it fail? Not on the bursal surface, where acromial compression is greatest. On the articular surface, where the glenoid rim applies a shearing load to the deep fibers of the insertion.(6) In an animal model, increasing abrasion of the cuff tendon by the acromion produced only bursal-side lesions and never the intratendinous or articular-side defects seen clinically.(6) The articular side is also less stiff in compression than the bursal side.(23) The dominant pathological force on the articular surface is shear, not compression.

Once fibers begin to fail, the process accelerates. Each fiber failure increases the load on neighboring fibers, a zipper phenomenon.(6) Failed fibers expose the tendon to joint fluid containing lytic enzymes, compromising any healing response. Scar tissue that forms lacks normal tendon resilience and is at increased risk for subsequent failure under the same loading conditions.

Blonna and colleagues found a dose-response relationship: isolated supraspinatus tears averaged a CSA of 36 degrees, while combined supraspinatus-infraspinatus tears averaged 40 degrees.(24) Garcia and colleagues found that a CSA exceeding 38 degrees produces an odds ratio of 14.8 for full-thickness retear after arthroscopic repair.(25) Gerber showed that arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty reduces the CSA from 37.5 to 33.9 degrees, and patients whose CSA was corrected to 33 degrees or less had 25 percent greater abduction strength and lower retear rates than those whose CSA remained above 35 degrees.(26)

The chain is: high CSA (morphology) produces increased deltoid shear (force shift), which overloads the supraspinatus in tension while compression remodels it into fibrocartilage (tissue adaptation), leading to articular-side fiber failure and progressive tearing (pathology). Correcting the morphology corrects the force environment and improves outcomes.

### 4b. Low Critical Shoulder Angle and Osteoarthritis

A low CSA means a shorter acromion and a more medially inclined glenoid. The deltoid's force vector is directed more compressively into the glenoid. Less shear is generated. The cuff is spared because the shear demand is low. But the articular cartilage receives greater cumulative compression.

Moor found a mean CSA of 28 degrees in patients with osteoarthritis, compared to 33 degrees in controls.(1) In a low-CSA shoulder, the deltoid acts more as a compressor and less as an elevator. The joint reaction force passes more directly through the articular surface. The cartilage is loaded well within its design parameters on any given cycle, but the cumulative compressive dose is higher.

Cartilage responds to chronic compression overload predictably. It thins. Subchondral bone scleroses, densifying along the compression vector exactly as Wolff's Law predicts. The joint medializes as the glenoid concavity deepens under asymmetric load. Once cartilage is lost, the coefficient of friction between opposing surfaces rises approximately tenfold.(27) What was previously low-friction compression becomes high-friction shear, and the degeneration accelerates.

The inverse relationship between cuff tears and osteoarthritis is not mutual protection. It is pathway selection. The same morphological parameter, the critical shoulder angle, determines the ratio of compression to shear at the glenohumeral joint. A high ratio preferentially loads the cuff. A low ratio preferentially loads the cartilage. Once one pathway is established, the altered geometry makes the other less likely. In a shoulder with a high CSA and a large cuff tear, superior migration reduces compressive loading on the articular surface. In a shoulder with a low CSA and advancing OA, the intact cuff prevents superior migration. The pathologies diverge because they are competing branches of the same mechanical system.

### 4c. Glenoid Retroversion, Posterior Instability, and Progressive Posterior Wear

The critical shoulder angle operates in the coronal plane. Glenoid version operates in the axial plane. The principle is identical: geometry determines the direction of force, and the tissue that receives the misdirected force is the one that fails.

Glenoid retroversion rotates the glenoid centerline posteriorly. The net humeral joint reaction force, which in a normal shoulder is directed into the center of the concavity, now has a component that exceeds the posterior balance stability angle. Centered compression becomes posterior-directed shear. The posterior labrum, designed to deepen the concavity, is sheared off its bony attachment. The posterior capsule, designed for intermittent end-range tension, is chronically loaded and plastically deforms. Matsen measured an average capsular elongation of 19 percent in shoulders with recurrent anterior dislocation compared to the contralateral side.(10) Each episode of instability makes the next easier by further degrading the tension restraint.

Meyer and colleagues showed that posterior acromial morphology compounds this effect. A posterior acromial height of 30.9 mm was associated with posterior instability, compared to 19.5 mm in anterior instability (OR 39 at a PAH cutoff of 23 mm).(28) Hochreiter tested three-dimensional printed scapula models and found that a high, flat acromial configuration reduced the force required for posterior displacement by 23 to 60 percent.(29) Correcting glenoid retroversion alone was insufficient; both glenoid and acromial morphology had to be corrected to normalize posterior stability.

What happens next is the cascade that connects instability to arthritis. Once the humeral head subluxates posteriorly, it loads the posterior glenoid eccentrically. Concentrated compression on a small area of posterior cartilage exceeds its adaptive capacity. The cartilage erodes. As it erodes, contact area shrinks and contact pressure rises, accelerating the erosion. Simultaneously, the anterior capsule, now chronically unloaded, shortens. This is Davis's Law operating in reverse: tissue contracture under reduced tension. The shortened anterior capsule creates obligate posterior translation. Matsen quantified this directly: with capsular shortening equivalent to 9 mm of overstuffing, obligate translation begins 30 degrees earlier in the arc of motion.(27) The anterior contracture drives more eccentric posterior compression, which drives more erosion, which permits more subluxation.

Capsulorrhaphy arthropathy proves this mechanism iatrogenically. Matsen described it explicitly: "excessive anterior or posterior capsular tightening forces the head of the humerus out of its normal concentric relationship with the glenoid fossa. The eccentric glenohumeral contact increases contact pressure and joint surface wear."(27) The same cascade, abnormal tension leading to eccentric compression leading to shear and erosion, produced by a surgeon's hands rather than by constitutional morphology.

Walker and colleagues provided longitudinal confirmation: 89 percent of B1 glenoids progressed to B2 or B3 over a mean of 74 months, while only 19 percent of concentrically worn A1 glenoids progressed at all.(30) Once eccentric loading is established, the system is committed to a trajectory.

Domos and colleagues described the B0 glenoid as the earliest identifiable stage: static posterior humeral decentering without glenoid erosion, occurring in young men with increased retroversion.(31) If B0 is the same entity as the C1 constitutional posterior instability described by Akgun and Gerber, then posterior instability in a twenty-five-year-old and the need for total shoulder arthroplasty with a B2 glenoid three decades later are not separate conditions. They are sequential stages of a single morphology-driven cascade.

The chain is: retroversion plus adverse acromial morphology (morphology) converts centered compression into posterior shear (force shift), which shears the posterior labrum, stretches the posterior capsule, and drives eccentric posterior compression (tissue adaptation), leading to progressive posterior glenoid erosion and eventually end-stage arthritis (pathology). Gerber's SCOPE procedure, which corrects both glenoid version and acromial morphology, addresses the mechanical environment rather than the tissue that adapted to it.(32)

### 4d. Cuff Tear, Loss of Concavity Compression, and Cuff Tear Arthropathy

When a rotator cuff tear disrupts the transverse force couple, concavity compression is lost. Parsons and colleagues demonstrated that an isolated supraspinatus tear produces no significant change in joint reaction force.(33) The subscapularis and infraspinatus maintain the transverse couple. Once the tear extends to include the infraspinatus, disrupting the couple, the joint reaction force drops 61 percent and maximum abduction collapses from 85 to 41 degrees.(33) This is a threshold event, not a gradual decline. The system compensates until it cannot.

Without the cuff compressing the head into the glenoid, the deltoid's superiorly directed vector goes unopposed. The humeral head migrates superiorly. Superior migration erodes the superior glenoid concavity through eccentric compression. As the concavity flattens, the balance stability angle decreases in the superior direction, which permits further migration, which causes further erosion. The system enters a positive feedback loop.

Matsen described the threshold at which this loop becomes irreversible. He called it the boutonniere deformity of the shoulder. When the residual cuff tendons slide below the equator of the humeral head, "a shoulder with a buttonholed cuff is victimized by the conversion of balancing forces into unbalancing forces."(6) The very muscles that were compressing the head into the glenoid now pull it superiorly. This is not gradual deterioration. It is a phase transition: the system flips from stable to catastrophically unstable at a specific anatomic threshold.

The acromion records this mechanical history. The coracoacromial ligament, loaded in tension by the superiorly migrated head, transmits traction forces to the undersurface of the acromion. Matsen showed that the type III hooked acromion is "acquired in response to traction forces applied via the CAL and are not congenital."(15) The hooked acromion is not a cause of impingement. It is a mechanotransduction artifact: bone remodeling in response to altered tension, written into the anatomy as a permanent record of the force environment.

### 4e. Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty and Scapulothoracic Overload

When a reverse shoulder prosthesis eliminates the cuff and medializes the center of rotation, the biomechanical demand shifts to the scapulothoracic system. Walker and colleagues measured scapulohumeral rhythm by fluoroscopy across three implant designs and found that the ratio drops from 3:1 in the normal shoulder to 1.3:1 after rTSA.(34) The scapula must contribute roughly double its normal share of arm elevation.

Kawashima and colleagues found that this altered demand has direct clinical consequences. Patients with an SHR of 2 or greater had significantly higher functional scores than those with an SHR below 2 (ASES 94.3 vs. 82.1, P = 0.007).(35) The scapulothoracic system, not the prosthesis, becomes the rate-limiting element.

This observation is consistent with the framework. The rTSA eliminates the cuff's compression function and replaces it with a fixed mechanical constraint. The scapula must now reposition the glenoid through a larger arc, more frequently, with muscles that may already be compromised by chronic dysfunction, fatty infiltration, or sarcomere loss.(36) If the periscapular muscles cannot meet the demand, the patient's functional ceiling is set not by the implant but by the upstream system that positions it.

## 5. The Unifying Principle

Five pathologies. Five different tissues failing. In every case, the same mechanism: a tissue received a force type it was not designed to bear.

The rotator cuff tendon, designed for tension, received compression and adapted by forming fibrocartilage, then failed when asked to bear the increased tensile load that the altered morphology demanded. Articular cartilage, designed for distributed compression, received concentrated or cumulative overload and responded with the thinning and sclerosis that characterize osteoarthritis. The posterior labrum, designed to deepen the glenoid concavity, received shear from a posteriorly subluxating humeral head and detached. The posterior capsule, designed for end-range tension, was chronically loaded and plastically deformed. The superior glenoid, designed for centered compression, received eccentric loading from a superiorly migrated humeral head and eroded.

In every case, the tissue responded correctly to the information it received. It adapted its structure and composition to match its mechanical environment, exactly as Wolff, Davis, and Frost described. What clinicians call pathology may be the tissue doing precisely what it was designed to do, in an environment it was not designed for.

If this framing is correct, three implications follow.

First, durable treatment requires correcting the mechanical environment, not just repairing the failed tissue. Gerber's lateral acromioplasty corrects the CSA and reduces cuff retear rates.(26) His SCOPE procedure corrects glenoid and acromial morphology and restores glenohumeral centering in patients who failed prior soft-tissue stabilization.(32) Arner showed that adverse acromial morphology predicts failure of arthroscopic posterior capsulolabral repair.(37) The tissue repair fails because the upstream force environment was not addressed. This is the prediction the framework makes, and the early evidence supports it.

Second, the framework extends beyond the shoulder. Lateral epicondylitis may represent a compressive tendinopathy: the deep surface of the extensor carpi radialis brevis is compressed against the radial head during forearm rotation, producing the same fibrocartilaginous metaplasia seen in the supraspinatus under compression.(38) The histopathology, angiofibroblastic hyperplasia with chondroid change, is localized to the deep surface, exactly where compression is greatest. If the same principle operates at the elbow, the framework is not shoulder-specific. It is a property of how tissues respond to force.

Third, rehabilitation may need to be reconceived. If scapulothoracic positioning determines the force environment of every downstream structure, then prehabilitation before shoulder surgery should aim not at conventional strengthening but at restoring the tension balance that positions the scapula correctly. This is particularly relevant before rTSA, where the postoperative demand on the scapulothoracic system approximately doubles.(34)

Several things this framework does not claim. It does not claim that all shoulder pathology is mechanically determined. Genetic predisposition, vascular insufficiency, systemic inflammation, and acute trauma are real and independent contributors. It does not claim that tensegrity is the only viable structural model. It does not claim that a single universal scaling law governs all tissues. And it does not claim that the specific threshold values for each tissue's adaptation response are known. They are not. Frost acknowledged in 2003 that the mechanostat's specific thresholds for non-osseous tissues remain undefined.(8) What the framework claims is that the *principle* of threshold-dependent adaptation to specific force types, already established for bone, operates in the shoulder's soft tissues as well, and that the scapula's morphology and position determine which tissue reaches its threshold first.

## 6. Falsification Criteria and Directions for Investigation

A framework that cannot be proven wrong is not a framework. It is an opinion. The following predictions are specific enough to test and, if refuted, would undermine the central claims.

First, if the CSA determines the compression-to-shear ratio, then surgical correction of the CSA below 28 degrees should paradoxically increase osteoarthritis risk by shifting the force environment to compression-dominant loading. If lateral acromioplasty reduces cuff retears but patients subsequently develop accelerated articular cartilage wear, the framework is supported. If they do not, the relationship between CSA and joint surface loading is more complex than the framework assumes.

Second, pre-operative scapulothoracic kinematics should independently predict outcomes after rTSA, given the increased scapulothoracic dependence these prostheses impose. A prospective study measuring three-dimensional scapulothoracic motion before surgery, correlated with functional outcomes at one and two years, would test whether the upstream system limits the downstream result.

Third, a prospective cohort of B0 glenoids tracked with serial imaging should show progression to B1 and B2 at rates predicted by the degree of glenoid retroversion. If B0 glenoids with retroversion exceeding 15 degrees progress faster than those with less retroversion, the morphology-driven cascade is supported. If progression is independent of retroversion, the mechanical pathway is not the primary driver.

Fourth, dynamic glenoid version during functional loading should differ between patients who develop posterior glenoid wear and those who do not. Measurement of glenoid orientation using biplanar fluoroscopy during loaded functional tasks, correlated with subsequent wear progression on serial imaging, would establish whether the scapulothoracic system contributes a modifiable component to posterior glenoid loading.

Each of these studies is feasible. Each tests a specific claim. And each, if the result is negative, would force revision or abandonment of the corresponding part of the framework. This is by design.
