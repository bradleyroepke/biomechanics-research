# Pathology as Adaptation: How Scapular Architecture May Reveal the Mechanical Origins of Degenerative Shoulder Disease

Brad Roepke, MD

Department of Orthopedic Surgery, University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, Oklahoma City, OK

Corresponding author: Brad Roepke, MD, Department of Orthopedic Surgery, University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, P.O. Box 26901, Oklahoma City, OK 73126. Email: brad.roepke@example.com

## Abstract

Rotator cuff tears, glenohumeral osteoarthritis, posterior instability, cuff tear arthropathy, and suboptimal reverse arthroplasty outcomes are treated as distinct conditions with separate etiologies. Yet each can be traced to the same underlying principle: a tissue receiving a force it was not designed to bear, delivered by the same upstream structure. The scapula has no bony articulation with the axial skeleton. It is stabilized entirely by muscle tension. Its morphology and its dynamic position determine the ratio of compression to shear at the glenohumeral joint, and the tissue that receives the misdirected force is the one that fails. Bone and cartilage are compression elements. Tendons and ligaments are tension elements. When scapular morphology converts centered compression into shear, the supraspinatus is overloaded and tears. When it directs excessive compression into the glenoid, cartilage fails. When posterior acromial morphology reduces posterior restraint, the humeral head subluxates posteriorly, initiating a cascade from instability to glenoid erosion. When the rotator cuff fails entirely, the loss of concavity compression produces superior migration and cuff tear arthropathy. And when a reverse prosthesis doubles the biomechanical demand on the scapulothoracic system, the scapula's capacity becomes the ceiling on functional recovery. In every case, the tissue response is predictable from first principles and consistent with established adaptation laws. What clinicians call pathology may represent correct cellular responses to the mechanical information received. If so, durable treatment requires correcting the mechanical environment, not simply repairing the tissue that adapted to it. This framework is falsifiable: if correcting morphological parameters does not alter tissue adaptation trajectories in prospective study, the mechanical causation claim fails.

Level of Evidence: Level V; Expert Opinion / Narrative Review

Keywords: scapula; critical shoulder angle; tensegrity; mechanotransduction; concavity compression; rotator cuff; osteoarthritis; posterior instability; reverse shoulder arthroplasty

## Introduction

Glenohumeral osteoarthritis and rotator cuff tears are among the most common degenerative conditions of the shoulder, yet they almost never coexist in the same joint. Moor and colleagues demonstrated that a single radiographic measurement, the critical shoulder angle, separates the two with an area under the curve of 0.92.^1^ The measurement is congenital and does not change over time.^2^ A morphological parameter determined at birth predicts which degenerative disease develops decades later.

Articular-side partial-thickness rotator cuff tears outnumber bursal-side tears by roughly two to one. If acromial impingement were the primary mechanism, the opposite would be expected. Codman described the rim rent in 1934, noting that cuff tears begin on the deep surface and that "it would be hard to explain this by erosion from contact with the acromion process."^3^ Ozaki confirmed this in 200 cadaver shoulders: acromial changes appeared only after a tear reached the bursal surface.^4^ The acromion responds to the tear. It does not cause it.

Posterior instability produces worse arthritis than anterior instability.^5^ The posterior glenoid has thinner cartilage and less subchondral bone stock, yet current models do not explain why the direction of instability determines the severity of degeneration.

The supraspinatus is the first cuff tendon to fail in the majority of patients. If tendon size determined vulnerability, the teres minor, which is smaller, should fail first. It does not. The supraspinatus tears at its anterior insertion near the biceps tendon, where the mechanical loads are greatest.^6^ Its biology is not fundamentally different from its neighbors. Its mechanical environment is.

These are four observations from four different research groups studying four different conditions. Each is well established. Each is currently treated as a separate finding with a separate explanation. Taken together, they point toward a single principle.

## Tissues Are Designed for Specific Force Types

Every force acting on the shoulder can be decomposed into compression, tension, and shear. Each musculoskeletal tissue is designed to bear one of these.

Bone resists compression. Trabecular architecture aligns along principal stress directions. Wolff recognized this in 1892, and Frost formalized it with the mechanostat: bone switches between qualitatively different biological programs at specific strain thresholds.^7,8^ Below approximately 100 microstrain, it resorbs. Between 100 and 1,500, it maintains. Above 1,500, it models. These are set points, not gradients.

Articular cartilage distributes compression. Its layered structure is optimized to spread compressive load across the subchondral plate. It requires cyclic loading to maintain its matrix.

Tendons resist tension. Collagen fibers align along the tensile vector. When a tendon is subjected to sustained compression, it undergoes fibrocartilaginous metaplasia: the tissue forms cartilage-like cells in the compressed zone. Riley and colleagues described this in the supraspinatus as "an adaptation to mechanical forces, including compression."^9^ The tendon is not failing. It is adapting to an environment it was not designed for, by becoming a tissue that handles compression better and tension worse.

Ligaments resist tension at end range. The glenohumeral ligaments are lax through most of the arc of motion and stabilize only near the limits of rotation.^10^

The tendon-bone insertion embodies this principle in miniature. Clark and Harryman showed that the rotator cuff insertion transitions through four zones: tendon, nonmineralized fibrocartilage, mineralized fibrocartilage, and bone.^11^ The zone where tension transitions to compression is mechanically optimized but biologically vulnerable. It is where cuff tears begin.

The cellular machinery that reads these forces is well characterized. Integrins spanning the cell membrane transmit mechanical forces to the nucleus in milliseconds, faster than any chemical signal can diffuse.^12^ Engler showed that mesenchymal stem cells differentiate into neurons on soft substrates, muscle on intermediate substrates, and osteoblasts on stiff substrates, using substrate stiffness alone.^13^ The mechanical commitment precedes and constrains the chemical response.

Wolff's Law, Davis's Law, the Hueter-Volkmann principle, and the mechanostat are all taught in orthopedic training. Taken together, they describe a single reality: living tissue continuously reads its mechanical environment and adapts accordingly. When the environment delivers the right force, the adaptation is homeostatic. When it delivers the wrong force, the adaptation produces what we call disease.

## The Shoulder as a Tension-Compression System

The shoulder sacrificed bony stability for range of motion. In reptiles, the glenohumeral joint achieves stability through osseous congruity. In humans, the relationship is a golf ball on a tee.^14^ Three diarthrodial joints, each with minimal bony conformity, stabilized by a continuous network of capsule, ligaments, and muscle. A system in which rigid compression elements are suspended within a continuous tension network is a tensegrity structure. Ingber demonstrated that cells themselves are tensegrity structures: a prestressed cytoskeleton in which microtubules resist compression while actin filaments bear tension.^12,15^

The central stability mechanism of the glenohumeral joint is concavity compression. The rotator cuff compresses the humeral head into the glenoid concavity. The deeper the concavity and the greater the compressive force, the more translational force is required to dislocate the joint. Matsen quantified this as the stability ratio: 50 to 60 percent in the superior-inferior direction and 30 to 35 percent in the anterior-posterior direction.^16^ His critical distinction was that the cuff's primary stability function is compression, not depression: "the primary stabilizing function of the cuff muscles is through head compression into the glenoid."^6^

The glenohumeral joint remains stable as long as the net humeral joint reaction force is directed within the effective glenoid arc.^10^ Anything that reduces the concavity or redirects the force vector narrows this margin. A negative pressure normally exists within the joint; equalize it by arthrotomy and inferior subluxation occurs.^16^ Pre-stress is a defining feature of tensegrity architecture.

[**Figure 1: Scapulothoracic force couples** — Neumann DA, Kinesiology of the Musculoskeletal System, 2017]

The scapula is the only major bone in the shoulder girdle with no bony articulation to the axial skeleton. Its position at any moment is a vector summation of the tension forces in the trapezius, serratus anterior, rhomboids, levator scapulae, pectoralis minor, and the muscles originating from its surfaces. Matsen described two complementary functions: "the glenohumeral muscles strive to direct the joint reaction vector toward the glenoid and the muscles controlling the scapula move the glenoid toward the force."^17^ The first is the cuff compressing the head into the socket. The second is the scapulothoracic system positioning the socket to receive the compression.

The sensitivity of this system has been quantified. A change in thoracic posture alone reduces active shoulder abduction by 23.6 degrees and abduction muscle force by 16.2 percent.^18^ Differences in pectoralis minor resting length among healthy individuals alter scapular kinematics in three rotational planes simultaneously.^19^ Kibler reported that 51 percent of total kinetic energy in a tennis serve originates from the legs and trunk, all transmitted through the scapulothoracic articulation.^20^

The scapula exerts its upstream influence through two mechanistically distinct pathways. The first is static morphology: the innate bony architecture of the acromion and glenoid, including the critical shoulder angle, posterior acromial height, and acromial coverage. Passaplan showed that the CSA does not change over a decade, establishing these as stable, innate features.^2^ Static morphology is not modifiable. The second is dynamic positioning: scapulothoracic orientation during functional movement, governed by the balance of periscapular muscle forces and potentially modifiable through rehabilitation or surgery. Both mechanisms converge at the same outcome: they alter the force environment experienced by downstream structures. The following clinical evidence illustrates how.

## High Critical Shoulder Angle and Rotator Cuff Disease

Nyffeler introduced the acromion index, demonstrating that lateral acromial extension is significantly greater in patients with rotator cuff tears (AI 0.73) than in those with intact cuffs (AI 0.64, P<0.0001).^21^

[**Figure 2: CSA measurement** — Moor BK et al., Bone Joint J, 2013]

Moor developed the critical shoulder angle, which combines lateral acromial extension with glenoid inclination. In 298 shoulders, the mean CSA was 33.1 degrees in controls, 38.0 degrees in rotator cuff tears, and 28.1 degrees in osteoarthritis.^1^ What changes biomechanically with a high CSA is the deltoid's force vector. Viehöfer demonstrated that a CSA of 38 degrees produces a 23 percent higher shear-to-compression ratio compared to 33 degrees.^22^ Gerber confirmed this on a physical simulator: supraspinatus tendon load increases 13 to 33 percent for a CSA of 38 compared to 33 degrees.^23^

[**Figure 3: Force vectors in OA vs RCT shoulders** — Gerber C et al., J Orthop Res, 2014]

The supraspinatus is now caught between two forces. It must generate more tension to counteract deltoid shear. Simultaneously, it is compressed between the humeral head and the acromion, with subacromial pressure rising from 8 mm Hg at rest to 56 mm Hg during loaded elevation.^24^ The tendon responds to this compression by forming fibrocartilage, an adaptation that weakens the tissue in tension.^9^ It is being asked to do more tension work while being remodeled into a tissue less capable of bearing it.

Where does it fail? Not on the bursal surface. On the articular surface, where the glenoid rim applies a shearing load to the deep fibers.^6^ The articular side is also less stiff in compression than the bursal side.^25^ Once fibers begin to fail, the process accelerates: each fiber failure increases the load on neighboring fibers, and exposure to joint fluid containing lytic enzymes compromises any healing response.^6^

Blonna demonstrated a dose-response relationship: isolated supraspinatus tears averaged a CSA of 36 degrees, while combined supraspinatus-infraspinatus tears averaged 40 degrees.^26^ Garcia found that a CSA exceeding 38 degrees produces an odds ratio of 14.8 for full-thickness retear after repair (P<0.01).^27^ A systematic review by Docter corroborated the association in the majority of included studies.^28^

[**Figure 4: Lateral acromioplasty pre/post** — Gerber C et al., Arthroscopy, 2018]

Gerber showed that arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty reduces the CSA from 37.5 to 33.9 degrees (P<0.001) without compromising the deltoid origin. Patients whose CSA was corrected to 33 degrees or less had 25 percent greater abduction strength than those whose CSA remained above 35 (P=0.04), and postoperative CSA was significantly larger in failed repairs than in healed repairs (P=0.026).^29^ Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that not all studies have replicated these findings: Bjarnison found CSA associated with osteoarthritis but not with rotator cuff tears.^30^ The weight of evidence tilts toward clinical relevance, but the debate remains live.

The chain is: high CSA produces increased deltoid shear, which overloads the supraspinatus in tension while compression remodels it into fibrocartilage, leading to articular-side fiber failure and progressive tearing. Correcting the morphology corrects the force environment and improves outcomes.

## Low Critical Shoulder Angle and Osteoarthritis

A low CSA means a shorter acromion and a more medially inclined glenoid. The deltoid's force vector is directed more compressively into the glenoid. The cuff is spared because the shear demand is low. But the articular cartilage receives greater cumulative compression. It thins. Subchondral bone scleroses along the compression vector exactly as Wolff's Law predicts. Once cartilage is lost, the coefficient of friction rises approximately tenfold.^31^ Low-friction compression becomes high-friction shear, and degeneration accelerates.

The inverse relationship between cuff tears and osteoarthritis is not mutual protection. It is pathway selection. The critical shoulder angle determines the ratio of compression to shear. A high ratio preferentially loads the cuff. A low ratio preferentially loads the cartilage. Once one pathway is established, the altered geometry makes the other less likely. The pathologies diverge because they are competing branches of the same mechanical system.

## Posterior Acromial Morphology, Instability, and Glenoid Erosion

The critical shoulder angle operates in the coronal plane. Glenoid version and posterior acromial morphology operate in the axial plane. The principle is identical.

[**Figure 5: Posterior acromial morphology measurements** — Meyer DC et al., JBJS Am, 2019]

Meyer found that posterior acromial height measured 30.9 mm in patients with posterior instability versus 19.5 mm in anterior instability (P<0.001). At a cutoff of 23 mm, the odds ratio for posterior instability was 39.^32^ Akgun expanded this using full-trunk MRI, demonstrating that constitutional static posterior instability (Type C1) involves the entire shoulder girdle: glenoid retroversion averaged 20.1 versus 5.8 degrees, posterior coverage was reduced, and scapulothoracic orientation differed, with less upward rotation, less scapular tilt, and less thoracic kyphosis.^33^ Beeler showed that patients with dynamic and static posterior instability share the same bony morphology, suggesting these represent stages of a single pathway.^34^

Hochreiter tested three-dimensional printed scapula models and found that a high, flat acromial configuration reduced the force required for posterior displacement by 23 to 60 percent. Correcting glenoid retroversion alone was insufficient; both glenoid and acromial anatomy had to be corrected.^35^ Cadaveric validation confirmed that posterior acromial bone grafting restored stability comparable to corrective osteotomy.^36^

Glenoid retroversion converts centered compression into posterior-directed shear. The posterior labrum is sheared off. The posterior capsule, designed for intermittent end-range tension, is chronically loaded and plastically deforms.^10^ Each episode of instability makes the next easier. Once the humeral head subluxates posteriorly, it loads the posterior glenoid eccentrically. Concentrated compression on a small area exceeds the cartilage's adaptive capacity. As it erodes, contact area shrinks and contact pressure rises, accelerating the erosion. Simultaneously, the anterior capsule shortens under reduced tension, creating obligate posterior translation.^31^ The shortened anterior capsule drives more eccentric compression, which drives more erosion, which permits more subluxation.

This prediction is confirmed clinically. Arner showed that patients requiring revision after arthroscopic posterior capsulolabral repair had significantly higher posterior acromial height than those whose repairs succeeded (P=0.04).^37^ Moroder reported that the PACS procedure for constitutional posterior instability improved symptoms but posterior subluxation indices returned to baseline, describing it as "symptomatic therapy that does not reverse the underlying cause."^38^ Addressing the downstream tissue without correcting the upstream force environment produces symptom relief while the mechanical cause persists.

[**Figure 6: Walch B glenoid progression** — Walker KE et al., JBJS Am, 2018]

The connection from instability to arthritis has longitudinal support. Walker followed 65 shoulders with serial CT: 89 percent of B1 glenoids progressed to B2 or B3 over 74 months, while only 19 percent of A1 glenoids progressed at all.^39^ Domos described the Walch B0 glenoid as pre-arthritic posterior subluxation without erosion, occurring in young men with increased retroversion.^40^ If B0 and C1 are the same entity at different stages, then posterior instability in a twenty-five-year-old and the need for total shoulder arthroplasty with a B2 glenoid three decades later are sequential stages of a single morphology-driven cascade. No longitudinal study has tracked B0 glenoids from identification to determine the rate of this progression. This would be among the most important studies this framework motivates.

Gerber has translated this reasoning into practice with the SCOPE procedure, combining glenoid and acromial osteotomy planned using three-dimensional statistical shape models. In nine patients, six of whom had failed prior soft-tissue stabilization, the subjective shoulder value improved from 40 to 87.5 percent. The one failure was attributed to inadequate correction of acromial morphology.^41^

## Cuff Tear Arthropathy: Loss of Concavity Compression

When a rotator cuff tear disrupts the transverse force couple, concavity compression is lost. Parsons demonstrated that an isolated supraspinatus tear produces no significant change in joint reaction force.^42^ The subscapularis and infraspinatus maintain the couple. Once the tear extends to include the infraspinatus, disrupting it, the joint reaction force drops 61 percent and maximum abduction collapses from 85 to 41 degrees.^42^ This is a threshold event, not a gradual decline.

Without the cuff compressing the head into the glenoid, the deltoid's vector goes unopposed. The humeral head migrates superiorly, eroding the superior glenoid through eccentric compression. As the concavity flattens, it permits further migration, which causes further erosion. The system enters a positive feedback loop.

Matsen described the threshold at which this loop becomes irreversible: when the residual cuff tendons slide below the equator of the humeral head, "a shoulder with a buttonholed cuff is victimized by the conversion of balancing forces into unbalancing forces."^6^ The muscles that were compressing the head into the glenoid now pull it superiorly. This is a phase transition: the system flips from stable to catastrophically unstable at a specific anatomic threshold.

The acromion records this mechanical history. The superiorly migrated head loads the coracoacromial ligament in tension, transmitting traction to the undersurface of the acromion. Matsen showed that the type III hooked acromion is "acquired in response to traction forces applied via the CAL and are not congenital."^14^ The hooked acromion is a mechanotransduction artifact, bone remodeling written into the anatomy as a permanent record of the force environment. Capsulorrhaphy arthropathy proves the same mechanism iatrogenically: excessive capsular tightening forces the head out of its concentric relationship, producing eccentric contact and accelerated wear.^31^

## Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty and Scapulothoracic Overload

When a reverse prosthesis eliminates the cuff and medializes the center of rotation, the demand shifts to the scapulothoracic system. Walker measured scapulohumeral rhythm across three implant designs and found that the ratio drops from 3:1 in the normal shoulder to 1.3:1 after rTSA.^43^ The scapula must contribute roughly double its normal share of arm elevation. Kawashima found that patients with an SHR of 2 or greater had significantly higher functional scores than those below 2 (ASES 94.3 versus 82.1, P=0.007).^44^

The significance of persistent scapulothoracic dysfunction is underscored by Mercurio, who showed that scapular dyskinesis persists after successful rotator cuff repair despite improved range of motion and Constant scores.^45^ If dyskinesis were simply a consequence of the cuff tear, it would resolve after repair. The fact that it does not suggests the scapulothoracic dysfunction is upstream of the cuff pathology.

[**Figure 7: Scapulohumeral rhythm measurement** — Walker D et al., JSES, 2015]

If the periscapular muscles cannot meet the doubled demand, the functional ceiling is set not by the implant but by the upstream system that positions it. Gibbons showed that massive rotator cuff tears produce sarcomere loss and fiber shortening, with sarcomere length below the optimal operating range in massive tears.^46^ If similar remodeling occurs in the periscapular muscles of patients with chronic dysfunction, the scapulothoracic system may be structurally committed to its current state before surgery is performed, providing a rationale for prehabilitation targeting sarcomere architecture rather than conventional strengthening.

## The Unifying Principle

Five pathologies. Five different tissues failing. In every case, the same mechanism: a tissue received a force it was not designed to bear.

[**Figure 8: Unifying framework schematic** — original figure]

The supraspinatus, designed for tension, received compression and adapted by forming fibrocartilage, then failed when asked to bear increased tensile load. Articular cartilage, designed for distributed compression, received cumulative overload and thinned. The posterior labrum, designed to deepen the glenoid concavity, received shear and detached. The posterior capsule, designed for end-range tension, was chronically loaded and deformed. The superior glenoid received eccentric compression from a migrated humeral head and eroded. The periscapular muscles, asked to double their contribution after rTSA, may be limited by architectural remodeling that preceded the operation.

In every case, the tissue responded correctly to the information it received. It adapted its structure and composition to match its mechanical environment, exactly as Wolff, Davis, and Frost described. What clinicians call pathology is the tissue doing what it was designed to do, in an environment it was not designed for.

If this framing is correct, durable treatment requires correcting the mechanical environment, not simply repairing the tissue that adapted to it. Gerber's lateral acromioplasty corrects the CSA and reduces retear rates.^29^ His SCOPE procedure corrects glenoid and acromial morphology in patients who failed soft-tissue stabilization.^41^ Arner showed that adverse acromial morphology predicts failure of posterior repair.^37^ The tissue repair fails because the upstream force environment was not addressed.

## Falsification Criteria and Directions for Investigation

A framework that cannot be proven wrong is not a framework. It is an opinion.

First, if the CSA determines the compression-to-shear ratio, then surgical correction below 28 degrees should paradoxically increase osteoarthritis risk by shifting the force environment to compression-dominant loading. If lateral acromioplasty reduces cuff retears but patients subsequently develop accelerated articular cartilage wear, the framework is supported. If they do not, the relationship is more complex than the framework assumes.

Second, pre-operative scapulothoracic kinematics should independently predict outcomes after rTSA. A prospective study measuring three-dimensional scapulothoracic motion before surgery, correlated with functional outcomes at one and two years, would test whether the upstream system limits the downstream result.

Third, a prospective cohort of B0 glenoids tracked with serial imaging should show progression to B2 at rates predicted by the degree of glenoid retroversion and posterior acromial height. If progression is independent of morphology, the mechanical pathway is not the primary driver.

Fourth, dynamic glenoid version during functional loading should differ between patients who develop posterior glenoid wear and those who do not. Bryce showed that scapular rotation changes measured glenoid version by up to 22.5 degrees.^47^ Malavolta found significant kinematic differences during motion in Walch B patients, including 15 degrees greater anterior tilt during internal rotation at 90 degrees of elevation, though no resting alignment difference.^48^ Measurement of glenoid orientation using biplanar fluoroscopy during loaded functional tasks, correlated with subsequent wear on serial imaging, would establish whether the scapulothoracic system contributes a modifiable component to posterior glenoid loading.

Each of these studies is feasible. Each tests a specific claim. Each, if the result is negative, would force revision or abandonment of the corresponding part of the framework.

## Limitations

This paper emphasizes extrinsic mechanical factors. Intrinsic tendon biology, genetic predisposition, vascular insufficiency, and acute trauma contribute independently to shoulder pathology and are not displaced by this framework. The CSA-cuff tear association has not been replicated in all cohorts.^30^ Some morphological associations may reflect shared genetic or developmental programs rather than direct mechanical causation. The B0-to-B2 progression hypothesis rests on cross-sectional morphological data and limited longitudinal evidence; no study has tracked B0 glenoids specifically, and causation between scapulothoracic orientation and posterior wear has not been established. Frost acknowledged that the mechanostat's specific thresholds for non-osseous tissues remain undefined.^8^ What the framework claims is that the principle of threshold-dependent adaptation to specific force types, established for bone, operates in the shoulder's soft tissues as well, and that the scapula's morphology and position determine which tissue reaches its threshold first.

## Conclusion

The scapulothoracic articulation is a compression element suspended in a continuous tension network. Its static morphology and its dynamic position determine the force environment of every downstream structure. The critical shoulder angle governs the compression-to-shear ratio in the coronal plane. Posterior acromial morphology governs posterior restraint in the axial plane. When a tissue receives a force it was not designed for, it adapts predictably, and what we call disease is the consequence of that adaptation. Whether this represents a single biomechanical reality or coincidental convergence cannot be determined from existing literature alone. But the predictions are specific, the studies are feasible, and the clinical implications are already being tested. The questions this framework raises are worth answering.
