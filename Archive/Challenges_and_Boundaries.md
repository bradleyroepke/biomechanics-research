# Challenges, Counterevidence, and Honest Boundaries

*What threatens the framework, what it cannot explain, and where we may be fooling ourselves.*
*Updated: 2026-03-25*

---

## Confirmation Bias Risk

This framework was developed by reading literature through the lens of "does this support the mechanical adaptation thesis?" That framing inherently biases toward finding support. Every finding has been interpreted through the framework's logic. The same data, read by someone with a different thesis, might support a different conclusion. The counterevidence below was sought deliberately to test whether the framework survives hostile scrutiny.

---

## Serious Threats to the Framework

### 1. Genetics may matter more than morphology
- Twin studies: rotator cuff disease heritability ~18%, shared environment 44%, unique environment 38%
- Siblings of cuff tear patients: 2.85x risk of developing tears
- GWAS: specific genetic variants identified (DEFB1, FGFR1, FGF3, RUNX1, ADAM12, TGFBR2)
- **The alternative explanation:** CSA might correlate with cuff tears not because it drives force, but because the genes that produce a certain scapular shape also produce inherently weaker tendon. CSA would then be a genetic marker, not a mechanical cause.
- **What would distinguish:** Prospective interventional data. If changing the CSA (lateral acromioplasty) changes the outcome, it's causal. If it doesn't, it may be a marker.

### 2. The CSA-cuff tear association is weaker than we've been treating it
- 2021 meta-analysis: heterogeneity I² = 93%, 95% prediction interval includes no difference
- Mean difference ~3.3 degrees (smaller in larger, better-designed studies)
- Bjarnison 2017: no association at all (OR 1.12, p = .63)
- Effect sizes larger in smaller samples and higher-risk-of-bias studies — classic pattern of inflated effects
- **What this means:** The AUC of 0.92 from Moor may overstate the true discriminatory power. The real effect may be weaker and less consistent than our paper implies.

### 3. CSA measurement is clinically unreliable
- 5-degree scapular anteversion error changes CSA by >2 degrees
- Up to 76% of clinical radiographs don't meet quality standards
- Disease group differences (3-5 degrees) overlap with measurement error
- **What this means:** Even if the biology is right, the clinical tool may be too imprecise to apply

### 4. "Association does not imply causation" (2020 editorial)
- Direct argument: treating CSA as causal is like assuming leg length causes basketball ability
- CSA may be an epiphenomenon of deeper genetic/developmental programming
- All studies to date are retrospective and cross-sectional

### 5. Intrinsic hypoxia may be the primary driver
- HIF-1alpha and VEGF elevated in torn cuff tendons
- Hypoxia-to-apoptosis cascade operates independently of external loading
- The vascular "critical zone" creates vulnerability independent of morphology
- **The alternative:** Tendon degenerates because of poor blood supply, not because of force. Mechanical loading is secondary or permissive.

### 6. Wolff's Law is an oversimplification
- Modern bone biology: genetic regulation, hormonal signaling, osteocyte networks all modulate remodeling
- Mechanostat thresholds are "genetically determined internal standards" (Frost's own words)
- If thresholds are genetic, calling the framework "mechanical" understates genetic regulation

## Conditions the Framework CANNOT Explain

### Fully outside the framework:
- **Rheumatoid arthritis:** Immune-mediated, not mechanical. RA hits small joints (MCPs, PIPs) first and worst, NOT the most mechanically loaded joints. If force determined severity, hips/knees should be destroyed first. They're not. The primary driver is immunological.
- **Septic arthritis:** Infection. Mechanics irrelevant to cause.
- **Avascular necrosis:** Vascular, not mechanical primary etiology.
- **Tumors:** Genetic/cellular. Not mechanical.
- **Parsonage-Turner syndrome:** Idiopathic brachial plexus neuritis. Likely immune/viral.
- **Crystalline arthropathy:** Metabolic crystal deposition.

### Partially outside (mechanics modifies but doesn't cause):
- **RA pattern:** Even though immune-driven, mechanics determines WHERE within RA damage is worst and which deformities develop (ulnar drift follows unresisted force vectors). The fire is immunological; the pattern is mechanical.
- **Adhesive capsulitis:** Fibrotic phase may be mechanical (absence of stretch signal → contracture). But inflammatory phase preceding it may be chemical, not mechanical.
- **Traumatic injuries:** The injury = force exceeding material strength. Framework doesn't explain the injury. But everything AFTER (stiffness, osteopenia, muscle wasting, compensatory changes) = tissue adapting to altered mechanical environment.

## The "Rational Adaptation" Labeling Problem

One of the most serious philosophical challenges: is calling pathology "rational adaptation" actually adding explanatory power, or is it just renaming degeneration?

**The test:** Does the reframe change clinical decisions? If it leads to treating the mechanical environment (CSA correction, scapulothoracic optimization) rather than just the tissue (repair, debridement), then it's not just a label. If it doesn't change what we do, it's philosophy.

Currently: the evidence that changing the mechanical environment changes outcomes is preliminary (Gerber's acromioplasty data, no RCT). Until an RCT shows boundary-condition correction improves outcomes, the "rational adaptation" framing remains a hypothesis that generates predictions, not a proven clinical paradigm.

## The Continuous Network / Tensegrity Caveat

The tensegrity model is conceptual scaffolding. The specific Snelson stick-and-cable architecture is almost certainly wrong as a literal description. Bones are not floating. Joints have rigid contacts. The useful content is:
- Baseline prestress exists throughout connective tissue
- Forces propagate through the continuous network
- Changing geometry at one point alters the mechanical environment at distant points

Whether you call this tensegrity, biotensegrity, or just connective tissue mechanics doesn't matter. What matters is whether forces distribute continuously and cells adapt to what they receive. The evidence for that is strong regardless of the structural model.

## Things We Predicted That Turned Out to Be More Complex

### "CSA cleanly separates two diseases"
Reality: the meta-analysis shows high heterogeneity. Mantell showed 48% of dual-pathology tears below 35 degrees. The separation is probabilistic, not deterministic. The bifurcation model may be an idealization of something more continuous.

### "Mechanical information is the primary channel"
Reality: Engler showed chemistry and mechanics synergize. Neither alone is sufficient. The honest framing is "necessary co-determinant," not "primary."

### "Tissue response to yoga / end-range loading"
Reality: Hypermobile patients may not benefit from end-range loading. The response depends on the individual's baseline tissue compliance, which is genetically determined. The same intervention is beneficial for some and harmful for others depending on where they start.

### "RA should follow mechanical patterns"
Reality: RA hits small joints first, not the most loaded joints. Mechanics modifies the pattern but doesn't drive it. This is an honest limitation, not a failure of the framework — it simply defines the boundary.

---

## The Honest Version of the Framework

The framework's contribution is not "mechanics causes everything." It is:

**Mechanical information has been systematically underweighted relative to genetics and chemistry in how we think about degenerative musculoskeletal disease. Incorporating it explains patterns that current models cannot account for, generates testable predictions that current models do not, and suggests interventions that current models do not consider.**

That claim is defensible. The stronger claim ("pathology is adaptation") is a useful reframe that may or may not survive prospective testing. The strongest claim ("the body is a tensegrity network where all pathology follows mechanical logic") is almost certainly overstated.

We should present the defensible version. Not the strongest version.
