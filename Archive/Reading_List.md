# Mechanotransduction Framework — Reading List

Organized by conceptual layer, from deepest foundations upward.

---

## Layer 0: Foundations (Thermodynamics, Emergence, Information)

### Must-Read
- [ ] **Anderson PW** (1972) "More is Different" *Science* 177:393-396
  - 4 pages. The definitive statement on emergence. Why reductionism alone cannot explain complex systems. Start here.
- [ ] **Prigogine I** (1977) Nobel Lecture: "Time, Structure, and Fluctuations"
  - Dissipative structures — systems maintained far from equilibrium by continuous energy expenditure. This is what living tissue *is*.
- [ ] **D'Arcy Thompson W** (1917) *On Growth and Form*
  - The grandfather text. Physical forces constrain biological form. Public domain.

### Supporting
- [ ] Prigogine I, Stengers I. *Order Out of Chaos* (1984) — Book-length treatment of dissipative structures
- [ ] Schrödinger E. *What is Life?* (1944) — The original question of how physics constrains biology

## Layer 1: Scaling Laws & Universality

### Must-Read
- [ ] **West GB, Brown JH, Enquist BJ** (1997) "A General Model for the Origin of Allometric Scaling Laws in Biology" *Science* 276:122-126
  - Why metabolic rate scales as 3/4 power of mass. Fractal distribution networks. The toolkit for asking whether mechanical adaptation follows universal laws.
- [ ] **West GB, Brown JH, Enquist BJ** (1999) "The Fourth Dimension of Life: Fractal Geometry and Allometric Scaling of Organisms" *Science* 284:1677-1679

### Supporting
- [ ] West GB. *Scale* (2017) — Book-length treatment, very readable
- [ ] Bak P, Tang C, Wiesenfeld K (1987) "Self-organized criticality: An explanation of the 1/f noise" *Physical Review Letters* 59:381-384
  - Systems that spontaneously organize to critical states. Relevant to understanding why tissues exist at mechanical thresholds.
- [ ] Bak P. *How Nature Works* (1996) — Book-length treatment of self-organized criticality

## Layer 5: The Synthesis (Our Framework)

### The claim
Pathology is not tissue failure. It is the correct cellular response to corrupted mechanical information. Tissue always does the right thing given its inputs. The inputs are wrong.

### Key evidence needed
- Engler 2006 (substrate stiffness → lineage specification)
- Embryological mechanical gradient data
- Clinical demonstration across multiple tissue types (scapula, tendon, cartilage)

### The question to answer
Is there a universal relationship between mechanical signal and biological response that holds across tissues, scales, and timescales? If yes → theory. If no → useful framework.
