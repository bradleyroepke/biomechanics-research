## I. Scapular Morphology and Shoulder Pathology

### Critical Shoulder Angle

1. **Does CSA predict rotator cuff repair failure, and should it change surgical planning?**
   - Supporting: Garcia 2017 (OR 14.8 for retear if CSA >38°), Li 2018, Scheiderer 2018
   - Contradicting: Lee 2017 (no outcome association at 24 months), Como 2021, Arnar 2017 (no RCT association)
   - Gerber 2018 showed lateral acromioplasty can correct CSA safely
   - Status: Debated. Weight of evidence tilts toward CSA mattering, but not universal.
   - **Needed:** Prospective RCT comparing cuff repair ± lateral acromioplasty stratified by CSA

2. **Is there a CSA threshold above which lateral acromioplasty should be standard at time of cuff repair?**
   - Gerber 2018 used CSA ≥34° as threshold
   - Olmos 2021 showed acromioplasty cannot sufficiently reduce CSA if >40°
   - **Needed:** Multi-center prospective study with standardized CSA cutoffs and standardized lateral acromioplasty technique

3. **Does CSA predict primary cuff tear development in a prospective cohort?**
   - Passaplan 2021 showed CSA is stable over 10+ years (innate, not acquired)
   - Moor 2013, Davide 2016 showed cross-sectional association
   - **Needed:** Prospective longitudinal cohort — measure CSA in young adults, follow for cuff tear development over decades

4. **Can CSA be used as a screening tool for cuff tear risk?**
   - Mantell 2017: CSA >35° is 90% specific and 52% sensitive for RCT in OA patients (AUC 0.84)
   - **Needed:** Validation of CSA screening in asymptomatic populations

### Posterior Acromial Morphology

5. **Does correcting posterior acromial morphology reliably treat posterior instability?**
   - Meyer 2019: PAH ≥23mm → OR 39 for posterior instability
   - Hochreiter 2023: glenoid correction alone insufficient — must correct acromion
   - Gerber 2025 SCOPE: n=9, preliminary, 1 failure, 6/9 revisions from failed prior approaches
   - **Needed:** Larger prospective series with longer follow-up for SCOPE procedure

6. **Is posterior acromial morphology assessable on standard radiographs in a way that's clinically practical?**
   - Meyer 2019 used true lateral (Y-view) under fluoroscopic control
   - **Needed:** Reliability study of PAH measurement on standard (non-fluoroscopic) radiographs

## II. Dynamic Glenoid Version and the B0 Cascade

7. **Does scapulothoracic position dynamically alter effective glenoid version during functional movement?**
   - Bryce 2010: scapular rotation changes measured version by up to 22.5° (framed as measurement artifact)
   - **Needed:** Biplanar fluoroscopy study measuring dynamic glenoid version during functional tasks (reaching, overhead, carrying) vs. static CT version. Compare in normal controls.

8. **Do patients with Walch B0 glenoids have different scapulothoracic orientation than matched controls?**
   - Akgun 2024: C1 patients (≈B0) have less upward rotation, less scapular tilt, different acromial morphology
   - Akgun found LESS thoracic kyphosis in C1 — partially contradicts posture-driven hypothesis
   - **Needed:** Replication in a larger cohort, with dynamic kinematic assessment (not just resting CT position)

9. **Does chronic functional retroversion from scapulothoracic malposition predict posterior glenoid cartilage changes over time?**
   - Domos 2017: described B0 → B1 → B2 progression
   - **Needed:** Prospective cohort — measure scapulothoracic kinematics + glenoid cartilage status (MRI) at baseline and follow longitudinally. The definitive study, but requires long follow-up.

10. **Is the B0 phenotype preventable or modifiable through scapulothoracic intervention?**
    - Completely untested
    - **Needed:** Identification of B0 patients, randomized to scapulothoracic rehab vs. observation, followed for glenoid progression

11. **After glenoid component implantation (aTSA), does scapulothoracic malposition create dynamic asymmetric loading on the prosthetic glenoid?**
    - Moroder 2020: scapulothoracic orientation influences optimal humeral component retrotorsion
    - **Needed:** Computational model or in-vivo biplanar fluoroscopy study of prosthetic glenoid loading as a function of scapulothoracic position

## III. Scapulothoracic Function and Arthroplasty Outcomes

### Pre-operative Assessment

12. **Does pre-operative scapular dyskinesis predict worse outcomes after rotator cuff repair?**
    - Mercurio 2026: dyskinesis persists after cuff repair (SHR unchanged, p>.05)
    - **Nobody has published this.** This is the most accessible study to do.
    - **Needed:** Prospective cohort — assess scapular kinematics pre-operatively, correlate with post-operative outcomes (ASES, retear rate)

13. **Does pre-operative scapular dyskinesis predict worse outcomes after rTSA?**
    - Walker 2015: rTSA demands more from ST system (SHR 1.3:1)
    - Kawashima 2025: better SHR → better outcomes (n=20)
    - Shekhbihi 2025: calls this "the scapulothoracic conundrum" and identifies it as an open question
    - **Needed:** Prospective cohort — measure pre-op scapular kinematics and periscapular muscle strength, correlate with rTSA outcomes at 1-2 years

14. **Should pre-operative scapulothoracic assessment be incorporated into arthroplasty planning software?**
    - Moroder 2020, 2022: showed scapulothoracic orientation affects optimal component positioning
    - Bauer 2023: identified scapular posture as an unaddressed variable in rTSA planning
    - Current planning software does not account for scapulothoracic position
    - **Needed:** Computational study showing how different scapulothoracic positions change optimal component orientation

### Implant Selection

15. **Should rTSA lateralization be matched to pre-operative scapulothoracic reserve ("scapula-sparing" implant selection)?**
    - Walker 2015: all 3 implant designs showed SHR ~1.3:1, but designs differed in lateralization
    - Levin 2024: implant configuration affects muscle-tendon lengths (deltoid originates from scapula)
    - **Needed:** Comparative study — outcomes of lateralized vs. medialized rTSA stratified by pre-operative scapulothoracic function

16. **Does implant lateralization differentially affect scapulothoracic demands?**
    - Theoretical: lateralized design might restore more GH contribution, reducing ST demands
    - **Needed:** Kinematic study (biplanar fluoroscopy) comparing SHR across lateralized vs. medialized rTSA designs

### Scapular Notching

17. **Does pre-operative scapular dyskinesis predict scapular notching rate, independent of implant positioning?**
    - Simovitch 2007: 44% notching rate, correlated with glenosphere-scapular neck angle
    - Notching is a dynamic event — scapular position during movement determines pillar position
    - **Needed:** Prospective study — pre-op scapular kinematics + post-op notching rate, controlling for glenosphere position

## IV. Scapular Rehabilitation

18. **Does scapular prehab before cuff repair improve surgical outcomes?**
    - Camargo 2015: RCT showed scapular rehab improved kinematics/function/pain in impingement
    - Burkhart 2003: 100% return with scapular rehab alone for SICK scapula
    - **Needed:** RCT — standard pre-op protocol vs. 6-8 week scapular prehab program before cuff repair, outcomes at 1 year

19. **Does scapular prehab before rTSA improve outcomes?**
    - Completely untested
    - Theoretical basis: pre-op rehabilitation could restore sarcomere architecture (Gibbons 2016) before imposing new demands
    - **Needed:** Randomized pilot — standard rTSA rehab vs. 6-12 week scapular prehab + standard rehab

20. **Is there a minimum duration of scapular prehab needed for sarcomere remodeling?**
    - Williams & Goldspink: sarcomere adaptation takes weeks in animal models
    - Burkhart 2003: 50% scapular repositioning in 2-3 weeks, full repositioning in 3 months
    - **Needed:** Serial kinematic or imaging assessment during structured prehab to define the time course of scapulothoracic normalization

21. **Should post-operative rehabilitation after rTSA specifically target scapular stabilizers?**
    - Walker 2015: "particular attention to the periscapular muscles" in rTSA rehab
    - **Needed:** RCT — standard rTSA rehab vs. scapular-targeted rTSA rehab, outcomes at 1-2 years

## V. Tensegrity and Biomechanical Modeling

22. **Does the scapulothoracic muscle attachment pattern match tensegrity-optimized configurations?**
    - Chen & Ingber 1999: tensegrity minimizes metabolic cost through tension-dominant architecture
    - Roboticists have built tensegrity shoulders (Baltaxe-Admony 2016, Li 2022) with solutions resembling anatomy
    - **Needed:** Computational optimization study — given scapula geometry and motion requirements, derive minimum-energy muscle arrangement using tensegrity math, compare to actual anatomy

23. **Can a computational tensegrity model of the scapulothoracic system predict clinical kinematic patterns?**
    - Qualitative predictions match: pre-stress dependence (Kebaetse 1999), global reorganization (Borstad 2005, Burkhart 2003), self-stabilization (Mercurio 2026)
    - **Needed:** Build a quantitative tensegrity model, computationally "weaken" serratus anterior, and compare predicted scapular position changes to Ludewig 2000's measured kinematics

24. **Does the scapulothoracic system exhibit strain-hardening (a tensegrity signature)?**
    - Cellular tensegrity demonstrates strain-hardening: stiffness increases with strain (Wang 2001, PNAS)
    - **Needed:** Biomechanical study measuring scapulothoracic stiffness as a function of applied load — does it show the strain-hardening curve characteristic of tensegrity?

25. **Can tensegrity modeling predict scapulothoracic demands after rTSA?**
    - Model native scapulothoracic tensegrity, then replace GH joint with rTSA geometry
    - Predict new tension requirements for each scapular stabilizer
    - Compare to Walker 2015 SHR data and Kawashima 2025 outcome data
    - **Needed:** Computational study — collaboration with biomedical engineer

## VI. Evolutionary and Mechanotransduction Questions

26. **Is the scapulothoracic muscle attachment pattern consistent with evolutionary optimization for metabolic efficiency?**
    - Kuhn 2016: shoulder evolved for throwing
    - Selby 2017: two fundamental scapular changes during hominoid evolution
    - Chen & Ingber 1999: tensegrity minimizes mass for given structural requirements
    - **Needed:** Comparative anatomy study — scapular muscle attachment patterns across primates mapped onto tensegrity optimization models

27. **Does chronic scapulothoracic malposition produce measurable mechanotransduction changes in periscapular muscles?**
    - Ingber 1997, 2003, 2014: cellular tensegrity mediates mechanotransduction — chronic abnormal loading → altered gene expression
    - Gibbons 2016: cuff tears produce sarcomere loss and fatty infiltration
    - **Needed:** Biopsy or advanced imaging (MR spectroscopy?) of periscapular muscles in patients with chronic dyskinesis vs. matched controls — looking for architectural changes, fatty infiltration, or altered sarcomere properties

28. **Are there measurable differences in scapulothoracic biomechanics between modern sedentary humans and physically active populations?**
    - Kuhn 2016: shoulder designed for intermittent high-performance use
    - Modern use case (sedentary, forward-oriented) differs from evolutionary design parameters
    - **Needed:** Cross-cultural or occupational study comparing scapulothoracic kinematics and pathology rates

## VII. Assessment and Measurement

29. **Can objective scapular assessment tools (wearable sensors, machine learning) replace unreliable visual assessment?**
    - Vila-Dieguez 2026: visual dyskinesia assessment Kappa = 0.12
    - Mercurio 2026 used magnetic inertial measurement units (3D kinematic tracking)
    - **Needed:** Validation study of wearable sensor-based scapular kinematic assessment against gold standard (bone pins or biplanar fluoroscopy)

30. **Should the Scapular Assistance Test be standardized and validated as a clinical outcome measure?**
    - Seitz 2012: SAT changes subacromial space but not cuff strength
    - Currently no standardized protocol or scoring system
    - **Needed:** Reliability study of standardized SAT protocol, followed by validity study against kinematic assessment

## VIII. Tensegrity Predictions Beyond the Shoulder

### Lateral Epicondylitis as a Tensegrity Vulnerability

31. **Is the lateral epicondyle a high-concentration tension node that is specifically vulnerable under the tensegrity model?**
    - The lateral epicondyle is the convergence point for 5-6 tension elements (ECRB, ECRL, EDC, EDM, ECU, supinator) on a single small area of compression element (distal humerus)
    - In tensegrity, nodes with more cable attachments bear more stress — the lateral epicondyle is one of the highest-concentration tension nodes in the upper extremity
    - Additionally a force redirection point — stress concentration at direction changes is basic engineering
    - Scarr 2012 specifically proposed tensegrity relevance to lateral epicondylitis but did not test it
    - **Needed:** Computational tensegrity model of the elbow comparing predicted stress concentration at the lateral epicondyle vs. other nodes

32. **Does the ECRB's "dual loading violation" explain its specific vulnerability?**
    - In a tensegrity system, cables should bear tension only — never compression
    - The ECRB's deep surface contacts and slides against the lateral edge of the capitellum during pronation/wrist extension — simultaneously loaded in tension AND subjected to local compression
    - This dual loading violates the tensegrity design principle — the ECRB is operating outside its structural design parameters
    - This may explain why the ECRB specifically (not EDC, not ECU) is the most common site of tendinopathy at the lateral epicondyle
    - **Needed:** Biomechanical measurement of ECRB compressive contact pressure against capitellum as a function of forearm position and wrist extension force

33. **Do patients with lateral epicondylitis have a higher prevalence of scapular dysfunction than matched controls?**
    - If the upper extremity functions as a hierarchical tensegrity (scapula → shoulder → elbow → wrist), then upstream dysfunction should increase stress at downstream nodes
    - The lateral epicondyle, as the highest-concentration tension node at the elbow, would be the predicted site of failure when the kinetic chain is compromised upstream
    - This could explain: (a) why some people develop epicondylitis while others doing the same activities don't, (b) why epicondylitis is notoriously recurrent when treated with only local interventions, (c) why desk workers develop it despite low "overuse" loads — postural/scapulothoracic dysfunction may be the upstream driver
    - **Needed:** Cross-sectional study comparing scapulothoracic kinematics and periscapular muscle function in lateral epicondylitis patients vs. matched controls

34. **Does addressing upstream kinetic chain dysfunction reduce lateral epicondylitis recurrence?**
    - Conventional treatment (cortisone, PRP, eccentric loading, bracing) is local
    - If upstream scapulothoracic dysfunction increases elbow loading, local treatment alone addresses the consequence, not the cause
    - **Needed:** RCT comparing standard local treatment vs. local treatment + kinetic chain optimization (scapular rehab, postural correction) for lateral epicondylitis recurrence rates

### The Scapular Protraction → ECRB Pathway

35. **Do patients with lateral epicondylitis have more forearm pronation during functional tasks than matched controls, and does it correlate with scapular protraction?**
    - Mechanical chain: scapular protraction → altered glenoid orientation (Bryce 2010) → compensatory humeral IR → increased forearm pronation → increased ECRB-capitellum contact (Tanaka 2011) → chronic dual loading
    - Measurable with wearable sensors (magnetic inertial units) on forearm + scapula simultaneously
    - **Needed:** Cross-sectional kinematic study — lateral epicondylitis patients vs. controls, measuring resting forearm pronation, pronation during keyboard/tool use, and scapular protraction. Test for correlation between scapular protraction and forearm pronation.

36. **Is the ECRB-capitellum contact pressure curve steep or flat with respect to forearm rotation angle?**
    - Tanaka 2011 measured contact pressure at different positions, but the SLOPE of the pressure-rotation curve determines whether small upstream changes (a few degrees from scapular protraction) are clinically meaningful
    - If steep: even small positional changes from scapular dysfunction matter. If flat: the upstream mechanism is quantitatively insignificant.
    - **Needed:** Re-analysis of Tanaka's data or new cadaveric study with finer angular resolution (1° increments) to characterize the pressure-rotation relationship

37. **Does scapular protraction correction reduce forearm pronation and ECRB loading during functional tasks?**
    - If correcting scapular position (via SAT or rehab) measurably reduces forearm pronation during constrained tasks, this closes the causal loop
    - **Needed:** Within-subjects design — measure forearm pronation during keyboard use with and without manual scapular correction (SAT), quantify the change

## IX. Rotator Cable-Crescent and Tensegrity Integration

### Cable-Crescent as Tensegrity Subsystem

38. **Does cable integrity correlate with scapulothoracic function?**
    - Burkhart 1993 (384 cites): the cable redistributes force across the cuff like a tensegrity cross-link
    - Denard 2012 (79 cites): cable integrity — not tear size — determines pseudoparalysis
    - If scapulothoracic dysfunction alters cuff loading angles, the cable experiences abnormal stress
    - Patients with poor scapulothoracic kinematics should have more cable attenuation
    - **Needed:** Cross-sectional study — MRI cable morphology assessment + scapulothoracic kinematic analysis in cuff tear patients

39. **Does CSA predict cable degeneration specifically (not just tear occurrence)?**
    - Gerber 2014: supraspinatus tendon load is dependent on CSA
    - Viehöfer 2016: high CSA → 23% more shear
    - High CSA alters the shear-to-compression ratio, which would change cable loading specifically
    - **Needed:** CSA measurement + MRI cable morphology grading — does high CSA correlate with cable attenuation?

40. **Does cable integrity at time of repair predict retear independently of tear size and repair quality?**
    - If the cable is the critical tensegrity cross-link, its status determines whether the repair has a functional force-distribution mechanism
    - A repaired crescent with an attenuated cable has no cross-link to redistribute load → higher retear risk
    - **Needed:** Prospective study — intraoperative cable assessment (visual grading) + retear surveillance at 12-24 months, controlling for tear size, repair technique, and CSA

41. **Can scapular rehabilitation improve function in symptomatic partial/small full-thickness cuff tears without surgical repair?**
    - If scapulothoracic pre-stress determines how well the cable redistributes load, optimizing scapulothoracic function should improve compensation
    - Burkhart 2003 Part III: 100% return with scapular rehab for SICK scapula
    - Camargo 2015: RCT showing scapular rehab improved impingement outcomes
    - **Needed:** RCT — symptomatic small cuff tears randomized to scapular-focused rehab vs standard cuff-focused rehab vs surgical repair, with functional outcomes at 1 year

### Extrinsic-Intrinsic Integration

42. **Is there a measurable molecular signature of chronic altered mechanotransduction in cuff tendons from patients with scapular dysfunction?**
    - Ingber 1997, 2003: chronic abnormal loading → altered gene expression through tensegrity-mechanotransduction
    - If scapular dyskinesis chronically alters cuff loading patterns, tenocytes should show different gene expression
    - **Needed:** Tendon biopsy at time of cuff repair — compare gene expression profiles (collagen turnover markers, MMP expression, vascular markers) between patients WITH vs WITHOUT pre-operative scapular dyskinesis

43. **Does correcting scapulothoracic position alter the force distribution across the rotator cable in real time?**
    - This would close the loop: prove that scapular position changes cable loading
    - **Needed:** Cadaveric or computational study — measure cable strain distribution as a function of scapulothoracic position (neutral, protracted, anteriorly tilted). If cable strain changes with scapular position, the upstream connection is mechanically proven.

### Pseudoparalysis as Tensegrity Collapse

44. **Does scapulothoracic function independently predict pseudoparalysis in patients with massive cuff tears?**
    - Ernstbrunner 2020: scapular morphology independently predicts pseudoparalysis
    - If the scapulothoracic tensegrity state modulates the critical pre-stress threshold, patients with better ST function should tolerate larger tears without pseudoparalysis
    - **Needed:** Prospective cohort — massive tear patients assessed for scapulothoracic kinematics + cable integrity (MRI) + functional status (pseudoparalysis vs pseudoparesis vs functional). Multivariate analysis: does ST function predict status independently of tear size and cable integrity?

45. **Can scapulothoracic optimization reverse pseudoparalysis without surgery?**
    - Anecdotally reported — some patients recover active elevation through rehab alone
    - Tensegrity framework predicts this is possible by shifting the pre-stress balance back above the critical threshold
    - **Needed:** Prospective case series — patients with pseudoparalysis from massive tears who decline surgery, enrolled in intensive scapulothoracic rehab (12 weeks). Outcome: recovery of active elevation >90°. Even a small series demonstrating reversal would be hypothesis-generating.

46. **Is the transition from pseudoparesis to pseudoparalysis binary (consistent with tensegrity collapse) or gradual?**
    - Tensegrity predicts a sharp threshold — function maintained until critical pre-stress is breached, then rapid collapse
    - **Needed:** Longitudinal kinematic study — patients with progressive massive tears assessed serially. Plot active elevation vs time/tear progression. If the decline is sudden rather than gradual, this supports the tensegrity collapse model.
