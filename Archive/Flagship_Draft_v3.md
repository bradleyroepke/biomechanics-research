# Pathology as Adaptation: A Mechanical Framework for Understanding Morphology-Driven Shoulder Disease

---

## Abstract

Glenohumeral osteoarthritis and rotator cuff tears appear mutually exclusive, yet no mechanistic explanation for this pattern has been proposed. We argue that both conditions represent tissue adaptation to the force environment determined by scapular morphology, specifically the critical shoulder angle. A low angle creates a compression-dominant environment that drives articular cartilage toward osteoarthritis. A high angle creates a shear-dominant environment that overloads the rotator cuff. The tissues in each case are not failing. They are adapting rationally to the mechanical information they receive. We extend this reasoning to posterior acromial morphology and instability, propose the concept of dynamic glenoid version, and generate six testable predictions, including the prediction that over-correction of the critical shoulder angle should paradoxically increase osteoarthritis risk.

---

## 1. The Problem

Between 200,000 and 400,000 rotator cuff repairs are performed annually in the United States.¹ The number keeps rising. The results do not. A systematic review of long-term outcomes reported a 41% retear rate at 14 years.² McElvany and colleagues, reviewing over 8,000 shoulders across 108 studies, concluded that "there is little evidence that the results of rotator cuff repair are improving."³

If the problem were purely technical, if it came down to suture configuration or anchor placement or single-row versus double-row, three decades of refinement should have solved it. It has not.

Gerber posed a set of questions that reframe the issue.⁴ Why does a professional tennis player who serves thousands of times per week not have a rotator cuff tear? Why are supraspinatus tears equally common in the dominant and non-dominant arm? Why are they not more frequent in manual laborers than office workers? And why does a given patient develop either a rotator cuff tear or glenohumeral osteoarthritis, almost never both?

That last question is the most revealing. Moor and colleagues measured the critical shoulder angle on a standard AP radiograph and found it separated rotator cuff tears from osteoarthritis with an AUC of 0.92.⁵ Cuff tears: mean 38 degrees. Osteoarthritis: mean 28 degrees. Controls: 33 degrees.

Blonna and colleagues showed the relationship is dose-dependent.⁶ Isolated supraspinatus tears averaged 36 degrees. Large tears involving both supraspinatus and infraspinatus averaged 40 degrees. Bigger angle, bigger tear. Passaplan and colleagues followed shoulders for twelve years and found no significant change in the angle over time, even in shoulders that developed new tears during follow-up.⁷ The critical shoulder angle is congenital. It does not change with age or disease.

A single measurable angle on a radiograph predicts both the type and severity of degenerative shoulder disease. The question is why. Why does a continuously varying parameter produce two discrete conditions rather than a smooth gradient of mixed pathology? The answer lies in principles that every orthopedic surgeon already accepts.

---

## 2. Familiar Ground

None of what follows is new. Every orthopedic surgeon learned these principles in training and applies them without controversy. The point is not to teach them but to notice what happens when they are taken together.

Wolff recognized in 1892 that bone remodels along lines of mechanical stress.⁸ Trabeculae in the femoral neck align with principal stress directions. A tennis player's dominant humerus may be 20 percent thicker than the non-dominant side. Frost quantified this with the mechanostat: below roughly 200 microstrain, bone resorbs; between 200 and 2,500, it maintains itself; above 2,500, it adds mass; above 3,000, microdamage accumulates faster than it can be repaired.⁹ These are not gradual responses. They are set points that switch different biological programs on and off, like a thermostat. Frost recognized in his 2003 update that the same logic likely applies to "fascia, joint, ligament, tendon, or growth plates," though with threshold values that remain unknown.

The Hueter-Volkmann principle extends this to growth plates: compression inhibits growth, tension stimulates it.¹⁰ Angular deformities progress because asymmetric loading drives asymmetric growth. The growth plate is not malfunctioning. It is responding to its mechanical environment exactly as designed.

Davis's Law describes the same behavior in soft tissue. Ligaments under chronic tension tighten. Tendons under repetitive load hypertrophy. Remove the demand, and the tissue atrophies. Stress shielding under a plate is the same principle: bone correctly reading a reduced load and adjusting accordingly. Cartilage, too, requires cyclic loading to maintain its matrix. Immobilize it, and it degrades.

At the cellular level, the mechanism is direct. Integrins spanning the cell membrane link the extracellular matrix to the cytoskeleton and nucleus, transmitting mechanical forces to the genome in milliseconds, faster than any chemical diffusion pathway.¹¹ Engler and colleagues demonstrated that mesenchymal stem cells on substrates of different stiffness differentiate into neurons on soft substrates, muscle on intermediate, and bone on stiff, with identical chemical conditions.¹² After three weeks, chemistry cannot override what the mechanical environment has specified.

Taken individually, these are uncontroversial observations about bone, cartilage, tendon, and cells. Taken together, they describe a single principle: living tissue reads its mechanical environment and adapts its structure and composition accordingly. The adaptation is directional and threshold-dependent. Within the parameters each tissue is designed for, it is rational.

If tissue always adapts to the forces it experiences, and if scapular morphology determines which forces each tissue experiences, then what we call degenerative shoulder disease may not be tissue failure. It may be tissue doing the right thing in a mechanical environment it was not built for.

## 3. Tipping Points

Shoulder pathology does not develop linearly. A patient is fine for years, then tips. A partial tear is stable, then progresses. A shoulder with mild posterior subluxation is asymptomatic for a decade, then cartilage loss accelerates.

Parsons and colleagues showed why.¹³ Using a dynamic shoulder simulator with an instrumented prosthesis, they progressively sectioned the rotator cuff and measured the joint reaction force after each stage. A complete supraspinatus tear produced no significant change in force magnitude or direction. The transverse force couple, subscapularis balanced against infraspinatus and teres minor, kept the humeral head centered.

When the tear extended to include the infraspinatus, the force dropped 61 percent and shifted 15 degrees. The abduction arc collapsed from 85 to 41 degrees. Not a proportional worsening. A mechanical state change.

Burkhart made the same observation clinically.¹⁴ His distinction between functional and dysfunctional rotator cuff tears is a threshold distinction. A massive tear with an intact transverse force couple maintains concavity compression. The patient functions. The same-sized tear with a disrupted force couple produces pseudoparalysis. What changed was not the amount of tissue lost but which tissue was lost, and whether its loss crossed the line between compensation and collapse.

Gerber extended this to muscle.¹⁵ After tendon release in sheep, muscle architecture degenerates progressively over 16 weeks. The pennation angle increases from 29 to 55 degrees as fibers shorten and reorient. Fatty infiltration appears and, once established, does not reverse with standard repair. This has been treated as irreversible damage.

But Gerber also showed something else.¹⁶ Slow, continuous traction at one millimeter per day, applied over weeks rather than restored in a single surgical act, completely normalized the pennation angle. New sarcomeres were added in series. The fibers lengthened. The architecture that had been called irreversible turned out to be the tissue's response to an absence of longitudinal tension. Restore the tension at the right rate, and the tissue rebuilt itself. Fatty infiltration did not reverse, but its progression stopped. Different tissue compartments within the same muscle had different thresholds for reversibility.

The critical shoulder angle does not produce disease gradually. It sets the mechanical environment, and the tissue adapts within that environment until a threshold is crossed. Once crossed, the adaptation is what clinicians recognize as disease.

## 4. Two Pathways from One Angle

The critical shoulder angle combines glenoid inclination and lateral acromial extension into a single measure. Together, these determine the direction of the deltoid force vector relative to the glenohumeral joint.

A low angle means a short acromion and less-inclined glenoid. The deltoid pulls more directly into the joint. Concavity compression is high. The cartilage bears a chronic compressive load.

A high angle means a long lateral acromion and more-inclined glenoid. The deltoid pulls more superiorly, more shear, less compression. The supraspinatus must compensate to keep the humeral head centered. The cuff bears a chronic tensile and shear load.

Viehofer and colleagues measured this on a robotic simulator: a five-degree change in the angle produced an 8.5 percent change in joint reaction force, with the compression-to-shear ratio averaging about 1.8 to 1 and shifting predictably with the angle.¹⁷ Beeler and colleagues broke the angle into its components and found that lateral acromial roof extension, not glenoid inclination, had the largest effect size for separating cuff tears from osteoarthritis.¹⁸

The low-angle shoulder develops osteoarthritis. Chronically elevated compression thins the cartilage. Subchondral bone stiffens, Wolff's Law applied to the subchondral plate. The joint medializes as the surface remodels toward a new equilibrium. Each step is individually rational. Together they constitute OA.

The high-angle shoulder develops cuff disease. The supraspinatus, the smallest of the four cuff muscles with a work capacity of 4.0 compared to the subscapularis at 15.1,¹⁹ is chronically overloaded. Hashimoto's histologic study of 80 torn cuff stumps found universal degeneration and zero inflammation, worst on the articular surface where intrinsic stress concentrates, not the bursal surface where impingement would act.²⁰ These are not inflammatory changes. They are matrix remodeling driven by the local mechanical environment. When cumulative damage exceeds repair, tearing begins. When the tear disrupts the transverse force couple, the system tips: superior migration, progressive overload, massive failure. The muscle, detached and unloaded, replaces contractile tissue with fat. Metabolically, this is the correct response to absent demand.²¹

The two conditions rarely coexist because each pathway alters the geometry in ways that protect against the other. Medialization in OA shortens the deltoid moment arm, reducing shear on the cuff. Superior migration after a cuff tear unloads the articular surface. They are not two diseases that avoid each other. They are two branches selected by the same parameter.

Mantell and colleagues found that patients who did have both conditions clustered around a critical shoulder angle of 35 degrees, the boundary zone where both pathways are accessible and other factors determine which threshold is reached first.²² Nearly half the concurrent tears occurred below 35 degrees.

The CSA-OA association is robust and replicated by every study, including those that challenge the cuff tear link. The CSA-cuff tear association is supported by most studies but not all. Bjarnison and colleagues found no association in a well-designed case-control study, with a cuff tear group mean of 33.9 degrees, essentially identical to controls.²³ This cannot be dismissed. Their inability to stratify by tear size is a limitation, as Blonna's data show the signal is strongest for large tears, but the dissent is genuine and the question is not fully settled. The biomechanical logic, however, is settled: the angle determines the force vector, and force vectors have consequences.

## 5. The Scapula as a Whole

The critical shoulder angle describes force balance in the coronal plane. The same logic applies in other planes.

Meyer and colleagues found that posterior acromial height predicted the direction of shoulder instability with an odds ratio of 39 when it exceeded 23 millimeters.²⁴ Hochreiter and colleagues confirmed this biomechanically: a high, flat acromion reduced the force needed for posterior displacement by up to 60 percent.²⁵ When Gerber performed combined glenoid and acromial osteotomies to restore normal scapular geometry through the SCOPE procedure, six of nine patients whose prior soft-tissue repairs had failed were stabilized.²⁶ The geometry, not the tissue, had been the problem.

The critical shoulder angle and posterior acromial height together define a morphological map. Different positions on this map predict different pathological endpoints: cuff tears, osteoarthritis, or instability in a specific direction. The map is determined at birth and does not change.

But one parameter does change: scapulothoracic posture. Bryce and colleagues showed that scapular rotation alters the measured glenoid version by up to 22.5 degrees.²⁷ This was reported as a CT measurement artifact. It is also a functional reality. A scapula that sits chronically in more internal rotation presents a more retroverted glenoid during loading. Akgun and colleagues found exactly this pattern in patients with constitutional posterior instability: less scapular upward rotation, half the normal scapular tilt, and a flatter thoracic kyphosis.²⁸

We have not found this concept named in the existing literature. The term dynamic glenoid version describes it: the effective version of the glenoid as determined by the scapula's position on the thorax at the time of loading. Unlike the critical shoulder angle or posterior acromial height, this parameter can be changed. Scapulothoracic rehabilitation, strengthening serratus anterior and lower trapezius, correcting protraction, can change how the scapula presents the glenoid to the humeral head.

After reverse total shoulder arthroplasty, scapulothoracic function becomes the dominant variable. Walker showed the scapulohumeral rhythm drops to 1.3:1.²⁹ Kawashima showed patients with better rhythm had ASES scores of 94.3 versus 82.1, a clinically meaningful difference.³⁰ Mercurio showed scapular dyskinesis persists after successful cuff repair.³¹ Nobody screens for scapulothoracic function before arthroplasty. Nobody targets it specifically in rehabilitation. The scapula is upstream of the problems we are treating, and we are not looking at it.

## 6. Predictions

A framework that cannot be tested is not useful. These six predictions follow from the model, differ from what current models predict, and can each be evaluated with existing or feasible study designs.

**1.** Patients with concurrent osteoarthritis and rotator cuff tears should cluster around a critical shoulder angle of 30 to 35 degrees. Isolated OA should cluster below 30; isolated cuff tears above 35. A retrospective radiographic study of 500 or more classified shoulders can test this.

**2.** Rotator cuff repair combined with lateral acromioplasty reducing the critical shoulder angle to 33 degrees or below should produce lower retear rates at 10-year follow-up than repair alone.

**3.** Over-correction of the critical shoulder angle below 28 degrees should increase the risk of glenohumeral osteoarthritis. No current model predicts this. A retrospective review of acromioplasty patients at long follow-up can test it.

**4.** Scapulothoracic posture at baseline should predict progression from Walch B0 to B1/B2 glenoid morphology on serial imaging.

**5.** Pre-operative scapulothoracic kinematics should independently predict functional outcomes after reverse total shoulder arthroplasty.

**6.** Rehabilitation protocols that reduce compressive loading in lateral epicondylitis should outperform standard eccentric protocols if compressive tendinopathy follows the same mechanical logic.

---

## 7. What This Does Not Explain

This paper presents a way of thinking, not proof. Several points of honesty are required.

Bjarnison's null finding for the CSA-cuff tear association is presented here alongside the supporting data because a model that hides its counterevidence is not worth considering.²³ The balance of evidence favors the association for larger tears, but the question is legitimately open.

Garcia's odds ratio of 14.8 for retear at a critical shoulder angle above 38 degrees is based on eight retears.³² It is suggestive, not settled.

No controlled trial has shown that reducing the critical shoulder angle improves outcomes. Gerber's lateral acromioplasty data suggest it matters, but the study had no control group.³³

Only 21 percent of clinical radiographs are adequate for reliable CSA measurement.³⁴ Clinical application faces a practical measurement problem.

The model applies to degenerative shoulder conditions where mechanical environment is altered by morphology. It does not apply to acute trauma, inflammatory arthritis, or genetic disease. It does not claim all pathology is mechanical.

The prevention hypothesis, that scapulothoracic optimization might delay or prevent disease in morphologically at-risk shoulders, is untested. It follows from the logic but has not been demonstrated.

The observations cited belong to the investigators who made them. What is offered here is a synthesis, a way of seeing work from Gerber, Moor, Frost, Burkhart, Hashimoto, Cook, Engler, and many others as aspects of a single principle rather than separate findings.

---

## 8. A Different Question

We began with a clinical frustration: cuff repairs that keep failing despite better techniques. We end with a reframe.

The critical shoulder angle is congenital, stable, and measurable. It determines the ratio of compression to shear at the glenohumeral joint. Tissues adapt to whichever force dominates, and when the adaptation crosses a threshold, clinicians recognize disease. Low angles drive compression and osteoarthritis. High angles drive shear and cuff tears. They are two responses to one parameter.

If the force environment drives the pathology, then repairing tissue without changing the environment treats the effect and leaves the cause. Correcting the critical shoulder angle, optimizing scapulothoracic function, and assessing scapular kinematics before arthroplasty all follow from this reasoning.

The tissue is not failing. It is adapting. The question worth asking is not why the tissue failed, but what information the tissue received.
