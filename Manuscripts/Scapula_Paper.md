# Rethinking the Scapula: How a Single Upstream Structure May Influence the Spectrum of Shoulder Pathology

Brad Roepke, MD

Department of Orthopedic Surgery, University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, Oklahoma City, OK

Corresponding author: Brad Roepke, MD, Department of Orthopedic Surgery, University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, P.O. Box 26901, Oklahoma City, OK 73126. Email: brad.roepke@example.com

## Abstract

The scapula has no bony articulation with the axial skeleton and is stabilized entirely by the balance of tension forces in the surrounding musculature. Its position is therefore a vector summation of the tensions acting upon it. Because the scapula positions the glenoid, anchors the rotator cuff, and transmits force from the trunk to the arm, alterations in this tension balance influence force distribution across every downstream structure. Recent evidence suggests that the scapula exerts this upstream influence through two mechanisms, its static morphology and its dynamic positioning, producing distinct downstream pathologies depending on which structure is overloaded. We present a framework unifying four clinical examples of this relationship and propose testable predictions for clinical investigation.

Level of Evidence: Level V; Expert Opinion / Narrative Review

Keywords: scapula; scapulothoracic; critical shoulder angle; tensegrity; reverse shoulder arthroplasty; scapulohumeral rhythm; posterior instability; glenoid wear

## Introduction

Shoulder pathology encompasses a broad spectrum of conditions, including rotator cuff tears, glenohumeral instability, osteoarthritis, and arthroplasty complications, each of which is typically studied as a distinct entity with a separate etiology. Yet a convergence has emerged in recent literature that is difficult to ignore: investigations of each pathology independently identify the scapula as a significant contributing factor.

The critical shoulder angle, a measurement of scapular morphology, predicts both rotator cuff tears and osteoarthritis.(1-3) Posterior acromial morphology predicts the direction of glenohumeral instability.(4,5) Scapulothoracic orientation differs significantly in patients with constitutional posterior humeral decentering.(6) Scapulohumeral rhythm after reverse total shoulder arthroplasty (rTSA) correlates with functional outcomes.(7,8) And scapular dyskinesis persists after successful rotator cuff repair, raising the possibility that scapular dysfunction may precede rather than follow cuff pathology.(9)

These findings, taken together, raise a question worth considering: are these separate observations, or are they different manifestations of a single biomechanical reality?

The purpose of this paper is to draw attention to a pattern in the evidence that has not been fully appreciated. The scapula's morphology and its dynamic position influence force distribution to every downstream structure, and the specific pathology that develops in a given patient may depend on which structure bears the consequence. We present this framework as a starting point for discussion and investigation, not as a definitive model.

## The Scapulothoracic Articulation: A Summation of Tension Forces

The scapula is the only major bone in the shoulder girdle with no direct bony articulation to the axial skeleton. It has no joint capsule, no ligamentous attachment to the spine or rib cage. All stability is provided by the surrounding musculature: the serratus anterior, three divisions of the trapezius, rhomboids, levator scapulae, pectoralis minor, and the muscles originating from its surface. Scapular rotation during arm elevation is produced by coordinated force couples, validated through three-dimensional electromagnetic tracking and electromyographic analysis.(12,32,34) This arrangement, a compression element suspended within a continuous tension network, is consistent with tensegrity architecture, a principle validated at the cellular level by Ingber and colleagues(25-27) and extended to the musculoskeletal system by Chen and Ingber.(28)

The scapula's position at any moment is a vector summation of all the tension forces acting upon it. Altering any single tension element changes the summation and shifts the scapula to a new equilibrium position.

The sensitivity of this system has been quantified in several investigations. Kebaetse et al. demonstrated that a change in thoracic posture alone, simply slouching, reduces active shoulder abduction by 23.6 degrees and abduction muscle force by 16.2%.(10) Borstad showed that differences in pectoralis minor resting length among healthy individuals alter scapular kinematics in three rotational planes simultaneously.(11) These are not pathologic populations; they are healthy subjects in whom a single alteration in the tension balance produces measurable kinematic consequences. The subacromial space, as reviewed by Borstad, has been measured at 4.1 mm in healthy subjects at 90 degrees of elevation and found to narrow to 1.4 mm in subjects with impingement,(11) illustrating how small the margin is between functional and pathologic mechanics, and how directly scapulothoracic positioning influences that margin.

The downstream consequences extend beyond kinematics. A sustained positional shift changes every attached muscle's resting length and therefore its position on the length-tension curve, altering force output for a given neural activation.(24) This changes force couple balance,(34) which may alter motor control timing.(35,36) If sustained, structural sarcomere remodeling may reinforce the new position,(24) creating a self-reinforcing pattern of altered mechanics, motor control, and muscle architecture.

The scapula also serves as the sole link between the axial skeleton and upper extremity. Kibler reported that 51% of total kinetic energy in a tennis serve originates from the legs and trunk, all transmitted through the scapulothoracic articulation.(12)

## Two Modes of Upstream Influence

Before examining the clinical evidence, an important distinction must be drawn. The scapula exerts its upstream influence through two mechanistically distinct pathways.

The first is static morphology: the innate bony architecture of the acromion and glenoid. This includes the critical shoulder angle, the posterior acromial height, acromial coverage, and acromial tilt. Passaplan et al. showed that the CSA does not change over a decade, even in shoulders that develop new rotator cuff tears during follow-up, establishing these parameters as stable, innate anatomical features.(17) Static morphology is not modifiable through rehabilitation or behavioral change; it is something the patient is born with.

The second is dynamic positioning: the scapula's orientation on the thorax during functional movement, which is governed by the balance of periscapular muscle forces and is potentially modifiable through rehabilitation, postural correction, or surgical intervention. Dynamic positioning is what Kebaetse,(10) Borstad,(11) and Ludewig(32) have measured in their kinematic studies.

Both mechanisms converge at the same outcome: they alter the force environment experienced by downstream structures. The following clinical examples illustrate how each mechanism operates.

## Scapular Morphology and Downstream Pathology

### Rotator Cuff Disease

The relationship between scapular morphology and rotator cuff disease has been progressively clarified over the past two decades. Nyffeler et al. introduced the acromion index, demonstrating that the lateral extension of the acromion is significantly greater in patients with rotator cuff tears (AI 0.73) than in those with intact cuffs (AI 0.64, P<0.0001).(13) Building on this observation, Moor et al. developed the critical shoulder angle (CSA), which combines lateral acromial extension with glenoid inclination into a single measurement (Figure 1). In their study of 298 shoulders, the mean CSA was 33.1 degrees in controls, 38.0 degrees in rotator cuff tears, and 28.1 degrees in osteoarthritis, with an AUC of 0.92 for discriminating cuff tears from controls and 0.90 for osteoarthritis.(1)

[FIGURE 1: Garcia CSA measurement]

Rotator cuff tears and primary glenohumeral osteoarthritis are inversely related: high CSA predicts cuff tears while low CSA predicts OA, and the two conditions rarely coexist. The biomechanical explanation centers on the shear-to-compression ratio at the glenohumeral joint. Viehöfer et al. demonstrated that a CSA of 38 degrees produces a 23% higher shear-to-compression ratio compared to 33 degrees, requiring supraspinatus forces that exceed physiological values.(2) Gerber et al. confirmed this on a physical shoulder simulator: supraspinatus tendon load increases 13 to 33% for a CSA of 38 degrees compared to 33 degrees.(14) In a high-CSA shoulder, the deltoid generates a greater superior shear vector, and the supraspinatus must counter this shear to maintain joint stability, chronically overloading it while sparing articular cartilage. In a low-CSA shoulder, the deltoid's force vector is directed more compressively into the glenoid, the cuff is spared, and the articular cartilage bears greater cumulative load.(1,2,14) Through a single geometric parameter, the scapula determines which downstream structure is preferentially loaded. Blonna et al. demonstrated a dose-response relationship: CSA averaged 34 degrees in controls, 36 degrees with isolated supraspinatus tears, and 40 degrees with combined supraspinatus and infraspinatus tears (OR 1.7, CI 1.4 to 2.0 after multivariate adjustment).(3)

What elevates these findings from academic observation to clinical significance is the surgical outcome data. Garcia et al. found that a CSA exceeding 38 degrees produces an odds ratio of 14.8 for full-thickness retear after arthroscopic rotator cuff repair (P<0.01).(15) In response to the emerging biomechanical evidence, Gerber et al. showed that arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty (lateral, and not the traditional anterior approach described by Neer) safely reduces the CSA from a mean of 37.5 degrees to 33.9 degrees (P<0.001) without compromising the deltoid origin. Patients whose CSA was corrected to 33 degrees or less had 25% greater abduction strength than those whose postoperative CSA remained at or above 35 degrees (P=0.04), and postoperative CSA was significantly larger in failed repairs than in healed repairs (P=0.026).(16) A systematic review by Docter et al. further examined the relationship between CSA and both cuff tear incidence and repair outcomes, corroborating the association in the majority of included studies while noting the heterogeneity in the literature.(33)

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that not all studies have replicated these findings. Bjarnison et al. found CSA associated with osteoarthritis (OR 2.25, P=0.002) but not with rotator cuff tears (OR 1.12, P=0.63) in their cohort.(18) The weight of evidence tilts toward CSA being clinically relevant, but the debate remains live.

The observation most relevant to the framework we propose is this: the CSA is a measurement of scapular morphology. The acromion is the scapula. An innate morphological feature of the upstream structure influences the force environment of the downstream structure.

### Posterior Glenohumeral Instability

A parallel line of investigation from Gerber's research group has revealed a similar upstream relationship for glenohumeral instability, operating through a different anatomical parameter of the same bone. Meyer et al. found that posterior acromial morphology is significantly associated with posterior shoulder instability (Figure 2, Figure 3). Posterior acromial height (PAH) measured 30.9 mm in patients with posterior instability versus 19.5 mm in those with anterior instability (P<0.001). At a cutoff of PAH of 23 mm or greater, the odds ratio for posterior instability was 39.(4)

[FIGURE 2: Akgun 3D measurements]

[FIGURE 3: Gerber 3D overlay]

Akgun et al. expanded on these morphological findings using full-trunk MRI, demonstrating that patients with constitutional static posterior instability (Type C1) differ from healthy controls across multiple parameters simultaneously.(6) Glenoid retroversion averaged 20.1 degrees versus 5.8 degrees (P<0.001). Humeral retrotorsion was 14.9 degrees versus 34.4 degrees (P<0.001). Posterior acromial coverage was reduced and posterior acromial tilt increased. Glenoid dysplasia was present in 53% of affected shoulders. Scapulothoracic orientation differed: less upward rotation, less scapular tilt, and less thoracic kyphosis. This is not a single anatomical variant but a constitutional morphotype involving the entire shoulder girdle and trunk.

Beeler et al. extended this analysis by comparing scapular morphology in patients with dynamic posterior instability, static posterior instability, and healthy controls using three-dimensional CT.(38) Both instability groups shared the same abnormal morphology: a higher, flatter acromion with decreased posterolateral coverage and increased glenoid retroversion. The morphological profile was remarkably similar between the dynamic and static groups, suggesting a common anatomical substrate. If the same bony phenotype underlies both dynamic instability and static posterior subluxation, these may represent stages of a single morphology-driven pathway rather than distinct entities.

The biomechanical significance of these morphological differences was established by Hochreiter et al., who tested three-dimensional printed scapula models and found that a high, flat acromial configuration reduced the force required for posterior humeral displacement by 23 to 60% compared to normal anatomy. Correcting glenoid retroversion alone was not sufficient to restore normal posterior translation; both glenoid and acromial anatomy must be corrected to normalize posterior stability.(5) These findings were subsequently validated in cadaveric specimens, where the same group demonstrated that posterior acromial bone grafting restored stability comparable to corrective osteotomy.(39) The acromion is an active restraint to posterior humeral head translation, not a passive bystander.

This observation carries a direct clinical implication. If posterior instability has a substantial morphological component, soft-tissue repair alone should have predictable limitations in patients with adverse bony anatomy. Arner et al. confirmed this: patients requiring revision after arthroscopic posterior capsulolabral repair had significantly higher posterior acromial height than those whose primary repairs succeeded (13.5 mm versus 10.0 mm, P=0.04).(40) The bony morphology predicted the soft-tissue failure.

Moroder et al. reported the outcomes of a soft-tissue procedure (the arthroscopic posterior articular coverage and shift, or PACS) specifically designed for constitutional static posterior instability (Type C1).(41) At two-year follow-up, symptoms improved: pain decreased from 6.4 to 3.3 and all outcome scores improved. However, posterior subluxation indices returned to baseline. The authors described the procedure as "symptomatic therapy that does not reverse the underlying cause or stop the progressive pathology." This finding illustrates the central prediction of the framework presented here: addressing the downstream tissue without correcting the upstream force environment produces symptom relief while the mechanical cause persists.

Habermeyer et al. directly tested an alternative hypothesis, that rotator cuff muscle imbalance drives posterior subluxation in arthritic shoulders.(42) Using three-dimensional reconstruction with automated segmentation, they found no correlation between the anteroposterior muscle volume ratio and glenoid retroversion, posterior humeral subluxation, or Walch classification. The bony morphology, not the soft-tissue balance, appears to be the primary determinant.

Gerber et al. have translated the morphological reasoning into clinical practice with a combined glenoid and acromial osteotomy, the SCOPE procedure (Scapular Corrective Osteotomy for Posterior Escape), planned using three-dimensional statistical shape models of normal scapular anatomy.(19) In nine consecutive patients, six of whom had failed prior soft-tissue stabilization, combined scapular correction improved the subjective shoulder value from a median of 40% to 87.5%. Eight of nine shoulders were recentered. The one failure was attributed to inadequate correction of the acromial morphology. The scapular architecture, not the soft tissue, had been the primary problem.

The pattern is the same as for cuff disease, but the downstream pathology is different. An innate morphological feature of the scapula shapes the mechanical restraint environment of the glenohumeral joint. Change the scapular morphology, and you change which direction the shoulder fails. And when that morphology is ignored in surgical planning, the consequences are predictable.

## Scapulothoracic Positioning and Downstream Pathology

### Posterior Glenoid Wear: From Instability to Arthritis

The posterior instability section above demonstrates that scapular morphology determines the direction of glenohumeral instability. This section addresses what happens next: whether and how that instability progresses to degenerative disease.

Domos et al. described the Walch B0 glenoid as pre-arthritic posterior subluxation of the humeral head: static posterior decentering without posterior glenoid bone erosion, occurring in young men with increased glenoid retroversion and early arthritis symptoms.(20) They proposed it as the earliest identifiable stage in the evolution of B-type glenoid pathology (Figure 4).

[FIGURE 4: B0 progression]

Walker et al. provided longitudinal evidence for this progression.(43) In 65 shoulders followed with serial CT scans over a mean of 74 months, 89% of B1 glenoids (posterior subluxation with early degenerative changes) progressed to B2 (biconcave posterior erosion) or B3 (monoconcave severe erosion). By contrast, only 19% of concentrically worn A1 glenoids progressed at all, and only 7% crossed to the B pathway. Once posterior subluxation is established with any degenerative change, progression toward posterior erosion is highly likely.

The B0 glenoid and the C1 constitutional posterior instability described in the preceding section may represent the same pathological entity observed at different stages and from different clinical perspectives. C1 describes a young patient presenting to the sports medicine surgeon with posterior subluxation and functional limitations. B0 describes a young patient presenting to the arthroplasty surgeon with the same posterior subluxation and early arthritic changes. The morphological substrate is the same: increased glenoid retroversion, reduced posterior acromial coverage, and altered scapulothoracic orientation.(6,20) If this connection is correct, then posterior instability in a twenty-five-year-old and the need for a total shoulder arthroplasty with a B2 glenoid three decades later are not separate conditions but sequential stages of a single morphology-driven pathway. No longitudinal study has yet tracked a cohort of B0 glenoids from initial identification to determine whether and at what rate this progression occurs. This would be among the most important studies this framework motivates.

A key question is whether scapulothoracic positioning contributes to posterior subluxation and its progression. Bryce et al. showed that scapular rotation changes measured glenoid version by up to 22.5 degrees, with 0.42 degrees of relative anteversion for every degree of scapular abduction in the coronal plane.(21) Their paper framed this finding as a computed tomography measurement artifact. We propose an alternative interpretation: scapulothoracic position dynamically changes effective glenoid version during functional movement, a concept we term dynamic glenoid version.

Malavolta et al. tested this directly by measuring scapulothoracic alignment in patients with Walch B-type shoulders compared to healthy controls.(44) They found no significant difference in resting scapulothoracic alignment. However, they did find significant kinematic differences during motion, including 15 degrees greater anterior tilt during internal rotation at 90 degrees of elevation. This distinction is important: the force environment that drives posterior glenoid loading may not be determined by where the scapula sits at rest, but by how it moves under load. Dynamic glenoid version, if it proves to be a real and measurable biomechanical variable, would be potentially modifiable through scapulothoracic rehabilitation, unlike the fixed morphological parameters discussed in the preceding sections.

As noted in the preceding section, Akgun et al. found that C1 patients have less scapular upward rotation, less scapular tilt, and less thoracic kyphosis than controls (Figure 5).(6) The reduced kyphosis argues against a simple posture-driven model and suggests intrinsic skeletal geometry may determine scapulothoracic orientation.

[FIGURE 5: Akgun ST orientation]

Whether dynamic glenoid version during loaded functional tasks contributes to cumulative posterior glenoid loading is a testable question that has not been asked. Measurement of glenoid orientation using biplanar fluoroscopy during functional tasks, correlated with subsequent progression on serial imaging, would provide the evidence needed to confirm or refute this hypothesis. Together, the CSA and posterior acromial sections (static morphology) and this section (dynamic positioning) suggest that the scapula influences downstream pathology through both mechanisms, and that posterior instability and posterior glenoid erosion may describe sequential stages of the same morphology-driven process.

### Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty

When a reverse shoulder prosthesis is implanted, the biomechanics of the glenohumeral joint change fundamentally, and the consequences for the scapulothoracic system are significant. Walker et al. measured scapulohumeral rhythm using fluoroscopy with three-dimensional model-image registration across three different implant designs and found that SHR in rTSA is 1.3:1, compared to 3:1 in normal shoulders.(7) This means the scapulothoracic system contributes approximately half of arm elevation after rTSA, a dramatic increase from its normal contribution.

Kawashima et al. found that this altered scapulothoracic contribution has direct clinical consequences in a study of 20 patients with semi-inlay rTSA. Patients with SHR of 2 or greater had significantly higher ASES scores (94.3, SD 4.6) than those with SHR below 2 (82.1, SD 9.4, P=0.007), with a significant positive correlation between SHR and patient-reported outcomes (r=0.452, P=0.045).(8) While the sample size limits the generalizability of this finding, its consistency with the larger Walker dataset and with the broader biomechanical framework presented here suggests that further investigation in a properly powered study is warranted. Moroder et al. showed that scapulothoracic orientation directly influences optimal humeral component retrotorsion in rTSA, with scapular internal rotation correlating with the correction angle at R=0.91 when adjusted for glenoid retroversion.(22) Shekhbihi et al. framed this collective body of work as "the scapulothoracic conundrum," calling for integration of scapulothoracic assessment into rTSA planning and rehabilitation.(23)

The clinical significance of persistent scapulothoracic dysfunction is underscored by kinematic data from Mercurio et al., who prospectively showed that scapular dyskinesis persists after successful arthroscopic rotator cuff repair despite improved range of motion and Constant scores; scapulohumeral rhythm showed no significant change in their cohort (P>0.05) (Figure 6).(9) If dyskinesis were simply a consequence of the cuff tear, one would expect it to resolve after repair. The fact that it does not suggests that the scapulothoracic dysfunction is either independent of, or upstream of, the cuff pathology.

[FIGURE 6: Mercurio kinematic data]

The biological mechanism underlying this persistence may involve structural muscle remodeling. Gibbons et al. showed that massive rotator cuff tears produce sarcomere loss, fiber shortening, and, in massive tears, sarcomere length below the optimal operating range, representing architectural changes that may be irreversible even with successful tendon repair.(24) If similar remodeling occurs in the periscapular muscles of patients with chronic shoulder dysfunction (biologically plausible but unproven), then the scapulothoracic system may be structurally committed to dysfunction before surgery is performed. This reasoning provides a case for scapulothoracic prehabilitation before shoulder surgery: not to strengthen muscles in the conventional sense, but to restore sarcomere architecture and tension balance while remodeling remains possible, before imposing the new biomechanical demands of a prosthesis. Evidence from a randomized trial by Camargo et al. demonstrating that scapular-focused exercise improves kinematics, function, and pain in patients with impingement lends plausibility to the hypothesis that targeted prehabilitation could similarly benefit arthroplasty patients, though this has not been directly tested.(31)

## The Unifying Principle

The examples above involve four different pathologies, four different patient populations, and four different downstream structures failing. In every case, however, the scapula is upstream. In every case, an alteration in the scapula's morphology or position is associated with changed force distribution to a downstream structure. And in every case, the specific pathology that develops depends on which downstream structure bears the consequence.

One mechanism that may explain how the same upstream alteration produces different pathologies in different structures is cellular mechanotransduction. Ingber demonstrated that forces at the cell surface transmit through the cytoskeleton to the nucleus, influencing gene expression.(25-27) Engler et al. showed that identical stem cells differentiate into neurons, muscle, or bone depending solely on substrate stiffness.(37) In orthopedics, this principle is already recognized in tissue-specific forms: Wolff's Law, Davis's Law, and the Hueter-Volkmann principle. These are not separate phenomena but the same cellular machinery expressed in different cell types. When scapulothoracic mechanics change, they alter the force environment for bone, tendon, cartilage, and ligament simultaneously, and each tissue responds according to its own mechanosensitive program.

The scapulothoracic articulation also fits the structural principle of tensegrity (Figure 7): a compression element with no bony attachment, suspended within a continuous tension network.(25-27,28) The clinical observations presented here align with tensegrity predictions: pre-stress determines system function (Kebaetse(10)), geometric alteration changes force distribution throughout the network (CSA and posterior acromial data(1-5)), local perturbation produces global reorganization (Borstad(11), Burkhart(29)), and the system self-stabilizes at a new equilibrium after perturbation (Mercurio(9)).

[FIGURE 7: Tensegrity models]

Whether tensegrity is the correct structural model in a formal sense remains open. We raise it as a lens that organizes these observations, not as a claim to be defended.

## Questions Raised and Directions for Investigation

If the framework presented here has merit, several clinical and research questions follow.

Routine measurement of the CSA on pre-operative shoulder films takes 30 seconds and has demonstrated excellent reliability.(1) Whether lateral acromioplasty should be performed as an adjunct to cuff repair when the CSA exceeds a threshold is supported by Gerber's data(16) but has not been evaluated in a randomized trial. The standard anterior acromioplasty described by Neer does not alter the CSA; only lateral acromioplasty addresses this parameter. Similarly, posterior acromial morphology should be assessed before posterior stabilization surgery; the evidence that adverse acromial anatomy predicts repair failure(40) suggests that ignoring this parameter leads to avoidable recurrence. The scapular assistance test offers a simple clinical method to assess whether scapulothoracic positioning contributes to symptoms.(30)

Several research questions emerge. First, pre-operative scapulothoracic kinematics may independently predict outcomes after rTSA, given the increased scapulothoracic dependence these prostheses impose.(7,8) Second, scapulothoracic prehabilitation before arthroplasty, targeting sarcomere architecture rather than conventional strengthening,(24) may improve the system's capacity to meet prosthetic demands; evidence from Camargo et al. that scapular-focused exercise improves kinematics in impingement lends plausibility.(31) Third, measurement of dynamic glenoid version during functional loading could reveal whether patients who develop posterior wear have greater functional retroversion than static imaging indicates. Fourth, motor control timing at the scapulothoracic level, analogous to the feedforward activation patterns Hodges and Richardson described for spinal stabilizers,(35) may represent an underrecognized mechanism of dyskinesis distinct from pure force deficits.(36)

## Limitations

Several limitations must be acknowledged. This paper emphasizes extrinsic mechanical factors; intrinsic tendon biology, genetic predisposition, and vascular insufficiency contribute independently to rotator cuff disease and are not displaced by this framework. The CSA-cuff tear association has not been replicated in all cohorts,(18) and some morphological associations may reflect shared genetic or developmental programs rather than direct mechanical causation. The tensegrity model at the musculoskeletal level remains qualitative. The B0 hypothesis is supported by cross-sectional morphological data and limited longitudinal evidence for B1-to-B2 progression,(43) but no study has tracked B0 glenoids specifically, and causation between scapulothoracic orientation and posterior glenoid wear has not been established. The Malavolta finding of no resting scapulothoracic alignment difference in Walch B patients means dynamic glenoid version must operate during loading, not at rest, and this has not been directly measured in vivo.(44) The individual studies cited range from Level I(31) to Level IV,(19) and the evidence supporting the framework as a whole is limited.

Despite these limitations, the convergence of evidence on the scapula as a common upstream factor is difficult to dismiss. The predictions are specific and testable.

## Conclusion

The scapulothoracic articulation is a compression element suspended in a continuous tension network. Its static morphology influences force distribution to the rotator cuff and the glenohumeral joint. Its dynamic positioning influences effective glenoid orientation and subacromial space geometry. When we surgically alter the glenohumeral joint, the demands on this tension-dependent system increase. The specific downstream pathology that develops in a given patient, whether cuff disease, posterior instability, glenoid wear, or suboptimal arthroplasty outcome, depends on which downstream structure bears the consequence of the particular alteration in scapulothoracic mechanics.

Whether this represents a single biomechanical reality producing diverse clinical manifestations, or whether the convergence on the scapula reflects coincidence or confounding, cannot be determined from existing literature alone. The evidence presented here, drawn from morphological, kinematic, arthroplasty, and cellular biology investigations conducted independently by different research groups, points consistently toward the scapulothoracic articulation as a common and underappreciated variable. The questions this framework raises are specific enough to be tested and clinically relevant enough to warrant the effort.
