# The Mechanical Origin of Degenerative Disease: How Morphology, Force, and Cellular Adaptation Connect Common Musculoskeletal Conditions

William B. Roepke, M.D.

Department of Orthopedic Surgery and Rehabilitation, University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, Oklahoma City, OK

---

## Abstract

Rotator cuff disease, osteoarthritis, femoroacetabular impingement, lateral epicondylitis, and insertional Achilles tendinopathy are treated as distinct conditions. In a companion paper, we argued that four shoulder pathologies represent branches of the same mechanical system, with scapular morphology selecting which tissue receives an aberrant force and therefore which condition develops.^1^ The present paper extends that argument across the musculoskeletal system. At every major joint, the same logic applies: a measurable morphological parameter redirects the dominant force vector, a tissue receives a force type it was not designed to bear, and the tissue adapts its composition accordingly through conserved mechanotransduction pathways. The critical shoulder angle selects between rotator cuff disease and glenohumeral osteoarthritis. Cam morphology converts hip flexion into anterosuperior labral shear. Posterior tibial slope converts quadriceps compression into anterior cruciate ligament loading. Pelvic incidence determines whether spinal degeneration preferentially affects the posterior elements or the intervertebral discs. In each case, the tissue change traditionally named as the diagnosis is the predictable output of a cell reading its mechanical environment and changing its gene expression in response. The molecular apparatus that performs this reading, including mechanosensitive ion channels, a structural pathway from extracellular matrix to chromatin, and transcriptional regulators gated by cytoskeletal tension, has been identified and characterized. This paper reviews the clinical pattern, the molecular evidence, and the implications for how we understand, classify, and treat degenerative musculoskeletal disease.

---

## Introduction

Codman observed in 1934 that rotator cuff tears begin on the deep surface of the tendon and that "it would be hard to explain this by erosion from contact with the acromion process."^2^ Nearly a century later, subacromial decompression has fallen from favor, vindicating his skepticism about the impingement model.^3^ But the deeper question he raised, why does the pathology localize where it does, extends well beyond the shoulder. Why does lateral epicondylitis affect the deep surface of the extensor carpi radialis brevis, facing the capitellum, rather than the superficial surface?^4^ Why does insertional Achilles tendinopathy preferentially involve the anterior surface of the tendon at the calcaneal attachment?^5^ Why do articular-side partial-thickness cuff tears outnumber bursal-side tears roughly two to one?^6^ In each case, the pathology localizes to a surface that faces a bony structure, a surface receiving compression or shear rather than the tensile loading the tissue was designed to bear.

In a companion paper, we presented evidence that scapular morphology, including the critical shoulder angle, posterior acromial height, and glenoid version, determines the force environment at the glenohumeral joint, and that four conditions traditionally treated as separate entities are predictable consequences of different morphological configurations acting on the same system.^1^ That paper focused on a single joint. The purpose of the present paper is to examine whether the same principle, in which morphology sets force environment and force environment determines tissue adaptation, operates across the musculoskeletal system, to review the molecular evidence that explains how cells translate mechanical information into changes in gene expression, and to consider the implications for how degenerative disease should be understood and treated.

We should state what this paper proposes and where its limitations lie. We are arguing that a common mechanobiological thread connects conditions currently studied in separate subspecialty literatures. The molecular evidence supporting this thread is drawn primarily from cell culture and animal models; it has not been directly confirmed in human degenerative tissue at most of the clinical sites discussed. The clinical evidence supporting the morphology-pathology correlations varies in strength across joints, ranging from robust at the shoulder and knee to preliminary at the spine and ankle. Where we are making inferences or proposing novel framings, we identify them as such. The framework generates testable predictions, and we present those predictions alongside the evidence that motivates them.

## The Pattern Across Joints

### The Shoulder

The companion paper presents this case in detail.^1^ In summary: the critical shoulder angle, measured on a single anteroposterior radiograph, separates rotator cuff tears from glenohumeral osteoarthritis with an area under the curve of 0.92.^7^ A high CSA increases the shear component of the deltoid force vector relative to the glenoid surface; a low CSA directs force more compressively into the articular cartilage. Viehöfer demonstrated that a CSA of 38 degrees produces a 23 percent higher shear-to-compression ratio than a CSA of 33 degrees.^8^ Gerber confirmed this on a physical simulator and showed that arthroscopic lateral acromioplasty can reduce the CSA and improve outcomes.^9^ In the axial plane, posterior acromial morphology predicts posterior instability with an odds ratio of 39.^10^ Glenoid retroversion converts centered compression into posterior-directed shear, initiating a cascade from subluxation to posterior glenoid erosion.^1^ In each case, the morphology is the independent variable. The tissue adaptation is the dependent variable. What we call the diagnosis is the name we give to the adaptation.

### The Hip

Femoroacetabular impingement follows identical logic. Ganz and colleagues recognized that an aspherical femoral head-neck junction, the cam deformity, is a morphological boundary condition that converts normal hip flexion into a mechanical lever driving shear and compression into the anterosuperior labrum and adjacent cartilage.^11^ Beck demonstrated that the pattern of cartilage and labral damage is predictable from the morphology: cam impingement produces outside-in abrasion of the anterosuperior acetabular cartilage with labral detachment at the chondrolabral junction, while pincer impingement produces labral crush injury with a contrecoup lesion on the posteroinferior acetabular cartilage.^12^ The labral tear and the cartilage delamination are not the disease, they are the tissue's response to a force it was not designed to bear. This is why labral repair without correction of the cam deformity has inferior outcomes: the tissue is addressed, but the force environment that produced the adaptation is left in place.^13^

The hip also illustrates a complication that the shoulder avoids: the force environment at the hip is multiparametric. The neck-shaft angle, femoral offset, acetabular version, and lateral center-edge angle all modulate the abductor force vector and the distribution of joint reaction force across the acetabulum.^14^ This means no single parameter achieves the predictive elegance of the CSA. The framework still applies, where morphology determines force environment and force environment determines tissue adaptation, but the clinical prediction requires a multi-parameter morphological assessment rather than a single measurement.

### The Knee

At the knee, the dominant force generator is the quadriceps, the largest muscle group in the body, producing forces of 3 to 5 times body weight during stair descent and deceleration. Two morphological parameters redirect this force in clinically significant ways.

The posterior tibial slope determines how tibiofemoral contact force resolves into compressive and translational components. Dejour and Bonnin demonstrated that every 10 degrees of increased posterior slope is associated with 6 millimeters of anterior tibial translation.^15^ Bernhardson and LaPrade confirmed this cadaverically, showing that anterior cruciate ligament graft force increases linearly with posterior tibial slope across the full range tested.^16^ Meta-analyses consistently identify increased posterior tibial slope as an independent risk factor for both primary ACL injury and graft failure after reconstruction, with patients having a medial slope of 14 degrees or greater showing an 18.7-fold increased risk of revision failure.^17^ The emergence of slope-reducing osteotomy combined with revision ACL reconstruction represents an implicit acknowledgment by the surgical community that the morphological boundary condition (not just the tissue) must be addressed.

Coronal plane alignment determines which compartment receives the dominant compressive load. Varus alignment concentrates force medially; valgus alignment concentrates it laterally. High tibial osteotomy for medial compartment osteoarthritis is correction of this upstream mechanical parameter, and second-look arthroscopy studies have demonstrated fibrocartilage formation over previously denuded surfaces when the mechanical axis is appropriately corrected.^18^ The tissue was adapting to the force it received. When the force was redirected, the tissue readapted.

We note that the framing of posterior tibial slope as a continuous parameter selecting between cruciate ligament pathology (high slope, shear-dominant) and compartment degeneration (alignment-dependent, compression-dominant) appears to be a novel synthesis. The individual correlations are well established; their integration into a unified morphological selection framework has not, to our knowledge, been previously proposed and should be regarded as a testable prediction rather than an established finding.

### The Spine

Pelvic incidence is a fixed morphological parameter determined by the relationship between the sacral endplate and the hip axis, approximately stable after skeletal maturity.^19^ It determines the lumbar lordosis required for sagittal balance, and through lordosis, it determines the direction of the erector spinae force vector relative to the spinal column. High pelvic incidence requires greater lordosis, concentrating the paraspinal force vector posteriorly through the facet joints and pars interarticularis. Labelle and colleagues found mean pelvic incidence values of 68.5 degrees in low-grade isthmic spondylolisthesis and 79.0 degrees in high-grade, compared to 47 to 57 degrees in controls.^19^ Facet arthropathy in young patients has been independently associated with elevated pelvic incidence.^20^ Low pelvic incidence, conversely, is associated with flat lordosis and anterior column loading: lumbar disc herniation is significantly more common in Roussouly Type 1 and 2 spines, which correspond to low pelvic incidence morphotypes.^21^

We propose, acknowledging this as a novel framing, that pelvic incidence functions as the spinal analog of the critical shoulder angle: a single measurable morphological parameter, determined early in life, that selects between diverging degenerative trajectories by redirecting the dominant force vector. High values predispose to posterior element disease; low values predispose to disc disease. The individual correlations supporting this framing are published, but the explicit parallel to CSA has not been drawn. Prospective studies correlating pelvic incidence with the specific pattern of spinal degeneration (posterior versus anterior) would provide the most direct test.

### Compressive Tendinopathies

Cook and Purdam recognized that tendons subjected to chronic compressive loading against bony prominences develop a specific pattern of fibrocartilaginous metaplasia distinct from tensile tendinopathy.^5^ The pathology localizes to the deep surface of the tendon (the surface facing the compressing structure) and the histology shows chondroid cells, type II collagen, and increased proteoglycan content consistent with tissue adapting to compression rather than tension.

Lateral epicondylitis exemplifies this pattern. The extensor carpi radialis brevis is the only common extensor with a purely tendinous origin, separated from the radiocapitellar joint by a capsular layer measuring 3.3 millimeters at its thinnest point.^22^ The pathology consistently involves the deep surface (the surface facing the capitellum) with chondrocyte-like cells documented in the tendinosis tissue.^4,23^ Radiocapitellar contact pressures reach 676 kPa in supination.^24^ The deep surface of the ECRB receives compressive loading from the joint beneath it. Kraushaar and Nirschl themselves noted that "tendons that wrap around convex surfaces are subjected to compressive forces and assume a cartilage-like appearance, presumably by metaplasia", without connecting this observation to the localization of their own findings.^23^ Lateral epicondylitis has never been formally classified as a compressive tendinopathy despite meeting the criteria Cook and Purdam established.

Insertional Achilles tendinopathy with a Haglund deformity follows the same logic. The Haglund prominence is a posterosuperior calcaneal morphological variant that compresses the anterior surface of the Achilles insertion during dorsiflexion. The gastrocnemius-soleus complex generates the largest tendon forces in the body, reaching 6 to 8 times body weight during running as measured by shear wave tensiometry.^25^ When even a small fraction of this force is redirected into compression at the Haglund-tendon interface, the tissue at the contact zone adapts by forming fibrocartilage. Cook and Purdam demonstrated that modifying eccentric loading protocols to limit dorsiflexion, reducing the compressive component, increased success rates from 30 to 70 percent.^5^ The exercise was not different. The mechanical environment was different. Surgical resection of the Haglund prominence, like Gerber's lateral acromioplasty for the critical shoulder angle, is correction of the upstream morphological boundary condition.

The same principle applies to proximal hamstring tendinopathy at the ischial tuberosity during deep hip flexion, gluteus medius tendinopathy at the greater trochanter during hip adduction, and tibialis posterior tendinopathy around the medial malleolus. In each case, a tendon is compressed against a bony prominence, and the tissue at the compression interface undergoes the same predictable adaptation.

### The Common Thread

Across every joint examined, the pattern is the same. A morphological feature, congenital or acquired, redirects the dominant force vector. The tissue at the site of force mismatch reads the mechanical signal through conserved cellular machinery and adapts its composition accordingly. The adaptation is locally rational: the cell is doing exactly what the molecular pathways dictate in response to the force it senses. It is functionally pathological only because the force environment is wrong.

Traditional teaching names the adapted tissue as the diagnosis: rotator cuff tear, labral tear, lateral epicondylitis, insertional tendinopathy, disc herniation. The mechanobiological framework names the force environment as the etiology and the tissue change as the expected output.

## How Cells Read Force: The Molecular Basis

The clinical observations above require a biological mechanism: how does a cell know what type of force it is receiving, and how does it translate that information into changes in gene expression and tissue composition? This mechanism has been identified over the past two decades, and while its full characterization remains an active area of research, three systems have particularly strong evidence.

A structural pathway physically links the extracellular matrix to chromatin through integrins, the cytoskeleton, and a protein bridge spanning the nuclear envelope called the LINC complex.^26,27^ Force applied to the cell surface deforms the nucleus within seconds and recruits RNA polymerase to mechanically stretched chromatin within five seconds, before any diffusible chemical signal could reach the nucleus.^28,29^ This is not a signaling cascade. It is a mechanical linkage, and force propagates through it approximately 40-fold faster than soluble growth factor signaling measured with the same biosensor.^30^ We should note that disrupting this structural pathway does not abolish all mechanosensitive gene expression, as many mechanosensitive genes are activated through parallel cytoplasmic pathways, so the direct structural route, while real and fast, operates alongside rather than instead of conventional signaling cascades.^27^

A transcriptional switch determines cell fate based on mechanical state. The proteins YAP and TAZ shuttle between the cytoplasm and the nucleus depending on substrate stiffness, cell shape, and cytoskeletal tension.^31^ On stiff substrates, they enter the nucleus and activate osteogenic programs. On soft substrates, they are excluded and the cell adopts chondrogenic or adipogenic programs.^32^ Force opens nuclear pores to permit YAP entry through a direct physical mechanism.^33^ The critical finding for clinicians is that when mechanical regulation of YAP was blocked experimentally, biochemical signals could not compensate. The discoverers of this pathway stated that "mechanical regulation dominates over [Hippo-mediated phosphorylation]" and that activating the biochemical pathway "cannot rescue YAP/TAZ inhibition in cells with reduced mechanical stress."^34^ This has a direct implication for understanding compressive tendinopathy: a compressed, rounded tenocyte is in exactly the geometric state that excludes YAP from the nucleus, permitting the chondrogenic gene programs that produce fibrocartilaginous metaplasia.^35^ The same growth factor can drive opposite cell fates depending on mechanical context: smooth muscle differentiation on stiff substrates, chondrogenic differentiation on soft substrates, demonstrating that the mechanical environment does not merely contribute to cell fate but constrains which fates are accessible.^36^

A mechanosensitive ion channel provides the tissue-level mechanostat that Frost postulated but could not molecularly identify. Piezo1 opens in response to membrane deformation and permits calcium influx, triggering downstream signaling cascades that include YAP nuclear translocation through a pathway independent of canonical biochemical regulation.^37,38^ Conditional deletion of Piezo1 in osteoblasts produces spontaneous fractures and approximately 50 percent reduction in bone strength, a genetic recapitulation of Wolff's Law at the molecular level.^39,40^ In tendon, Piezo1 senses shear stress between collagen fibers and regulates cross-linking to maintain stiffness, functioning as a "tenocyte mechanostat."^41^ In cartilage, Piezo1 and the related channel TRPV4 together confer mechanosensitivity, with inflammation upregulating Piezo1 expression and cell stiffness to create a pathogenic feed-forward loop that makes normal loading produce a damage-level mechanical signal.^42,43^ The same channel promotes healthy adaptation in bone, regulates stiffness in tendon, and amplifies damage in inflamed cartilage, not because the channel functions differently, but because the mechanical environment it senses is different at each site.

These three systems (structural pathway, transcriptional switch, and mechanosensitive channel) are not independent discoveries from separate fields. They describe a single regulatory architecture in which the mechanical environment establishes the upstream context for cell fate decisions. Mechanical force activates Piezo1, triggering calcium and calcineurin, which drives YAP into the nucleus. Simultaneously, force transmitted through the cytoskeleton deforms the nucleus and opens nuclear pores to facilitate YAP import. Once nuclear, YAP activates gene programs that determine cell identity.^37,38^ The specific output depends on the type and magnitude of force: compression drives chondrogenic programs through YAP exclusion, tension maintains tendinous programs through YAP activation, and the threshold between programs may determine where adaptive tissue remodeling crosses into what we recognize clinically as disease.

## Why Small Morphological Differences Have Large Effects

The clinical significance of morphological parameters, such as a few degrees of CSA or tibial slope, seems disproportionate to their magnitude. Three mechanical principles explain this.

The sensitivity of the shear-to-compression ratio to angular change is proportional to the magnitude of the force being redirected. A morphological parameter that changes the angle of a small muscle's force vector by five degrees produces a trivial shift in joint mechanics. The same five-degree change applied to the deltoid, the largest muscle at the shoulder, or the quadriceps, the largest muscle group in the body, produces a clinically significant shift. This is why the morphological parameters with the greatest predictive power are found at joints where the largest forces are being geometrically redirected: the critical shoulder angle redirects the deltoid, the posterior tibial slope redirects the quadriceps, and pelvic incidence determines the direction of the erector spinae force vector relative to the posterior spinal elements.^8,15,16,19^

Frost's mechanostat demonstrated that bone does not respond along a continuous dose-response curve but switches between qualitatively different programs (resorption, maintenance, and formation) at specific strain thresholds.^44^ If a morphological parameter shifts local tissue strain across one of these thresholds, the biological response changes direction, not merely magnitude. The fibrocartilaginous metaplasia observed in compressed tendons may represent an analogous threshold phenomenon: below a compressive threshold, tenocytes maintain a tendinous phenotype; above it, they switch to a chondroid program.^5^ The transition appears discontinuous; the tissue switches programs rather than gradually blending phenotypes, though the specific threshold values for soft tissues remain undefined.

Biological tissues under cyclic loading follow power-law fatigue relationships in which cycles to failure scale with stress amplitude raised to a negative exponent, typically between 5 and 15 for bone and tendon.^45,46^ This nonlinear relationship means that a 20 percent increase in cyclic stress does not reduce fatigue life by 20 percent. With an exponent of 10, it produces an approximately 6-fold reduction. Over an estimated 1 to 2 million loading cycles per year, small morphological differences in stress amplitude accumulate into large differences in cumulative damage probability. This explains how a morphological parameter measured on a single radiograph can predict pathology that develops decades later.

## The Coupling of Aging and Mechanical History

A framework attributing degenerative disease to mechanical environment must address the observation that these conditions become more prevalent with age. We do not argue that biological aging is reducible to mechanical history. Telomere shortening, oxidative damage, and other time-dependent processes contribute independently to tissue degeneration. However, emerging evidence suggests that several processes traditionally attributed to biological aging are coupled to mechanical history through specific feedback loops.

Mechanical overload accelerates cellular senescence in chondrocytes through nuclear membrane instability, and overexpression of the responsible enzyme rescues the senescent phenotype, establishing a direct causal link from aberrant mechanical loading to the cellular aging process.^47^ Senescent tenocytes exhibit blunted matrix remodeling responses with sustained MMP activity that shifts the balance toward degradation.^48^ Advanced glycation end-products accumulate with chronological age and are genuinely time-dependent, but their mechanical consequence, specifically the elimination of collagen fiber sliding and loss of tissue viscoelasticity, alters the strain environment that resident cells experience, feeding back through the same mechanotransduction pathways that determine cell fate.^49,50^ Estrogen withdrawal impairs mechanosensation directly, reducing integrin localization at focal adhesion sites and producing defective downstream signaling in osteocytes, meaning postmenopausal bone loss is partly a disease of impaired mechanotransduction, not just hormone deficiency.^51^ The mechanosensitive stem cell niche in bone marrow is expanded by exercise and depleted by unloading, with Piezo1 deletion phenocopying unloading and producing functional consequences for both osteogenesis and immune function.^52^

The implication is not that aging is irrelevant but that the boundary between mechanical and biological aging in load-bearing tissues is less clean than traditionally assumed. Multiple age-associated processes are either driven by mechanical history or feed back through the same molecular pathways. The mechanical component may not be the entire explanation, but it is the largest modifiable component, and therefore the most clinically actionable.

## Clinical Implications

If the mechanical environment is the upstream determinant of tissue fate, then the primary therapeutic target should be the force environment, not the adapted tissue. This reframing is already being demonstrated at individual joints: Gerber's lateral acromioplasty for the critical shoulder angle, femoral osteochondroplasty for cam impingement, high tibial osteotomy for medial compartment osteoarthritis, slope-reducing osteotomy for ACL graft protection, Haglund resection for insertional Achilles tendinopathy, Cook and Purdam's range modification for compressive tendinopathy. Each of these interventions corrects an upstream morphological or mechanical parameter rather than simply repairing the downstream tissue. Each has evidence of superior durability compared to tissue-level treatment alone.^5,9,13,18^

What has not been done is to recognize these interventions as expressions of a single principle rather than isolated innovations at individual joints. A framework that takes the mechanical environment seriously as the upstream regulator would have several additional implications.

The terminology used to describe intra-articular findings may itself constitute a barrier to this recognition. Surgeons routinely describe hypertrophic synovium, tendon hyperemia, and capsular thickening as "inflammation," a term that implies immune-mediated pathology requiring direct treatment. Yet the cellular profile of synovitis in mechanically altered joints is distinct from that of autoimmune arthropathies. Rheumatoid synovitis is characterized by lymphocytic infiltration, CD4+ T cells, B cells, and TNF-alpha-dominant cytokine cascades.^54^ Mechanical synovitis is predominantly macrophage-driven, with innate immune activation triggered by damage-associated molecular patterns from altered tissue loading, and an IL-1-beta-dominant profile.^55^ These are biologically different processes that receive the same clinical label and, frequently, the same surgical treatment: debridement.

The neovascularization that gives mechanically stressed synovium its "angry" arthroscopic appearance represents increased perfusion and metabolic activity, a response that, in other tissues under altered mechanical demand, would be recognized as adaptive rather than pathological. Nerve ingrowth accompanying the new vessels likely explains the associated pain,^56^ but pain is a signal, not a diagnosis. When a surgeon debrids hypertrophic synovium or tenotomizes an inflamed biceps tendon without addressing the upstream mechanical environment, the response is treated while the stimulus persists. This is analogous to performing a cuff repair without correcting a pathological critical shoulder angle: the tissue is addressed, but the force environment that drove the adaptation remains in place.

This distinction has practical implications. If mechanical synovitis is a downstream indicator of an aberrant force environment, its presence should prompt investigation of the upstream mechanical cause rather than direct excision. The same logic applies to capsular changes: adhesive capsulitis, for example, correlates with states of reduced mechanical loading (postoperative immobilization, diabetes, hypothyroidism) rather than overload,^57^ suggesting a fibrotic response to altered or absent mechanotransduction signaling rather than classical inflammation. Recognizing these tissue responses as mechanically driven rather than independently pathological aligns clinical reasoning with the framework's central claim: that the force environment, not the adapted tissue, is the appropriate therapeutic target.

Morphological screening could identify individuals at risk for specific degenerative trajectories before symptoms develop. The critical shoulder angle, posterior tibial slope, pelvic incidence, and cam morphology are measurable with standard imaging. If prospective studies confirm that these parameters predict specific degenerative patterns with the same fidelity the CSA has demonstrated at the shoulder, screening and targeted loading modification, including exercise selection, range-of-motion management, and activity guidance, could constitute a rational prophylactic strategy. This has not been tested prospectively and remains a prediction of the framework.

The concept of "age-related degeneration" may require reexamination. If tissue adaptation is mechanically driven and morphological boundary conditions are set early in life, then degenerative disease is not an inevitable consequence of aging but a predictable consequence of cumulative mechanical cycles filtered through individual morphology. This would explain why some 80-year-olds have intact rotator cuffs and some 40-year-olds do not. The difference is not age but the interaction of morphology, loading history, and the molecular machinery that translates force into tissue fate. We present this as a hypothesis, not as an established finding, and we acknowledge that genuine age-dependent biological processes contribute independently.

## Limitations

Several limitations must be stated explicitly. The molecular mechanisms reviewed here have been demonstrated primarily in cell culture and animal models. Direct demonstration of specific pathway activity (YAP localization, Piezo1 expression) in human degenerative tissue at joints with characterized morphological parameters has not been performed at most sites discussed. The framework predicts that such measurements would show mechanically determined pathway activity consistent with the clinical tissue changes; these studies are feasible and would provide the most direct test of the framework.

The clinical evidence for morphology-pathology correlations varies substantially across joints. The shoulder data (CSA, AUC 0.92) and knee data (tibial slope, linear relationship to ACL force) are robust. The hip requires multiparametric assessment rather than a single measurement. The spine correlations (pelvic incidence to posterior element versus disc disease) and the classification of lateral epicondylitis as a compressive tendinopathy are proposed here as novel framings supported by existing evidence but not yet directly tested as integrated hypotheses.

Frost's mechanostat thresholds are well defined for bone but remain undefined for soft tissues. The framework assumes analogous threshold-dependent behavior in tendon, cartilage, and ligament, consistent with the observed sharp phenotypic transitions but not yet quantified.

The framework does not explain acute trauma, genetic connective tissue disorders, inflammatory arthropathies, or centralized pain states. It addresses load-bearing tissues under chronic mechanical perturbation. The claim is specific and falsifiable: if correcting morphological parameters does not alter tissue adaptation trajectories in prospective study, the mechanical causation argument fails.

## Conclusion

D'Arcy Thompson wrote in 1917 that "the form of an object is a diagram of forces."^53^ The scapula's form, its critical shoulder angle, its posterior acromial height, its glenoid version, is a diagram of the forces it will deliver to every downstream structure. The same is true of the femoral head-neck junction, the posterior tibial slope, the pelvic incidence, the posterosuperior calcaneus. Each is a morphological boundary condition that sets a force environment. Each force environment produces a predictable tissue adaptation through molecular machinery that has now been identified. This machinery is ancient, conserved, and shared across tissue types.

Codman recognized in 1934 that the pathology of the rotator cuff could not be explained by the mechanism his contemporaries assumed.^2^ Gerber demonstrated that correcting the morphological boundary condition changes the tissue outcome.^9,10^ The molecular evidence shows why: the cell's fate decisions are gated by mechanical input through pathways that operate independently of, and in some contexts dominantly over, canonical biochemical signaling.^34^ What clinicians have been diagnosing as separate diseases at separate joints may be better understood as the predictable output of a single conserved system doing exactly what it was designed to do — reading force and adapting accordingly. If so, the path to durable treatment lies in correcting the force environment, not simply repairing the tissue that faithfully adapted to it.
