## Figure 2: Force Vector Concept

**Prompt:**

Create a high-resolution medical illustration showing two side-by-side views of the glenohumeral joint for a peer-reviewed orthopedic journal. The purpose is to visually demonstrate how scapular morphology changes the direction of the dominant force acting on the shoulder joint. No specific numbers, angles, or measurements should appear anywhere in the figure. The image should communicate a concept, not data.

LEFT SIDE: A simplified anterior view of a shoulder with a short lateral acromion. The bone should be rendered in clean light gray or warm ivory tones with subtle shading to suggest three-dimensionality. The glenoid (socket) should be visible as a shallow concavity. The humeral head (ball) should sit centered in the glenoid. Draw the deltoid muscle as a smooth, anatomically shaped band of tissue in a muted warm red or coral color, originating from the short acromion and wrapping down to the humeral shaft. From the acromion, draw one bold, smooth arrow representing the primary deltoid force vector. This arrow should point predominantly INTO the glenoid face, angled only slightly upward. The arrow should be thick, confident, and visually dominant. It should be colored in a warm amber or gold tone. At the glenoid surface, show this force splitting into two components using smaller arrows: a LARGE downward-and-inward arrow labeled simply "Compression" in a calm blue color, and a SMALL upward arrow labeled "Shear" in a muted red. The compression arrow should be visually much larger than the shear arrow, making it immediately obvious that compression dominates. Below this panel, in clean text: "Compression-Dominant."

RIGHT SIDE: The same shoulder anatomy but with a noticeably longer lateral acromion that extends further over the humeral head. Everything else is anatomically identical. The deltoid originates from this longer acromion, and its force vector arrow now points more UPWARD and less into the glenoid. Same bold, smooth arrow style, same amber/gold color, but the direction has shifted noticeably superiorly. At the glenoid, the force again splits into compression and shear components, but now the proportions are reversed: the shear arrow (red) is LARGE and the compression arrow (blue) is SMALL. The supraspinatus tendon should be subtly visible on top of the humeral head, and a small annotation or visual cue should suggest it is under increased demand. Below this panel: "Shear-Dominant."

The two panels should be separated by a thin vertical line or subtle gap. The visual story should be graspable in three seconds: same muscle, different bone shape, completely different force direction. The left side pushes the ball into the socket. The right side pushes the ball up along the socket.

Do not include any degree markings, Newton values, axis labels, or numerical data. No graph elements. No grid lines. This is a concept illustration, not a data figure.

Style: Elegant, restrained medical illustration. Muted natural color palette (ivory bone, coral muscle, amber force vectors, blue compression, muted red shear). White or very light warm gray background. Subtle shadows for depth but no heavy 3D rendering. The quality should match illustrations in the Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery or Bone and Joint Journal. Clean, modern, authoritative.

Dimensions: landscape, 7 inches wide by 4 inches tall, 300 DPI.

## Figure 3: Competing Pathways — Conceptual Landscape

**Prompt:**

Create a high-resolution conceptual diagram for a peer-reviewed orthopedic journal that illustrates a central idea: a single morphological spectrum produces two diverging disease pathways that are mutually exclusive because each pathway's consequences geometrically prevent the other from developing.

The diagram should feel like a conceptual model or a landscape of disease, not a flowchart with boxes. Think of it as a topographic map of shoulder disease where the terrain is shaped by morphology.

TOP OF THE FIGURE: A smooth, wide horizontal gradient band representing the morphological spectrum. The left end transitions from deep, rich blue. The center is a neutral warm stone gray. The right end transitions to deep, warm crimson. No specific degree numbers anywhere on this gradient. Instead, label the left end simply "Short Acromion," the center "Neutral Morphology," and the right end "Long Acromion." This establishes that we are talking about a continuous spectrum of bony shape, not a binary classification.

FROM THE LEFT (blue zone), a pathway flows downward and to the left like a river descending a hillside. The flow should feel natural and gravitational, not like a rigid flowchart. Along this descending pathway, place key waypoints as subtle, elegant labels (not boxes) that describe the tissue changes in sequence:

"Compression-dominant loading"
then flowing to
"Cartilage adaptation"
then flowing to
"Subchondral stiffening"
then flowing to
"Joint remodeling"
arriving at a calm, prominent terminus labeled
"Osteoarthritis"

The visual language for this pathway should use blue tones throughout, suggesting depth, compression, and solidity.

FROM THE RIGHT (red zone), a parallel pathway flows downward and to the right. Same natural, gravitational flow. Waypoints:

"Shear-dominant loading"
then flowing to
"Tendon overload"
then flowing to
"Matrix remodeling"
then flowing to
"Progressive tearing"
then flowing to
"Force couple failure"
arriving at a warm, prominent terminus labeled
"Rotator Cuff Disease"

The visual language for this pathway should use warm red and coral tones throughout, suggesting tension and strain.

IN THE CENTER, between the two diverging pathways, a subtle zone of ambiguity where the colors blend and neither pathway has fully committed. Label this softly: "Transitional zone — outcome determined by individual biology and mechanical history."

THE CRITICAL ELEMENT — MUTUAL EXCLUSIVITY: Between the two pathways, near their lower portions, draw two graceful curved lines (not rigid arrows) that cross from one pathway toward the other but are visually BLOCKED or FADED before reaching the other side. One line sweeps from the OA pathway toward the RCT pathway with the concept: "Joint medialization shifts geometry away from shear." The other sweeps from the RCT pathway toward the OA pathway with: "Superior migration unloads the articular surface." These crossing lines should feel like natural barriers in the landscape, where the terrain shaped by one disease makes it impossible to reach the territory of the other.

Do not use rigid boxes, sharp corners, or flowchart connectors. The entire figure should feel organic and fluid, like a river delta splitting into two channels that can never rejoin. No specific numerical data anywhere. No degree values, no odds ratios, no p-values. Pure concept.

The figure should communicate one idea in five seconds of looking: shoulder disease diverges into two streams based on morphology, and once you are in one stream, the geometry won't let you reach the other.

Style: Elegant, modern, slightly artistic but scientifically rigorous. Think of the style used in Nature Reviews Disease Primers or Annual Review of Biomedical Engineering conceptual figures. Soft gradients, muted but rich colors, clean typography. White background. Text in a refined sans-serif font like Helvetica Neue Light or Avenir. The figure should be beautiful enough that a surgeon would want to frame it, and precise enough that a reviewer would find nothing to object to.

Dimensions: portrait, 6 inches wide by 8 inches tall, 300 DPI.

## Figure 1: The Enthesis Gradient — Molecular Scale

**Prompt:**

Create a high-resolution scientific illustration showing the graded tissue transition at a tendon-to-bone insertion site for a peer-reviewed orthopedic journal. The purpose is to demonstrate visually that tissue molecular composition changes continuously along a mechanical gradient, with tension-adapted tissue on one end and compression-adapted tissue on the other.

The illustration should show a smooth, elegant longitudinal section of tissue transitioning from left to right through four zones. The transition should feel continuous and gradual, like a color gradient, not like four distinct blocks.

FAR LEFT: Pure tendon. Dense, parallel wavy collagen fibers rendered in silvery-white with subtle blue undertones. The fibers should look like taut cables, aligned and organized. Elongated cells (tenocytes) are visible between the fibers, oriented along the fiber direction. The visual feeling is: organized, aligned, tensile. A subtle label: "Tendon — Tension."

LEFT-CENTER: The fibers begin to lose their strict parallel alignment. They become slightly more disorganized and the tissue takes on a slightly more purple or violet tone as fibrocartilage character appears. Cells become rounder. The transition is smooth. Label: "Fibrocartilage."

RIGHT-CENTER: Mineralization appears. Small granular deposits within the matrix, rendered as subtle white or pale gold flecks. The tissue color deepens. A visible wavy boundary (the tidemark) separates the unmineralized from the mineralized fibrocartilage. Cells are rounder, sitting in lacunae-like spaces. Label: "Mineralized Fibrocartilage."

FAR RIGHT: Bone. Lamellar structure with osteocytes visible in small dark lacunae. The color shifts to warm ivory or tan. The structure is dense and organized but in a completely different way from the tendon. Label: "Bone — Compression."

Below the tissue section, show two simple, elegant curves spanning the full width:

Curve 1: A smooth line labeled "Type I Collagen" that starts high on the left (tendon) and gracefully descends to near zero on the right (bone). Rendered in a calm blue.

Curve 2: A smooth line labeled "Proteoglycan / Type II Collagen" that starts near zero on the left and rises through the fibrocartilage zones. Rendered in warm orange or amber.

The two curves should cross approximately at the fibrocartilage zone, creating a visual X that communicates: the molecular identity of the tissue inverts as you move from the tension side to the compression side.

No specific numerical values on the curves. No axis labels with units. The curves communicate the concept of reciprocal molecular gradients, not specific data.

A single line of caption text at the bottom: "Tissue composition at every point reflects its local mechanical environment."

Style: Delicate, refined scientific illustration. The tissue section should have a watercolor-like quality with smooth color transitions rather than hard boundaries. The curves below should be clean and minimal. Overall feel: the molecular world is elegant and precisely organized by mechanical forces. Colors should be muted and natural. White background. This should look like a figure from a Cell or Nature paper.

Dimensions: landscape, 7 inches wide by 5 inches tall, 300 DPI.

## Figure 4: Muscle Architecture Adaptation

**Prompt:**

Create a high-resolution scientific illustration showing four stages of rotator cuff muscle adaptation after tendon injury, suitable for a peer-reviewed orthopedic journal. The purpose is to show that muscle architectural changes after cuff tears are progressive mechanical adaptations, not irreversible degeneration, because the final panel shows that restoring the correct mechanical signal reverses the architecture.

Four panels arranged left to right, each showing a schematic cross-section of muscle with its internal fiber architecture visible. The panels should be connected by a subtle horizontal arrow suggesting progression through time. The visual language should be clean, schematic, and immediately readable.

PANEL 1 — "Healthy":
A muscle belly shown in warm, vibrant red-pink. Internal fibers are drawn as clean parallel lines at a shallow angle to the tendon axis (approximately 25-30 degrees). The fibers are long and evenly spaced. Within each fiber, tiny repeating units (sarcomeres) are suggested by subtle banding or dashes. The tendon emerges from the muscle and connects to a small bone fragment. Everything looks organized, vital, and healthy. The overall shape is a full, robust muscle belly.

PANEL 2 — "After Tear (Adaptive)":
The tendon is disconnected from the bone (a visible gap). The muscle has retracted slightly and is somewhat smaller. The fiber angle has increased (steeper, approximately 35-40 degrees). The fibers are shorter. The color has faded slightly to a paler pink. But the fibers themselves look intact and organized, just reoriented. A subtle label or annotation: "Fibers shorten and reorient. Architecture changes. Fibers themselves remain viable."

PANEL 3 — "Chronic Tear (Advanced)":
The tendon is significantly retracted. The muscle is visibly atrophied (smaller belly). The fiber angle is steep (approximately 50-55 degrees). Between the muscle fibers, yellow-white deposits (fat) are visible, giving the muscle a marbled appearance. The overall color is a dull pinkish-gray with yellow streaks. The muscle looks compromised. Label: "Fatty infiltration. Shortened fibers. Functional capacity reduced."

PANEL 4 — "After Continuous Traction (Restored)":
A curved arrow connects back from Panel 3 to this panel, labeled "Slow, continuous mechanical traction." The muscle belly has regained volume. The fiber angle has returned to near-normal (25-30 degrees, matching Panel 1). The fibers are long again. The color has returned to a healthier pink, though some residual yellow fat deposits remain between fibers (not fully reversed but clearly improved). The overall appearance is close to Panel 1 but not identical, suggesting substantial but incomplete recovery. Label: "Architecture normalized. New sarcomeres added. Fat arrested but not fully reversed."

Below all four panels, a single conceptual statement: "Muscle architectural changes after rotator cuff tear represent mechanical adaptation to altered loading, not irreversible damage. Restoring the correct mechanical signal restores the architecture."

No specific numerical values (no degree numbers, no micron measurements, no Goutallier stages). The visual progression from healthy to compromised to restored tells the story without data.

Style: Clean, schematic, modern scientific illustration. Not photorealistic but anatomically informed. The muscle fibers should be clearly visible as internal lines within a smooth muscle silhouette. Fat deposits should be distinctly yellow or cream colored against the pink muscle. Bone rendered in clean ivory. The four panels should have identical framing so the eye naturally compares them. White background. Muted, natural color palette. This should look like a figure from a review article in JBJS or Clinical Orthopaedics.

Dimensions: landscape, 8 inches wide by 4 inches tall, 300 DPI.

## General Notes for All Figures

- NO specific numerical data in any figure (no degrees, no Newtons, no p-values, no microstrain values, no odds ratios)
- Every figure communicates a CONCEPT, not data
- The data lives in the manuscript text; the figures teach the reader how to think about the data
- All figures should work independently of the manuscript text — a reader who sees only the figures should understand the paper's argument
- Color palette should be consistent across all four figures: blues for compression/OA, warm reds for shear/RCT, amber/gold for force vectors, natural tones for anatomy
- All text in clean sans-serif font, minimum 8pt at final reproduction size
- All figures should reproduce clearly in both color (digital) and grayscale (print)
