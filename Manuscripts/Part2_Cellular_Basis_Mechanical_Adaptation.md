# The Cellular Basis of Mechanical Adaptation: How Tissues Read Force and Change Fate

William B. Roepke, M.D.

Department of Orthopedic Surgery and Rehabilitation, University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, Oklahoma City, OK

---

## Abstract

Musculoskeletal tissues adapt their composition to match the forces they receive. A tendon compressed against bone becomes fibrocartilage. Bone resorbs without load and forms under load. Cartilage degenerates under focal compression it was not designed to bear. These adaptations are well documented clinically, and a companion paper has argued that scapular morphology determines the force environment at the glenohumeral joint, producing predictable patterns of degenerative disease.^1^ What has been missing is a molecular explanation for how cells read their mechanical environment and translate that reading into changes in gene expression and tissue composition. That explanation now exists. Mechanosensitive ion channels, a structural pathway that transmits force from the extracellular matrix directly to chromatin, and transcriptional regulators whose nuclear localization is gated by cytoskeletal tension have been identified and characterized over the past two decades. Together, these systems form a conserved regulatory architecture in which the mechanical environment establishes the upstream context for cell fate decisions. This paper reviews the molecular evidence, connects it to clinical tissue adaptation, and identifies the assumptions and gaps that remain to be addressed.

---

## Introduction

In a companion paper, we argued that four seemingly distinct shoulder conditions — rotator cuff tears, glenohumeral osteoarthritis, posterior instability with glenoid erosion, and cuff tear arthropathy — are branches of the same mechanical system, with scapular morphology selecting which tissue receives an aberrant force and therefore which condition develops.^1^ That paper deliberately focused on clinical and biomechanical evidence, citing the molecular machinery only in passing. The present paper develops what was left implicit: the biological mechanisms by which cells sense mechanical force, interpret it, and alter their gene expression in response.

The claim that living tissue adapts to its mechanical environment is not new. Wolff described it for bone in 1892.^2^ Davis extended the principle to soft tissue. Frost formalized it with specific strain thresholds in the mechanostat model.^3^ These observations are taught in every orthopedic training program. What they share is a descriptive quality — they tell us that adaptation occurs without explaining how a cell converts a mechanical signal into a transcriptional response. The identification of the molecular apparatus that performs this conversion changes the clinical calculus in a specific way: if the machinery is conserved and the response is predictable, then the tissue changes we observe in degenerative disease are not stochastic failures but deterministic outputs of a system responding correctly to the wrong input.

This has a direct therapeutic implication. If the cellular response to mechanical force is governed by identified molecular pathways with defined inputs and outputs, then durable treatment requires correcting the mechanical input — not merely repairing the tissue that responded to it. The molecular evidence reviewed here provides the mechanistic basis for this claim.

We should state at the outset what this paper does and does not attempt. It reviews three mechanotransduction systems with the strongest evidence for regulating cell fate in load-bearing tissues. It connects these systems to clinical observations of tissue adaptation. It proposes that the mechanical environment functions as an upstream regulator that constrains the range of cell fates accessible to biochemical signaling. It does not claim that mechanical signals are the only relevant input, nor that the molecular pathways demonstrated in experimental systems have been directly confirmed in every human degenerative condition discussed. Where we are making inferences rather than citing direct demonstrations, we say so.

## Force Reaches the Genome Through a Structural Pathway

D'Arcy Thompson wrote in 1917 that "the form of an object is a diagram of forces."^4^ The cell appears to have internalized this principle architecturally. Integrins — transmembrane receptors that anchor the cell to the extracellular matrix — are not merely attachment points. They are mechanically coupled to the cytoskeleton in a way that other surface receptors are not. Wang, Butler, and Ingber demonstrated this in 1993 using magnetic twisting cytometry: when ferromagnetic beads bound to integrins via RGD peptides were subjected to controlled mechanical stress, the cell stiffened progressively with load, consistent with a prestressed structural network. Beads bound to non-integrin receptors showed no such response. Disrupting the cytoskeleton abolished the stiffening.^5^ The cell has a dedicated force-sensing apparatus at its surface, and that apparatus is structurally continuous with the interior of the cell.

Maniotis, Chen, and Ingber extended this observation in 1997 by demonstrating that the structural continuity reaches all the way to the nucleus. When integrins were pulled using micropipettes, the nucleus visibly distorted — nucleoli reoriented and the nuclear envelope deformed within seconds. Pulling on non-integrin receptors produced no nuclear distortion. The connection required intact intermediate filaments; disrupting them abolished nuclear deformation.^6^ This was not a signaling cascade being activated. It was a physical structure being pulled. The cell surface and the nucleus are mechanically linked through a continuous structural pathway.

The molecular identity of this pathway has since been established. The LINC complex (Linker of Nucleoskeleton and Cytoskeleton) consists of nesprin proteins spanning the outer nuclear membrane and SUN proteins spanning the inner nuclear membrane, forming a bridge across the nuclear envelope that connects the cytoskeleton to the nuclear lamina and, through it, to chromatin.^7,8^ Lombardi and colleagues confirmed its functional importance by showing that disrupting the LINC complex with dominant-negative constructs significantly reduced nuclear strain during mechanical loading and impaired nuclear positioning, cell polarization, and migration.^8^

The speed of this structural pathway is significant. Na and colleagues measured the kinetics of mechanotransduction using FRET-based Src kinase reporters and found that mechanical force applied to integrins activated Src at remote cytoplasmic sites within 0.3 seconds — approximately 40-fold faster than soluble growth factor (PDGF) stimulation measured with the same biosensor at the same distances.^9^ The structural pathway transmits force at speeds that diffusion-based chemical signaling cannot match.

The most striking demonstration comes from Tajik and colleagues, who used three-dimensional magnetic twisting cytometry to apply controlled stresses to cell surfaces while simultaneously visualizing chromatin deformation and gene expression. They showed that mechanical force directly stretches chromatin in a direction-dependent manner, that RNA polymerase II is recruited to mechanically stretched chromatin within five seconds of force application, and that transcription of the target gene is detectable within fifteen seconds.^10^ The speed and directionality of this response are inconsistent with a diffusion-mediated signaling cascade and consistent with a solid-state mechanical transmission pathway.

An important qualification is necessary here. Lombardi's group also reported a finding that complicates the narrative: when the LINC complex was disrupted, abolishing nuclear deformation, the expression of several classic mechanosensitive genes — including Egr-1, Iex-1, vinculin, and tenascin C — was not affected.^8^ These genes were still activated by mechanical strain through cytoplasmic pathways involving focal adhesions, Rho GTPases, and MAPK signaling. This means the LINC-to-chromatin structural pathway, while real and fast, operates in parallel with cytoplasmic mechanotransduction pathways rather than as the sole route for force-to-gene regulation. Additionally, Nava and colleagues demonstrated that rapid force-induced chromatin changes serve primarily as a genome protection mechanism — preventing DNA damage during mechanical stress — rather than as a direct gene activation mechanism, with the chromatin modifications occurring primarily at non-coding regions.^11^ The direct mechanical pathway to chromatin is genuine, but its primary function may be protective rather than transcriptional, with most mechanosensitive gene regulation occurring through cytoplasmic intermediaries. We do not yet know the relative contribution of the direct structural pathway versus cytoplasmic pathways in clinical tissue adaptation.

Ingber proposed that the structural framework enabling this force transmission is tensegrity — a prestressed architecture in which rigid compression elements (microtubules) are suspended within a continuous tension network (actin filaments and intermediate filaments).^12^ In a prestressed tensegrity structure, force applied at any point is transmitted globally through the network rather than dissipating locally. This architectural principle explains why mechanical signals can propagate across the cell so rapidly: the cytoskeleton is not a passive scaffold but a tuned mechanical transmission system. The same tensegrity principle operates at the tissue level — the scapula, for example, has no bony articulation with the axial skeleton and is stabilized entirely by muscle tension, functioning as a floating compression element in a continuous tension network.^1^ The recurrence of tensegrity architecture at cellular and tissue scales suggests it is a conserved structural solution to the problem of distributing mechanical information across biological systems, though this cross-scale analogy should be recognized as a conceptual framework rather than a proven equivalence.

## A Transcriptional Switch Gated by Mechanical State

The structural pathway establishes that force reaches the nucleus. The question of what happens when it arrives — how mechanical information is converted into specific transcriptional programs — was substantially answered by the identification of YAP and TAZ as mechanotransducers.

YAP (Yes-Associated Protein) and TAZ (transcriptional co-activator with PDZ-binding motif) are transcriptional co-activators that shuttle between the cytoplasm and the nucleus. Their localization determines their function: in the nucleus, they partner with TEAD transcription factors to activate genes governing proliferation, stemness, and osteogenic differentiation; in the cytoplasm, they are inactive and eventually degraded.^13^ The critical discovery, made by Dupont and colleagues in 2011, was that this localization is controlled by the mechanical state of the cell. On stiff substrates, YAP and TAZ are nuclear. On soft substrates, they are cytoplasmic. On large micropatterned islands where cells can spread, they are nuclear. On small islands where cells are confined, they are cytoplasmic. The regulation requires Rho GTPase activity and actomyosin tension but is independent of the canonical Hippo/LATS kinase cascade — a mechanically gated pathway operating in parallel to, and distinct from, the biochemical signaling pathway that had been the focus of the field.^13^

The functional consequences are direct. Dupont showed that YAP/TAZ knockdown inhibited osteogenic differentiation on stiff substrates and enabled adipogenic differentiation in the same mechanical environment — demonstrating that YAP/TAZ are not merely markers of mechanical state but required mediators of mechanically directed cell fate.^13^ Elosegui-Artola and colleagues subsequently identified the physical mechanism: force transmitted from the extracellular matrix through the cytoskeleton to the nucleus flattens the nucleus, which dilates nuclear pores and reduces the mechanical restriction to YAP import. Direct application of force to the nucleus using an atomic force microscope was sufficient to drive YAP nuclear translocation, even when the actin cytoskeleton was disrupted. The mechanism operates through molecular mechanical stability — proteins must partially unfold to pass through nuclear pores, and the ease of this unfolding relative to the pore restriction determines the rigidity threshold for translocation.^14^

The finding with the most direct clinical relevance comes from the comprehensive review by Piccolo, Dupont, and Cordenonsi, who stated: "mechanical regulation is not only formally distinct from Hippo-mediated YAP/TAZ phosphorylation, but, in fact, dominates over it. LATS1/2 inactivation is inconsequential — namely, it cannot rescue YAP/TAZ inhibition in cells with reduced mechanical stress."^15^ In plain language: when the mechanical context is wrong, activating the biochemical pathway cannot compensate. The mechanical signal is not one input among many. It is the gatekeeper that determines which biochemical inputs the cell can read. This does not mean mechanical signals always have larger effect sizes than chemical signals — Park and colleagues showed that TGF-β can produce larger fold-changes in specific differentiation markers than substrate stiffness alone.^16^ Rather, it means that the mechanical environment sets a permissive state that constrains which chemical signals can produce their intended effect. The same growth factor, TGF-β, drives smooth muscle differentiation on stiff substrates and chondrogenic differentiation on soft substrates — opposite fates from the same chemical signal, selected by the mechanical context.^16^

This has a specific implication for understanding compressive tendinopathy. When a tendon is compressed against a bony prominence, the resident tenocytes are physically deformed — flattened, rounded, confined. These are precisely the geometric conditions that exclude YAP from the nucleus. Hallström and colleagues confirmed that YAP/TAZ activity suppresses chondrogenic gene expression, and that reducing mechanoactive Rho signaling — which mimics the low-tension state of a compressed, rounded cell — produces a broad increase in chondrogenic gene expression through YAP/TAZ exclusion.^17^ The fibrocartilaginous metaplasia observed wherever tendons are compressed against bone^18^ may therefore be explained by the same YAP/TAZ mechanical gating demonstrated in culture: compression changes cell shape, cell shape excludes YAP, YAP exclusion permits chondrogenic gene programs. We should acknowledge that this specific chain has not been directly demonstrated in human tendinopathy specimens — no study has measured YAP localization in surgically retrieved tissue from a compressed tendon. This is a prediction of the framework, well-supported by the molecular evidence but awaiting direct histological confirmation.

## A Molecular Mechanostat: Piezo1 Across Tissues

Wolff observed in 1892 that bone adapts its structure to load. Frost proposed in 1987 that this adaptation is governed by strain thresholds — a mechanostat.^3^ The molecular identity of this mechanostat has now been substantially established through the characterization of Piezo1, a mechanosensitive ion channel that opens in response to membrane deformation and permits calcium influx.^19^

The evidence in bone is the most complete. Sun and colleagues generated osteoblast-specific Piezo1 conditional knockout mice and found severe bone defects: significantly reduced bone mineral density, trabecular number, and cortical thickness, with bone strength approximately half that of controls.^20^ The phenotype was present in both sexes and persisted at 16 weeks, indicating a fundamental and sustained requirement for Piezo1 in bone homeostasis rather than a transient developmental effect. Hindlimb suspension experiments were particularly informative: wild-type mice lost bone dramatically with unloading, while Piezo1 knockout mice showed no further deterioration — they were already at a floor indistinguishable from the unloaded wild-type state.^20^ This means that essentially all of the bone loss caused by mechanical unloading is Piezo1-dependent. Exercise (treadmill running) increased Piezo1 expression and osteoblast marker genes in wild-type but not in knockout animals.^20^ Piezo1 expression was significantly lower in bone from human osteoporosis patients, with strong positive correlations to osteoblast markers.^20^

Wang and colleagues extended this with a finding that connects mechanical sensing to bone remodeling more broadly: Piezo1 deletion in osteoblasts not only reduced bone formation but increased bone resorption by osteoclasts.^21^ The mechanism involves Piezo1-dependent expression of type II and IX collagen by osteoblasts, which binds to osteoclast integrins and inhibits osteoclast differentiation through a paracrine pathway. When the mechanosensor is removed, this inhibitory signal is lost, and resorption proceeds unchecked.^21^ Inducible deletion of Piezo1 in adult mice produced significant osteoporosis within two weeks, confirming that ongoing Piezo1 signaling is required for adult bone maintenance, not just developmental patterning.^21^

Zhou and colleagues identified the downstream signaling pathway connecting Piezo1 to gene expression: Piezo1/2 activation triggers calcium influx, which activates calcineurin, which dephosphorylates NFATc1, YAP1, and β-catenin simultaneously, driving their nuclear translocation and activating osteogenic gene programs.^22^ This pathway bypasses canonical Hippo kinases entirely — Mst1/2 and Lats1 phosphorylation were unchanged by fluid shear stress or Piezo1 agonist treatment.^22^ The connection between the tissue-level mechanostat (Piezo1 sensing membrane deformation) and the transcriptional switch (YAP entering the nucleus) is therefore direct, operating through calcium and calcineurin rather than through the Hippo cascade.

The same molecular sensor operates in tendon, though the evidence rests primarily on a single study and should be interpreted with that limitation in mind. Passini and colleagues developed a functional imaging system that allowed simultaneous calcium imaging and mechanical loading of tendon explants. They found that tenocytes detect mechanical forces through Piezo1, but the relevant stimulus is not direct stretch of the cell — it is shear stress generated by relative sliding between collagen fibers during tissue loading. Piezo1 activation drives calcium influx, which upregulates collagen cross-linking enzymes (LOX family) rather than collagen production itself, increasing tendon stiffness through matrix cross-linking rather than hypertrophy.^23^ Tenocyte-targeted Piezo1 knockout mice had reduced tendon stiffness; Piezo1 gain-of-function mice had increased stiffness. Human carriers of a PIEZO1 gain-of-function variant (E756del) showed a 13% increase in normalized jumping performance compared to non-carriers, though the sample size was small (n=65) and the variant also affects red blood cells.^23^ Passini described this as a "tenocyte mechanostat" — a feedback system in which the cross-link network self-tunes to maintain appropriate shear stress on resident cells, analogous to Frost's bone mechanostat but operating through a different effector mechanism.

In cartilage, the picture is more complex and argues against a simple "one channel, one tissue" model. Lee and colleagues showed that both Piezo1 and Piezo2 contribute to chondrocyte mechanosensitivity, with their combined knockdown virtually eliminating calcium responses to high-magnitude strain.^24^ However, Servin-Vences and colleagues demonstrated through direct electrophysiological measurement that Piezo1 and the mechanosensitive channel TRPV4 respond to different types of mechanical input in chondrocytes: Piezo1 is responsible for stretch-activated currents while TRPV4 mediates substrate-deflection responses.^25^ Only the combined ablation of both channels nearly eliminated mechanically gated currents. This genuine redundancy in cartilage mechanosensation means Piezo1 cannot be described as "the" cartilage mechanosensor — the system has built-in redundancy with stimulus-type specificity.

Perhaps the most clinically relevant finding comes from Lee and colleagues' 2021 demonstration that inflammation reprograms mechanical sensitivity in cartilage. IL-1α treatment upregulated Piezo1 expression and increased chondrocyte stiffness, creating a dual amplification: more channels and a stiffer cell that transmits more force to those channels.^26^ This produces a pathogenic feed-forward loop in which inflammation increases mechanical sensitivity, normal loading now generates a damage-level mechanical signal, and the resulting injury drives further inflammation. This finding dissolves the traditional dichotomy between "mechanical" and "inflammatory" pathology in osteoarthritis — inflammation does not compete with mechanical damage but reprograms the mechanical sensitivity of the tissue, making the two processes molecularly inseparable through the same ion channel.

## The Evolutionary Context

The molecular systems described above are not recent evolutionary innovations. Mechanosensitive ion channels in prokaryotes — the MscL and MscS families — are present across Bacteria and Archaea and likely date to the earliest membrane-bound cells.^27^ They function as osmotic safety valves, opening in response to membrane stretch to prevent cell lysis. These channels, along with basic chemosensory systems, represent some of the most ancient regulatory capabilities of living cells. The eukaryotic mechanosensors that operate in human tissues, including the Piezo family, share no sequence or structural homology with prokaryotic mechanosensitive channels — they evolved independently, representing convergent solutions to the problem of sensing the physical environment.^28^ The fact that mechanosensing has been independently reinvented across domains of life speaks to the fundamental nature of the problem it solves: any cell with a membrane must contend with mechanical forces.

This phylogenetic depth has a practical implication. Ancient, conserved regulatory systems tend to occupy deep positions in cellular hierarchies because subsequent evolution built on top of them. Engler and colleagues demonstrated this principle empirically: mesenchymal stem cells plated on substrates of varying stiffness committed to neurogenic, myogenic, or osteogenic lineages based on substrate stiffness alone, without chemical differentiation factors.^29^ The mechanical commitment was not instantaneous — during the first week, chemical differentiation factors could still redirect lineage. But over time, the mechanical instruction became progressively irreversible, constraining the cell's responsiveness to subsequent chemical signals.^16,29^ This time-dependent consolidation of mechanical commitment is consistent with the clinical observation that chronic postural or morphological perturbations produce structural tissue changes that become resistant to non-mechanical interventions.

We should be cautious about the scope of this evolutionary argument. The claim that mechanosensing is "at least as ancient" as chemical signaling is supported by the phylogenetic distribution of mechanosensitive channels.^27^ The stronger claim that it is therefore the "primary" regulator requires qualification. What the evidence supports is that mechanical signals have unique properties — speed of transmission, direct physical access to chromatin, independence from canonical biochemical cascades — that give them a privileged position in contexts where mechanical loading is continuous and large in magnitude. In load-bearing tissues, this privileged position may translate to functional dominance as a context-setter for cell fate. In tissues where mechanical loading is minimal, the same molecular machinery exists but may be less persistently activated and therefore less influential relative to chemical and genetic inputs.

## The Coupling of Mechanical and Biological Aging

A framework that attributes degenerative disease to mechanical environment must contend with the observation that these conditions become more common with age. The traditional interpretation is that aging itself — cellular senescence, hormonal changes, accumulated oxidative damage — independently weakens tissues and predisposes to failure. If this interpretation is correct, the mechanical framework explains at most a portion of the variance.

Emerging evidence suggests the relationship is more nuanced. Several of the processes traditionally attributed to "biological aging" appear to be coupled to mechanical history through specific feedback loops rather than operating independently of it.

Cellular senescence in load-bearing tissues is accelerated by mechanical overload. Kong and colleagues demonstrated directly that mechanical overloading drives chondrocyte senescence through Zmpste24-mediated nuclear membrane instability, and that Zmpste24 overexpression rescues the senescent phenotype.^30^ Stowe and colleagues showed that senescent tenocytes exhibit blunted extracellular matrix remodeling responses to mechanical stimulation, with sustained MMP activity shifting the remodeling balance toward degradation.^31^ The implication is that a portion of what presents as age-related tissue degeneration may reflect mechanically induced senescence — cells that entered a senescent state not because of chronological age but because of chronic mechanical overload. We do not yet know the relative contribution of chronological versus mechanically induced senescence in human degenerative tissue, and separating these will require studies that correlate tissue senescence markers with both age and loading history.

The accumulation of advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) in collagen is genuinely time-dependent — glucose reacts non-enzymatically with collagen, forming cross-links that accumulate with chronological age and are accelerated in diabetes.^32^ This is a biological clock that the mechanical framework does not explain. However, AGE cross-links do not simply stiffen tissue. Li and colleagues showed that AGEs drastically reduce collagen fiber-fiber sliding — the dominant mechanical consequence is loss of viscoelasticity and tissue plasticity rather than uniform stiffening.^33^ A tendon or cartilage matrix with accumulated AGE cross-links transmits strain to resident cells differently than a young matrix, even under identical external loading. This means AGE accumulation, while chemically driven, feeds back into the mechanical framework by altering the mechanical signals that cells experience. The "aging" tissue is not just older — it is mechanically different in ways that the mechanotransduction apparatus reads and responds to.

Hormonal changes modulate the gain of the mechanotransduction system itself. Geoghegan and colleagues demonstrated that estrogen withdrawal reduces integrin αvβ3 localization at focal adhesion sites in osteocytes, directly impairing the mechanosensing apparatus rather than simply removing a chemical signal.^34^ Estrogen-deficient osteocytes showed defective calcium and Cox-2 responses to oscillatory fluid flow and shifted their paracrine signaling toward increased RANKL/OPG ratios, favoring osteoclastogenesis.^34^ This means postmenopausal bone loss is not simply "estrogen withdrawal removes protection." It is estrogen withdrawal reducing the cell's ability to hear the mechanical signals that maintain bone — an impairment of the mechanotransduction pathway at the level of the integrin receptor. The same mechanical loading now produces less osteogenic output because the gain of the system has been reduced.

The mechanosensitive bone marrow niche described by Shen and colleagues provides further evidence that what appears to be age-related stem cell exhaustion has a mechanical component. Peri-arteriolar osteolectin-positive cells — osteogenic progenitors maintained by Piezo1-dependent mechanical stimulation — were expanded by voluntary running and depleted by hindlimb unloading. Conditional deletion of Piezo1 phenocopied unloading, reducing osteogenic progenitor frequency and impairing lymphopoiesis with functional consequences for infection clearance and survival.^35^ The niche that maintains tissue regenerative capacity is itself mechanically dependent.

We do not argue that biological aging is reducible to mechanical history. Telomere shortening, some aspects of oxidative damage, and other time-dependent processes contribute independently to tissue degeneration. What the evidence does suggest is that the boundary between "mechanical" and "biological" aging in load-bearing tissues is far less clean than traditionally assumed. Multiple age-associated processes — senescence, matrix stiffening, hormonal changes in mechanosensitivity, stem cell niche depletion — either are driven by mechanical history or feed back through the same mechanotransduction pathways that determine cell fate. The mechanical component is not the entire explanation, but it may be the largest modifiable component, which makes it the most clinically actionable.

## In Vivo Validation

Most of the molecular evidence reviewed above derives from cell culture experiments on two-dimensional substrates. This is a significant limitation that must be acknowledged. The substrate stiffness experiments of Engler, Dupont, and Park used polyacrylamide gels or tissue culture plastic.^13,16,29^ The nuclear pore measurements of Elosegui-Artola used isolated fibroblasts.^14^ The three-dimensional, heterogeneous, dynamically loaded environment of a living joint is different from a culture dish in ways that could alter the behavior of these pathways.

Three in vivo studies provide evidence that the molecular mechanisms demonstrated in culture are operative in living organisms with functional consequences.

Shen and colleagues identified a mechanosensitive peri-arteriolar niche in bone marrow that requires Piezo1 for maintenance, with mechanical loading expanding and unloading depleting the niche, and Piezo1 deletion phenocopying unloading — establishing a complete causal chain from mechanical stimulus to mechanosensor to niche maintenance to organismal function.^35^

Ransom and colleagues demonstrated that controlled mechanical distraction of the mandible in mice activates the focal adhesion kinase (FAK) pathway in skeletal stem cells, producing chromatin accessibility changes at over 14,000 loci and reactivating a neural crest gene-regulatory program — an embryonic developmental program awakened in adult tissue by mechanical force. FAK inhibition abolished new bone formation during distraction. Fracture alone, which provides injury signals but not controlled mechanical distraction, did not activate this program.^36^ This is arguably the strongest single demonstration that mechanical force, acting through an identified mechanotransduction pathway, can reprogram cell identity in vivo.

Chan and colleagues showed that hydraulic pressure controls mammalian embryo size and cell fate specification at the blastocyst stage — lumenal pressure increases during development, driving cortical tension in trophectoderm cells, which in turn determines cell position and allocation between inner cell mass and trophectoderm lineages.^37^ This places mechanical forces as upstream controllers of cell fate at the earliest stage of mammalian development.

These studies do not prove that the same pathways operate in a degenerating rotator cuff or an arthritic knee. They do establish that mechanotransduction pathways regulate cell fate in vivo, in mammals, with functional consequences — and that the in vitro observations are not artifacts of the culture system. The gap between in vivo demonstrations in bone marrow, mandible, and embryo on one hand, and the clinical tissue adaptations described in Part 1 on the other, is a gap of direct demonstration at specific clinical sites, not a gap of biological plausibility.

## Synthesis: The Mechanical Environment as Upstream Context-Setter

The three molecular systems reviewed here — the integrin-LINC-chromatin structural pathway, YAP/TAZ as a mechanically gated transcriptional switch, and Piezo1 as a tissue-level mechanostat — are not independent discoveries from separate fields. They describe a single regulatory architecture:

Mechanical force at the tissue level deforms cell membranes, activating Piezo1 and triggering calcium influx. Calcium activates calcineurin, which dephosphorylates YAP and drives it into the nucleus through a pathway that is independent of canonical Hippo signaling.^22^ Simultaneously, force transmitted through integrins and the cytoskeleton reaches the nucleus through the LINC complex, deforming chromatin and altering nuclear pore mechanics to further facilitate YAP import.^10,14^ Once in the nucleus, YAP partners with TEAD transcription factors to activate gene programs that determine cell identity — osteogenic, chondrogenic, adipogenic, or proliferative.^13^ The specific output depends on which genes are accessible, which in turn depends on the history of mechanical and chemical signals the cell has received.^16,29^

This architecture has properties that distinguish it from purely chemical signaling. It is fast — force propagates through the prestressed cytoskeleton within milliseconds, orders of magnitude faster than diffusion.^9^ It is direct — the structural pathway from integrin to chromatin does not require enzymatic amplification cascades, though such cascades operate in parallel.^10^ And it is dominant under conditions of sustained mechanical loading — when the mechanical context is insufficient, biochemical activation of the downstream pathway cannot compensate.^15^ In load-bearing tissues where mechanical signals are continuous and large in magnitude, these properties position the mechanical environment as the upstream context-setter that determines which cell fates are accessible and which biochemical signals can be read.

We propose — with the qualification that this is a framework for organizing existing evidence rather than a proven hierarchy — that the tissue adaptations observed in clinical degenerative disease represent the predictable output of this molecular machinery operating in a mechanical environment set by morphology. The fibrocartilaginous metaplasia of compressed tendons, the cartilage degeneration under focal loading, the bone resorption in unloaded regions, and the progressive remodeling of glenoid bone stock in the direction of applied shear are each consistent with what the identified mechanotransduction pathways would produce given the force environment that morphology creates. The companion paper demonstrated the clinical pattern.^1^ This paper has identified the molecular apparatus that could generate it.

## Limitations and Unanswered Questions

Several limitations of this framework should be stated explicitly.

The molecular evidence is derived predominantly from two-dimensional in vitro systems. While the in vivo studies reviewed above demonstrate that mechanotransduction regulates cell fate in living organisms, the specific pathways have not been directly confirmed in human degenerative tissue. No study has measured YAP localization in a degenerating supraspinatus tendon from a patient with a characterized critical shoulder angle, or Piezo1 expression in fibrocartilaginous ECRB tendinosis tissue. The framework predicts that such measurements would show YAP exclusion in compressed zones and Piezo1 upregulation in overloaded regions. These studies are feasible and would provide the most direct test of the framework's clinical applicability.

YAP/TAZ mechanotransduction is not specific to load-bearing tissues. The same pathway operates in liver, intestine, skin, and neural progenitors.^15^ The claim that mechanical signals are functionally dominant in musculoskeletal tissues specifically rests not on a tissue-specific mechanism but on the argument that mechanical loading is more persistent and larger in magnitude in these tissues, producing more sustained pathway activation. This argument is reasonable but has not been validated by quantitative comparison of mechanical versus chemical effect sizes across tissue types.

Frost's mechanostat thresholds are well defined for bone but remain undefined for soft tissues.^3^ The framework assumes that tendon, cartilage, and ligament exhibit analogous threshold-dependent responses to mechanical stimuli, switching between qualitatively different biological programs at specific strain values. This assumption is consistent with the observed sharp transitions in tissue phenotype — tendon to fibrocartilage, for example — but the specific threshold values and their molecular determinants in non-osseous tissues have not been established.

The framework does not address acute trauma, genetic connective tissue disorders, or centralized pain states, which have etiologies in which the mechanical environment plays a secondary or negligible role. The claim is specific: in load-bearing tissues under chronic mechanical perturbation, the mechanical environment functions as the dominant upstream context-setter for tissue adaptation. This claim is falsifiable — if correcting morphological parameters does not alter tissue adaptation trajectories in prospective study, the mechanical causation argument fails. The specific falsification criteria and proposed studies have been articulated in the companion paper.^1^

## Conclusion

The molecular apparatus by which cells read their mechanical environment and change their identity in response has been identified. Force reaches the genome through a structural pathway faster than any diffusible signal. A transcriptional switch gated by mechanical state determines which genes are expressed. A conserved mechanosensitive ion channel transduces tissue-level loading into the calcium signals that drive this switch. Together, these systems form a regulatory architecture in which the mechanical environment establishes the upstream context for cell fate decisions in load-bearing tissues.

This evidence provides the molecular basis for the clinical observations described in Part 1 of this series — that scapular morphology predicts which tissue adapts and how at the glenohumeral joint. In the companion paper that follows, we extend this framework beyond the shoulder to examine whether the same principle — dominant forces redirected by measurable morphological parameters, producing predictable tissue adaptations through the mechanotransduction pathways described here — operates across the musculoskeletal system. If it does, the conditions we currently diagnose and treat as separate diseases may be better understood as predictable outputs of a single conserved system responding to the mechanical environments that morphology creates.
