**Subject:** Manuscript for your review — scapulothoracic biomechanics and shoulder pathology

Dear Dr. Kuhn,

I am an orthopedic surgery resident at the University of Oklahoma, heading to the University of Colorado next year for a shoulder and elbow fellowship. I recently sent my good friend Jordan Moore to see you for an assessment of his scapular kinematics.

Your 2016 paper on throwing, the shoulder, and human evolution had a significant impact on how I think about shoulder biomechanics. It prompted me to look more carefully at the scapula as an upstream variable in shoulder pathology, and that investigation has led to the attached manuscript, which I am hoping you might be willing to review.

The paper argues that the scapulothoracic articulation, as the only major bone in the shoulder girdle with no bony connection to the axial skeleton, functions as an upstream determinant of shoulder mechanics through both its static morphology and its dynamic positioning. I present four clinical examples from recent literature showing that different alterations in scapulothoracic mechanics are associated with different downstream pathologies, including rotator cuff disease (CSA), posterior instability (posterior acromial morphology), posterior glenoid wear (a hypothesis about dynamic glenoid version), and variable outcomes after reverse shoulder arthroplasty (scapulohumeral rhythm). I also discuss the structural principle of tensegrity as a potential biomechanical framework for understanding why these observations may be connected.

My interest in this topic grew out of a personal experience with scapulothoracic dysfunction that was initially misattributed to a neurological deficit. That experience changed how I think about the shoulder, and the literature investigation that followed is what produced this paper.

I would be grateful for your honest assessment of whether the framework has merit, where it overreaches, and whether you think it warrants publication. If you see value in the work and would be willing to contribute as a senior author, I would welcome that, but I am primarily seeking your critical feedback and perspective as someone who thinks deeply about these questions.

Thank you for your time.
